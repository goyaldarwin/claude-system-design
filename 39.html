Kafka & Log-based Messaging

A traditional message queue deletes a message once it's consumed — one reader, then it's gone. Kafka makes a different bet: messages are an append-only, durable, replayable log that many independent consumers read at their own pace, and that stays around after being read. That one design choice — the log as the primitive — is why Kafka became the backbone of event streaming.

The trade-off A durable, replayable, multi-consumer event log with massive throughput — in exchange for ordering only within a partition, and operational weight (partitions, consumer groups, replication, rebalancing). The log lets you replay history, add consumers without affecting others, and ingest millions of events/sec. You give up global ordering (only per-partition), and you take on partition counts, consumer-group coordination, and the rebalance hazard.

What the log primitive is

The whole system rests on one idea: an append-only commit log. Producers append records to the end; each record gets a monotonically increasing offset; consumers read forward from an offset they control. Crucially, reading does not delete — the log is retained (by time/size or compacted), so many consumers can read the same data independently, and you can rewind and replay.

The structure that makes it scale:

  • Topic — a named stream of records (e.g. orders).
  • Partition — a topic is split into partitions, each an independent ordered log on a broker. Partitions are the unit of parallelism and the only scope of ordering.
  • Offset — a record's position within its partition; consumers commit the offset they've processed.
  • Consumer group — a set of consumers that share a topic's partitions (each partition goes to exactly one member), so adding members scales throughput up to the partition count.
The one-line frameKafka trades global ordering and operational simplicity for durability, replay, multi-consumer fan-out, and throughput. Because the log is partitioned, you only get ordering within a partition — and you must manage partition counts, consumer-group rebalancing, and replication. That's the price of a log you can replay and fan out to many readers.

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide first. If you know all three cold, skip to the comparison.

Scenario 1 — the 240-consumer rebalance storm (real incident) A team did a routine rolling restart of a 240-consumer group. New consumers took ~38 seconds to send their first heartbeat, but the session timeout was 30s — so Kafka kept declaring restarting consumers "dead," each batch triggering another rebalance. The result: 9 minutes of continuous rebalancing with throughput at zero. What is a rebalance, why did restarts cascade into a storm, and what changed to fix it?
Scenario 2 — the acknowledged-but-lost message A producer sends with acks=1 — the leader broker confirms as soon as it has the record, before replicas copy it. The leader broker then crashes before any replica got that record. What happens to the "successfully sent" message, and what acks setting would have prevented it — at what cost?
Scenario 3 — the double charge A payment consumer reads "charge $50," charges the card, then crashes before committing its offset. On restart it re-reads the same record and charges again. Which delivery semantic causes this, and what are the two ways to make the effect happen exactly once?

Scenario 1 is consumer groups and rebalancing. Scenario 2 is acks and ISR durability. Scenario 3 is delivery semantics. Each maps to a decision below.

The four decisions that define a Kafka design

Designing with Kafka is these four choices. Each has a named failure nested under it.

Decision 1 — How do you partition? (parallelism and ordering)

You choose the number of partitions and the partition key. The key (hashed) decides which partition a record lands in — and since order is guaranteed only within a partition, all records that must stay ordered relative to each other must share a key. Example: key by user_id so all of one user's events are ordered, while different users spread across partitions for parallelism. Partition count caps consumer parallelism (a group can't have more active consumers than partitions).

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the hot partition A skewed key (e.g. partitioning by country when 80% of traffic is one country, or by a single huge tenant) sends most records to one partition — one broker and one consumer do all the work while the rest idle. Throughput collapses to a single partition's limit and that consumer lags. Fix: choose a high-cardinality, evenly-distributed key; if one key is genuinely huge, salt it (split into sub-keys) and accept that those sub-streams aren't mutually ordered. This is the hot-shard problem in log form.

Decision 2 — How durable is a write? (acks and the ISR)

Each partition is replicated; one replica is leader, the rest are followers. The in-sync replica set (ISR) is the replicas currently caught up. The producer's acks setting decides when a send is "successful": acks=0 (fire-and-forget, may lose), acks=1 (leader only), or acks=all (all in-sync replicas confirmed). With acks=all plus min.insync.replicas≥2, a record survives a single broker loss.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — acks=1 data loss This is Scenario 2. With acks=1 the producer is told "success" once the leader has the record — but if the leader crashes before any follower replicates it, that record is gone and a new leader never had it. Setting acks=all with min.insync.replicas=2 guarantees at least two replicas hold every acknowledged record (survives one failure), at the cost of higher write latency (waiting for replicas). It's the same synchronous-vs-asynchronous durability knob from replication, expressed as acks.

Decision 3 — How do consumers share the work? (consumer groups & rebalancing)

Consumers in a group divide a topic's partitions among themselves — each partition is owned by exactly one consumer in the group. When a consumer joins, leaves, or is declared dead (missed heartbeats past the session timeout), Kafka rebalances: it reassigns partitions across the surviving members. Different groups read the same topic independently (fan-out).

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the rebalance storm This is Scenario 1. The classic (eager) rebalance is stop-the-world: every consumer stops processing while partitions are reassigned. During a rolling restart of a large group, if consumers take longer to start than the session timeout, Kafka keeps marking them dead, and each change triggers another full rebalance — a storm that drops throughput to zero (240 consumers → 9 minutes of churn). Fixes: raise session.timeout.ms above startup time, use static group membership (stable IDs so a restart looks like "the same consumer briefly left," not a new join), and use incremental cooperative rebalancing (Kafka 2.4+) so consumers keep processing partitions that aren't moving instead of all stopping.

Decision 4 — What delivery guarantee do you need?

Three semantics, by where you commit the offset relative to processing:

  • At-most-once — commit offset before processing; a crash loses the record. Rare.
  • At-least-once — commit after processing; a crash re-delivers. The common default — no loss, but duplicates.
  • Exactly-once (EOS) — Kafka's idempotent producer + transactions make a read-process-write cycle atomic within Kafka.
The failure that happens when you get this wrong — duplicate processing This is Scenario 3. At-least-once (the default) means a crash between processing and offset-commit causes re-delivery — harmless for an idempotent update, a double-charge for a payment. Two fixes: (1) make the consumer idempotent — dedupe on an idempotency key / record key so re-processing is a no-op (works for any external side-effect); (2) use Kafka exactly-once semantics — idempotent producer + transactions — but only when the side-effect is itself in Kafka (a Kafka-to-Kafka pipeline). EOS does not magically make an external card charge exactly-once; for that you need idempotency at the external system. "Exactly-once delivery" across systems is impossible; "exactly-once effect" via idempotency is what you actually build.

At a glance — Kafka vs a traditional queue

FacetTraditional queue (RabbitMQ/SQS)Kafka (log)
After consumptionMessage deletedRetained — replayable
Consumers per messageUsually oneMany independent groups (fan-out)
OrderingPer-queue (often none under concurrency)Per-partition
ThroughputGoodVery high (sequential disk, batching)
Replay historyNoYes (rewind offset)
Best forTask queues, RPC-ish work distributionEvent streaming, log aggregation, CDC, replayable pipelines
The rule in one lineReach for Kafka/a log when you need replay, many consumers of the same events, or huge ordered-per-key throughput. Reach for a traditional queue (Queues) for simple work distribution where a message is a task to do once and discard. (Full broker-choice guide in Choosing a Message Broker.)

Self-check — can you reconstruct the three scenarios?

  • Scenario 1 (rebalance storm): What is a rebalance, why did rolling restarts cascade into 9 minutes of churn, and what three things fix it?
  • Scenario 2 (lost message): Why does acks=1 lose the message, and what does acks=all + min.insync.replicas=2 buy and cost?
  • Scenario 3 (double charge): Which delivery semantic causes duplicates, and why is idempotency — not Kafka EOS — the fix for an external side-effect?
  • Why is ordering only per-partition, and what must records that need ordering share?
  • How does a log differ from a traditional queue, and when do you pick each?

The shape to remember: Kafka is 4 decisions — partitioning, acks/ISR durability, consumer groups, delivery semantics — each with a nested failure: hot partition, acks=1 loss, rebalance storm, duplicate processing.

The takeawayKinesis and Pub/Sub give you the log model without managing brokers, with the same core trade-offs (shards/partitions = ordering+parallelism unit, retention, consumer offsets). Managed Kafka (MSK/Confluent) keeps the exact Kafka API and the same rebalance/acks/partition decisions — you still own those choices; the cloud just runs the brokers.

Famous implementations

The log made concrete. Same facets — model, ordering/durability, scaling, and the failure to watch.

Kafka

  • Model. Partitioned, replicated commit log; pull-based consumers with committed offsets; consumer groups for fan-out + parallelism.
  • Ordering/durability. Per-partition order; acks/ISR durability; idempotent producer + transactions for EOS within Kafka.
  • Scaling. Add partitions/brokers; KRaft mode removes the ZooKeeper dependency (Kafka now self-manages metadata via Raft).
  • The failure to watch. Rebalance storms on large groups (Scenario 1); hot partitions from skewed keys; under-replicated partitions on broker loss.

Apache Pulsar

  • Model. Log like Kafka but with a separated storage layer (BookKeeper) and segment-level scaling; supports both queue and stream subscription modes.
  • Ordering/durability. Per-partition order; storage/compute separation eases scaling and tiered storage.
  • Scaling. Brokers are stateless (state in BookKeeper), so rebalancing is cheaper; built-in multi-tenancy and geo-replication.
  • The failure to watch. More moving parts (broker + BookKeeper + metadata) to operate.

Kinesis / Google Pub-Sub

  • Model. Managed log/stream; Kinesis uses shards (= partitions); Pub/Sub is a managed topic with subscriptions.
  • Ordering/durability. Kinesis: per-shard order, fixed retention. Pub/Sub: at-least-once, optional ordering keys.
  • Scaling. Kinesis: resharding to add capacity. Pub/Sub: autoscaling, no shard management.
  • The failure to watch. Kinesis shard limits + resharding friction; per-service quotas; ordering guarantees differ from Kafka's, so don't assume parity.

Build it yourself

These click when you design the data flow. Work each before opening the approach.

Choose a partition key for an order pipeline

You stream order-events (created, paid, shipped) and must process each order's events in order, while spreading load evenly. What's your partition key, and how many partitions relative to consumers?

Reveal the approach

Key by order_id: all events for one order hash to the same partition, so they stay ordered relative to each other (created → paid → shipped), while different orders spread across partitions for parallelism. Choose partitions ≥ your target peak consumer count (a group can't have more active consumers than partitions), with headroom for growth — e.g. 24 partitions for ~12 consumers. Avoid keying by something low-cardinality (status, region) which would create a hot partition. If a single huge customer dominates, you can't both keep their events globally ordered and spread them — pick which matters.

Make a consumer exactly-once-effective

Your consumer reads "charge $50" events and calls a payment API. At-least-once delivery means it may re-read after a crash. Design it so a card is charged at most once, even with redelivery.

Reveal the approach

Don't rely on Kafka EOS — the side-effect (the charge) is external. Make the effect idempotent: derive a stable idempotency key from the event (e.g. order_id + event_offset or a payment-intent id) and pass it to the payment API; the payment system records keys it has already executed and returns the original result for a repeat instead of charging again. Commit the Kafka offset only after the charge call returns success. Now redelivery re-sends the same idempotency key → the payment system recognizes it → no second charge. The pattern is at-least-once delivery + idempotent side-effect = exactly-once effect (see idempotency & the outbox, Payment System).

Design-it-yourself: the open-ended ones

No single right answer — reason it out, then follow the link.

  • Tune a large consumer group against rebalance storms. Given a 200-consumer group on a rolling deploy, set the configs and choose the rebalance protocol to keep throughput up. → Resilience Patterns.
  • Replace fragile dual-writes with a log. A service writes to its DB and a queue and they drift. Redesign with Kafka + the outbox / CDC so the log is the source of truth. → Stream Processing & CDC, Event-Driven Architecture.
  • Design a replayable analytics pipeline. Many teams need the same event stream for different views. Use the log's retention + consumer groups so a new consumer can backfill from history without affecting others. → Ad Click Aggregation.
