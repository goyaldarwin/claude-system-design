Replication Deep Dive

Keeping copies of your data on more than one machine is how you survive a disk dying, serve reads near the user, and take a node down for maintenance without an outage. But the moment there's more than one copy, they can disagree — and every replication design is really a set of answers about who can accept writes, when copies sync, and what happens when they diverge.

The trade-off Durability, read scale, and locality — in exchange for replication lag, the risk of stale or lost writes, and conflict resolution. More copies mean you can lose a node and stay up, spread reads, and put data near users. You pay with the gap between a write and its copies (lag), the bugs that gap causes, and the work of reconciling copies that drift apart.

What replication is

Replication means keeping a copy of the same data on multiple nodes. You do it for three reasons: durability/availability (a node dies, a copy survives), read scalability (spread reads across copies), and locality (put a copy near the user to cut latency). The hard part isn't copying — it's that the copies inevitably fall out of sync, and you must decide what correctness you're willing to give up while they're inconsistent.

Three topologies answer "who can accept a write":

  • Single-leader — one node (the leader/primary) takes all writes and streams them to read-only followers. Simple, no write conflicts; the leader is a bottleneck and a failover point.
  • Multi-leader — several nodes accept writes (often one per region) and replicate to each other. Great for multi-region writes; you must resolve conflicting concurrent writes.
  • Leaderless — any replica accepts any write (the Dynamo model); reads and writes use quorums. Highly available; you handle conflicts and read-repair.
The one-line frameReplication trades consistency and conflict-handling effort for durability, read scale, and locality. The instant a second copy exists, you've signed up for replication lag and the choice of what to do when copies disagree — and the topology you pick is that choice.

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide first. If you know all three cold, skip to the comparison.

Scenario 1 — GitLab, 31 January 2017 (real incident) A GitLab engineer, fighting a database-load spike, believed replication had stalled on the secondary because its data directory looked wrong. To re-seed it he ran rm -rf on the PostgreSQL data folder — but on the primary by mistake, deleting 300+ GB of live data. Then the recovery nightmare: of five backup/replication safeguards, none worked — the only usable backup was 6 hours old. Which two replication decisions does this disaster touch, and why did "we have replicas and backups" still mean data loss?
Scenario 2 — the lost acknowledged write Your database has one leader and async followers for speed. A write is accepted and acknowledged to the user by the leader. A heartbeat later, the leader's disk dies before any follower received that write. A follower is promoted to leader. Where did the acknowledged write go, and what knob would have prevented it — at what cost?
Scenario 3 — "I posted it, why is it gone?" A user updates their profile, then immediately reloads. The write went to the leader; the read was load-balanced to a follower that hadn't received it yet. The user sees their old profile and thinks the save failed. Name this bug and two others caused by the same root, and the cheap fix for each — without making every read hit the leader.

Scenario 1 is topology + failover and why backups aren't replication. Scenario 2 is sync vs async. Scenario 3 is hiding replication lag. Each maps to a decision below.

The four decisions that define a replication design

Every replication setup is these four choices. Each has a named failure that bites when you get it wrong — nested right under it.

Decision 1 — Who accepts writes? (the topology)

This is the foundational choice (single-leader / multi-leader / leaderless, above). It determines everything downstream: single-leader has no write conflicts but one bottleneck and a failover event; multi-leader and leaderless scale writes and survive more failures but make conflict resolution your problem. Pick the simplest topology that meets your write-scale and locality needs — for most systems that's single-leader.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — split-brain On a single-leader failover, if the old leader isn't reliably stopped and a new one is promoted, you can end up with two leaders both accepting writes — split-brain. The two diverge and must be merged by hand (exactly GitHub's 2018 24-hour ordeal). The fix is consensus-based failover (fence the old leader, elect via majority) and a fencing token so a zombie old-leader's writes are rejected. Part of Scenario 1: the engineer misjudged replication state during exactly this kind of operational stress.

Decision 2 — When do copies sync? (synchronous vs asynchronous)

When the leader gets a write, does it wait for followers to confirm before acknowledging the client (synchronous) or acknowledge immediately and copy in the background (asynchronous)? Sync guarantees the write survives a leader loss but makes every write as slow as the slowest follower and stalls if a follower is down. Async is fast and resilient to slow followers but risks losing recently-acknowledged writes. The common compromise is semi-synchronous: require one follower to confirm (durability) while the rest catch up async (speed).

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the lost acknowledged write This is Scenario 2. With pure async replication, a write acknowledged by the leader that hasn't reached any follower is gone if the leader dies before copying it — the user was told "saved" but it wasn't durable. Semi-sync (at least one confirming follower) prevents it at the cost of write latency. This is the same durability-vs-latency knob as quorum writes, and the heart of Scenario 1's data loss: async lag meant the promoted copy was behind, and backups were stale.

Decision 3 — How do you hide replication lag from users?

With async followers there's always a window where a follower is behind the leader (replication lag). Reads from a lagging follower cause three classic anomalies — and each has a cheap, targeted session guarantee as a fix, so you don't have to route everything to the leader:

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the three lag bugs (1) Read-your-writes violation (Scenario 3): you don't see your own just-made write. Fix: route a user's reads to the leader (or a known-current replica) for a short window after they write. (2) Monotonic-reads violation: a second read shows older data than the first, because it hit a more-lagged replica — time appears to go backwards. Fix: pin each user to one replica (sticky routing). (3) Consistent-prefix violation: you see an answer before its question, because writes arrived out of order across partitions. Fix: ensure causally-related writes go to the same partition / are ordered. The root cause of all three is the same lag; the fixes are session guarantees, not full strong consistency.

Decision 4 — How do you resolve conflicts? (multi-leader / leaderless only)

Once more than one node accepts writes, two clients can write the same key concurrently and both succeed. You must decide the resolution rule: last-write-wins (simple, but silently drops a write), application merge (keep both, let code reconcile), version vectors to detect concurrency, or a CRDT that merges automatically without loss. The right answer depends on the data: a counter must never lose increments; a "user bio" can tolerate last-write-wins.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — silent conflict loss Defaulting to last-write-wins on data that can't tolerate it — a shopping cart, a counter, a set — means concurrent writes vanish with no error, the worst kind of bug because nothing alerts you. Use version vectors to detect that two writes were concurrent, then resolve with an app-aware merge or a CRDT built for that data shape. Know your store's default conflict rule before you trust it.

The rule in one lineDefault to single-leader (simplest, no conflicts) and add session guarantees to hide lag. Go multi-leader only when you genuinely need writes in multiple regions, and leaderless when always-on availability beats consistency. The more nodes can write, the more conflict-resolution work you own.

Famous implementations

The topologies made concrete. Same facets — topology, sync model, conflict handling, and the failure to watch.

Single-leader — PostgreSQL / MySQL

  • Topology. One primary, N read replicas.
  • Sync model. Streaming WAL/binlog; configurable async, semi-sync, or sync standbys.
  • Conflict handling. None — only the primary writes.
  • The failure to watch. Failover is the danger moment: split-brain if the old primary isn't fenced; lost writes if a lagging replica is promoted. Replica lag causes the three read anomalies. (GitLab 2017 is the cautionary tale.)

Multi-leader — active-active (CockroachDB-style / multi-region MySQL)

  • Topology. Multiple write-accepting nodes, often one per region for low local write latency.
  • Sync model. Asynchronous between leaders (each region commits locally, replicates after).
  • Conflict handling. Required — last-write-wins, app merge, or CRDTs; the central design problem.
  • The failure to watch. Concurrent edits to the same key in two regions conflict; naive LWW silently drops one. Best reserved for partitionable data (per-region ownership) to minimize conflicts.

Leaderless — Cassandra / DynamoDB / Riak

  • Topology. Any replica accepts any read/write; placement via consistent hashing.
  • Sync model. Tunable quorums (R + W > N for strong-ish reads); read-repair + hinted handoff heal divergence.
  • Conflict handling. Version vectors detect concurrency; CRDTs or app logic resolve.
  • The failure to watch. Wrong quorum settings give silent staleness; forgetting scheduled anti-entropy repair lets replicas drift.

Build it yourself

Replication clicks when you design around its failure modes. Work each before opening the approach.

Implement read-your-writes over async replicas

Your reads are load-balanced across async followers (fast, but lagging). Design a scheme so a user always sees their own just-made write, without sending every read to the leader.

Reveal the approach

Track the moment (or the write's log position / timestamp) of a user's last write. For a short window after a write — or until a replica is known to have caught up past that position — route that user's reads to the leader or to a replica whose replication position is ≥ the user's last write. Everyone else keeps reading from any follower. You pay leader load only for recently-writing users, for a few seconds each, instead of forcing all reads to the leader. This is the read-your-writes session guarantee implemented as routing, not as global strong consistency.

Design a failover that can't split-brain

Your single leader can die. Design automated failover so exactly one new leader is promoted, the old one can't keep accepting writes if it comes back, and you don't lose acknowledged writes.

Reveal the approach

Use a consensus-backed election (or a coordination kernel, 36): a majority must agree on the new leader, so a minority-side old leader can't also win. Fence the old leader — revoke its write capability (STONITH / lease expiry) and give the new leader a higher fencing token that the storage layer enforces, so a zombie old-leader's writes are rejected. To avoid losing acknowledged writes, run semi-synchronous replication so any acknowledged write is on at least one follower, and only promote a follower that holds the latest committed position. The combination — majority election + fencing + sync-confirmed promotion — is what prevents both split-brain and silent data loss.

Design-it-yourself: the open-ended ones

No single right answer — reason it out, then follow the link.

  • Pick a topology per workload. For a global social app, decide single vs multi vs leaderless for: the user table, the activity feed, a per-region session store — and justify each by write-scale, locality, and conflict tolerance. → Multi-Region & DR.
  • Make backups actually restorable. Drawing on GitLab 2017, design a backup + replication strategy where no single mistake loses data and restores are tested. → Resilience Patterns.
  • Resolve cart conflicts without loss. A multi-region cart gets concurrent add/remove. Design conflict resolution that never loses an item the user added. → CRDTs.
