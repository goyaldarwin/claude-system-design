Object Storage & CDN

Big files — images, video, backups, model weights — don't belong in your database or on your app servers' disks. Object storage (S3 and friends) holds unlimited blobs cheaply and durably behind a simple HTTP API, and a CDN caches them at the network edge near users. Together they take the heaviest, highest-bandwidth traffic off your origin entirely — but only if you get the upload path, access control, and cache invalidation right.

The trade-off Cheap, near-infinite, durable storage and global low-latency delivery — in exchange for object (not file/block) semantics, eventual cache freshness, and the discipline of presigned access and invalidation. You offload bytes and bandwidth to systems built for it — 11-nines durability, edge caching worldwide. You give up filesystem semantics (no partial in-place edits), accept that cached content can be stale until invalidated, and must manage secure access and cache control yourself.

Why bytes leave the app tier

Storing large files on app-server disks or in your database is a trap: disks fill and aren't shared across servers, databases bloat and slow down, and serving big files ties up app threads and bandwidth. Object storage solves this: a flat namespace of objects (a blob of bytes + metadata, addressed by a key) in buckets, accessed over HTTP, with effectively unlimited capacity, very low cost, and extreme durability (e.g. S3's "11 nines"). It's not a filesystem — you PUT/GET/DELETE whole objects; there's no partial in-place edit, no real directories (just key prefixes).

A CDN (Content Delivery Network) then caches those objects (and other cacheable responses) at edge PoPs around the world, so a user in Tokyo is served from Tokyo, not your origin in Virginia — cutting latency and offloading the vast majority of read traffic from your origin.

The one-line frameObject storage + CDN trade filesystem semantics and instant freshness for near-infinite cheap durable storage and global low-latency delivery. You move the heaviest bytes and bandwidth out of your app entirely; in return you work with whole-object operations, manage secure access (presigned URLs), and accept that the edge can serve stale content until you invalidate or version it.

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide first. If you know all three cold, skip to the comparison.

Scenario 1 — the upload that melts the app servers Users upload large videos. Each upload streams through your app servers to object storage — so your app tier's CPU, memory, and bandwidth are consumed proxying bytes, and a burst of uploads knocks the API over. How should the bytes get from the user's browser to object storage without passing through your servers, and how do you keep that secure?
Scenario 2 — Fastly, 8 June 2021 (real incident) A single Fastly customer changed their CDN configuration. That change tripped a latent bug, crashed an edge node, and — because edges shared one global config service — the failure spread across the network. Within minutes ~85% of Fastly's traffic dropped; Reddit, Spotify, Amazon, and major news sites returned errors. What property of the CDN's design turned one customer's config change into a global outage, and what's the architectural lesson?
Scenario 3 — the deploy users can't see You ship a new version of your site's JavaScript and CSS. Users keep getting the old files for hours — the CDN cached them with a long TTL and doesn't know they changed. Forcing a purge of everything is slow and hammers your origin. How do you make new asset versions appear instantly without purging the cache or shortening TTLs?

Scenario 1 is presigned/direct uploads. Scenario 2 is cost/access & blast radius. Scenario 3 is cache invalidation. Each maps to a decision below.

The four decisions that define the design

Designing object-storage + CDN delivery is these four choices. Each has a named failure nested under it.

Decision 1 — How do clients upload and download? (presigned URLs, multipart)

The right pattern is to keep your app out of the byte path. For uploads/downloads, your app issues a presigned URL — a time-limited, permission-scoped URL the client uses to talk to object storage directly. The client uploads straight to the bucket; your app only mints the URL and records metadata. For large files, multipart upload splits the object into parts uploaded in parallel and reassembled, with retries of failed parts — resilient and fast.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — proxying bytes through your app This is Scenario 1. Routing uploads/downloads through your application tier means every byte consumes your servers' CPU, memory, bandwidth, and request threads — a few large concurrent uploads can exhaust the pool and take down your API, and you pay to move bytes your app doesn't even process. Presigned URLs eliminate this: the client transfers bytes directly to/from object storage (secured by the URL's scope and expiry), and your app handles only the lightweight metadata. Combine with multipart upload for big files (parallel parts, resumable) and you've removed the heavy traffic from your servers entirely.

Decision 2 — What durability and consistency do you actually get?

Object storage is built for durability (many redundant copies; S3 advertises 99.999999999% — 11 nines — so you effectively never lose an object) and high availability. Consistency has improved: S3 now offers strong read-after-write consistency for new objects (a GET right after a PUT sees the data). But you should still understand the model — overwrites and listings, and other stores' models, may behave differently, and you design around what's guaranteed, not what usually happens.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — assuming a consistency you don't have Historically S3 was eventually consistent for some operations, and code that did "write an object, then immediately list the bucket and expect to see it" could intermittently miss it — a flaky bug that's hard to reproduce. S3's read-after-write for new objects fixed the common case, but the lesson stands: know your store's exact consistency guarantees (read-after-write? list-after-write? overwrite visibility?) and don't build logic that assumes stronger consistency than documented. When in doubt, drive workflows off an explicit event/metadata record you control rather than off listing the bucket.

Decision 3 — How does the CDN cache, and how do you invalidate?

A CDN caches responses at the edge keyed by URL, for a duration set by cache-control headers (TTL). A cache hit serves from the nearby PoP (fast, offloads origin); a miss fetches from origin and caches it. The hard part is invalidation — when content changes, the edge still holds the old copy until its TTL expires. Two approaches: explicit purge (tell the CDN to drop a URL — slow, can stampede the origin) or, far better, content versioning (change the URL when the content changes).

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the stale-after-deploy cache This is Scenario 3. Long TTLs give great cache hit rates but mean a deploy's new assets are invisible — users get old JS/CSS until the TTL lapses. Purging everything on each deploy is slow and triggers a stampede of misses onto your origin. The clean fix is cache-busting via versioned URLs / content hashing: name each asset by a hash of its content (app.9f2c1a.js) and set a very long TTL (immutable). A new build produces a new filename → a new URL → an automatic cache miss → instant freshness, with no purge and maximal caching of unchanged files. (Invalidation is famously "one of the two hard things in computer science" — versioning sidesteps it.)

Decision 4 — How do you control cost, access, and blast radius?

Two more levers. Cost: storage classes (hot/standard vs infrequent-access vs archival/Glacier) and lifecycle policies move objects to cheaper tiers or delete them by age — you pay for access speed you actually need. Access & safety: keep buckets private by default (presigned URLs or signed CDN URLs for private content), and treat configuration changes as dangerous — the CDN/edge config is a global control plane.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — one change taking down everything This is Scenario 2. Fastly's edges shared a single global configuration domain, so one customer's config change could activate a latent bug and cascade across the entire network — 85% of traffic gone in minutes. The architectural lesson is about blast radius: a global, instantly-propagating control plane means a single bad change (or bug) is a single point of failure for everyone. Mitigations are the same ones you'd apply to any config system — staged/canary config rollout (don't push to all edges at once), validation before propagation, fast rollback, and isolating tenants/cells so one customer's change can't take down others. (Public-facing note: a CDN is also your DDoS/edge security layer — another reason the edge config is high-stakes.)

The takeawayThe canonical web architecture is object store as origin + CDN in front: static assets and media live in S3/GCS/Blob, the CDN caches them at the edge, clients upload directly via presigned URLs, and assets are content-hashed for instant cache busting. Independent CDNs (Cloudflare, Fastly, Akamai) add edge compute, WAF, and DDoS protection — and, as Fastly 2021 showed, become a critical dependency whose config changes carry global blast radius.

Famous implementations

The stores and CDNs made concrete. Same facets — what it is, the model, and the failure to watch.

S3 / Google Cloud Storage / Azure Blob

  • What it is. Massively-durable object stores; flat buckets of key-addressed objects over HTTP.
  • Model. PUT/GET/DELETE whole objects; 11-nines durability; read-after-write consistency for new objects; presigned URLs, multipart upload, storage classes, lifecycle, versioning.
  • The failure to watch. Treating it like a filesystem (no partial edits, no real dirs); assuming stronger consistency than documented; accidental public buckets.

CloudFront / Cloudflare / Fastly / Akamai

  • What it is. Global edge cache + delivery networks; many also offer edge compute, WAF, and DDoS protection.
  • Model. Cache by URL at PoPs with TTL/cache-control; origin pull on miss; purge or (better) versioned URLs for freshness; signed URLs for private content.
  • The failure to watch. Global config blast radius (Fastly 2021); cache-key/invalidation mistakes serving stale or wrong content; becoming a critical single dependency.

Composed patterns (static site, media pipeline)

  • What it is. The standard compositions — static-site hosting (S3+CloudFront), user-upload pipelines (presigned PUT → processing → CDN delivery), and video (object store + transcode + CDN, see YouTube).
  • Model. Object store as the durable origin; CDN as the read-scaling edge; app only on the control/metadata path.
  • The failure to watch. Forgetting cache-busting (stale deploys); proxying bytes through the app; unbounded storage cost without lifecycle policies.

Build it yourself

These click when you design the byte path. Work each before opening the approach.

Design a direct browser upload for large files

Users upload multi-GB videos from the browser. Design the flow so bytes never touch your app servers, uploads are resumable, and only authorized users can upload.

Reveal the approach

(1) Browser asks your app to start an upload; your app authenticates the user, decides the object key, and returns presigned URLs for a multipart upload (one presigned URL per part, plus an upload id). (2) The browser uploads parts directly to the bucket in parallel using those URLs — resumable (retry just the failed part), and your servers never see the bytes. (3) The browser tells your app the upload is complete; your app finalizes the multipart upload and records metadata (and may emit an "upload complete" event to kick off transcoding/virus-scan). Security: presigned URLs are scoped to the exact key+operation and expire quickly, so they can't be reused or abused. This removes the byte traffic from your app (Scenario 1's fix) and handles huge files robustly.

Make a deploy's new assets appear instantly

Your site's JS/CSS is cached at the CDN with a 1-year TTL for performance, but deploys must take effect immediately. Design the freshness scheme without purging or shortening TTLs.

Reveal the approach

Use content-hashed filenames: the build names each asset by a hash of its contents (app.9f2c1a.js, styles.4be0.css) and marks them Cache-Control: immutable, max-age=31536000. The HTML (or a small manifest) references the current hashed filenames and is served with a short/no-cache TTL. On deploy, changed files get new hashes → new URLs the CDN has never seen → automatic cache miss → fetched fresh; unchanged files keep their old hashed URL and stay cached. Result: instant freshness for what changed, maximal caching for what didn't, no purge (so no origin stampede), and no risk of users mixing old and new files. This sidesteps cache invalidation entirely — you change the key instead of expiring the value (Scenario 3).

Design-it-yourself: the open-ended ones
