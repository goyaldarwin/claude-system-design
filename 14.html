A Framework for Approaching System Design

The repeatable process for assembling the building blocks under interview or design-doc pressure: clarify, estimate, define the API, model the data, sketch the high-level design, then deep-dive the bottleneck. Plus the anchor numbers that let you reach a defensible estimate in under five minutes.

The point A design review forgives a wrong component sooner than an estimate you can't defend. The framework's value is sequencing: requirements gate everything, the estimate sizes everything, and the deep-dive is where you spend your time — not drawing boxes nobody questioned.

The seven steps

A reliable order. The early steps gate the later ones — skipping ahead is how designs go wrong.

Step 1 — Clarify requirements & scope

Pin functional requirements (what it must do), non-functional ones (scale, latency, availability targets), and explicitly defer the rest. A design is only "right" relative to requirements — so nail them first. Ask: read- or write-heavy? How many users / requests per second? What's the consistency and latency SLA? Designing for unstated scale is the classic way to lose points.

Step 2 — Back-of-the-envelope estimation

The step a review will least forgive you skipping. Turn the requirements into numbers: write rate, read rate (and their ratio), storage/day, bandwidth, working-set size. The ratio of reads to writes alone often dictates the architecture (it's why Twitter pre-computes timelines). You don't need arithmetic speed — you need anchor numbers in your head to reach a defensible order of magnitude fast.

Step 3 — Define the APIs / interfaces

State the contract before the internals — the key endpoints, their parameters and return shapes. This forces clarity on what the system actually does and surfaces missing requirements. (A postTweet(...) / getTimeline(...) pair tells you more about a design than three boxes do.)

Step 4 — Design the data model

The core entities, their keys, and the access patterns. The model + access pattern is what drives the database choice and the sharding key — get this wrong and every query fights the store.

Step 5 — High-level architecture

Now draw the boxes: clients → edge/LB → stateless app tier → data tier (+ cache, queue, object store), and the request flow through them. Keep it the shape, not the detail — the detail comes next, only where it matters.

Step 6 — Deep-dive the bottleneck component

This is where you spend your time. Identify the one or two components that are hard at this scale (the fan-out, the hot shard, the consensus group) and design them in depth. A design review rewards depth on the hard part far more than breadth on the easy parts.

Step 7 — Identify bottlenecks, failure modes & evolution

Close by stress-testing your own design: where does it bend as load grows, what happens when each component fails, and how does it evolve? Naming the failure modes (the six bottlenecks) before someone else does is the principal-level move.

The estimation numbers worth memorizing

The framework lives or dies on Step 2. A small set of anchors lets you reach a defensible order of magnitude in minutes.

Powers of two, and the one conversion that matters

PowerApproxName
210~1 thousand1 KB
220~1 million1 MB
230~1 billion1 GB
240~1 trillion1 TB

The single most useful conversion in capacity planning: 1 day ≈ 86,400 s ≈ 105 s. So 1M events/day ≈ ~12/sec; 1B/day ≈ ~12k/sec; 100B/day ≈ ~1.2M/sec. Memorize the 1B/day → 12k/sec mapping and you can scale anything mentally. (Months ~2.6M s, years ~31.5M s ≈ π×107.)

Why this matters in the frameworkThe ladder converts an abstract SLO into a hard topology constraint. "p99 under 200 ms" with a 150 ms cross-continent round trip leaves almost no budget — so the design must serve from a regional replica or CDN edge. Step 6 uses the ladder to check whether a design can even hit the Step 1 SLO.

A worked sanity check — does it even fit on one box?

The fastest reality check: estimate the working set and compare to one machine. Example — cache the last 3 days of tweets at 100M/day × ~310 bytes ≈ ~90 GB. That fits in RAM on a single large box — so the first cut can be one replicated cache tier, not a sharded one. Running this "fits on one box?" check early tells you whether you even have a distributed-systems problem yet, or whether you're over-engineering (the scale-when-you-need-to discipline).

Self-check

  • List the seven steps in order, and say why the order matters (what gates what).
  • Why is Step 2 (estimation) the one a review least forgives, and what's the 1B/day→? conversion?
  • How does the latency ladder turn a "p99 < 200 ms" SLO into a constraint on topology?
  • What does the "does it fit on one box?" check tell you, and how does it guard against over-engineering?
  • In Step 6, how do you decide which component to deep-dive?

The shape to remember: clarify → estimate → API → data model → high-level → deep-dive the bottleneck → failure modes. Carry the anchor numbers so Step 2 takes minutes.

Apply it next on the design problems (15–29); the deep frame for failure modes is Foundations' six bottlenecks.

Practice it yourself

Run Step 2 cold on a new problem

Pick a system (say, a URL shortener at 100M new links/day). Without looking anything up, derive the write rate, read rate (assume 10:1 reads), 5-year storage, and whether the working set fits on one box.

Reveal the approach

Writes: 100M/day ≈ ~1,200/s (using 1B/day→12k/s, scaled down 10×). Reads at 10:1 ≈ ~12,000/s. Storage: ~500 bytes/link × 100M/day ≈ 50 GB/day × 365 × 5 ≈ ~90 TB over 5 years — too big for one box's disk comfortably, so you'll shard storage, but the hot working set (recent/popular links) is far smaller and caches in RAM. The discipline: every number ends in a design consequence (shard storage, cache the hot set), and you reached it in two minutes using only the anchor conversions.

Practice the full framework

  • Run all 7 steps on a design problem before reading its lesson — then compare. → Twitter, Uber, BookMyShow.
  • Stress-test an existing design (Step 7): take any system you run and name where it bends and how each component fails. → the six bottlenecks.
