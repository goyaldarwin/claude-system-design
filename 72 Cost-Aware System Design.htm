Cost-Aware System Design

Treating cost as a first-class design axis — the trade-offs, models, and instincts that separate a design that works from one that works affordably.

In this lesson

  • Why cost is a design dimension, not an afterthought
  • The cost model of the major components

  • Concrete cost-reduction techniques
  • The FinOps mindset: cost as an SLI

Why cost is a design dimension, not an afterthought

Every architecture decision you make is a recurring bill. A latency target, a replication factor, a "let's just cache everything" reflex — each one shows up on next month's invoice and the one after that, forever, multiplied by your traffic. At small scale this is rounding error. At scale, an architectural choice that doubles a per-request cost can be the difference between a healthy gross margin and a company that burns cash on every user it acquires.

Junior engineers treat cost as something the finance team worries about after the system ships. A principal engineer treats it the way they treat latency, availability, and consistency: as a first-class axis they own. The instinct is not "make it cheap" — it's "know what this design costs, know why, and be able to defend the spend against the value it delivers." A design that meets every functional and reliability requirement but costs 5x what a comparable design would is a failed design, even if nothing ever pages.

Key insight Cost is just another non-functional requirement, and it trades off against the others exactly like they trade off against each other. More availability costs money. Lower latency costs money. Stronger consistency costs money. The principal-level skill is making those trades explicitly and with numbers, rather than discovering them on an invoice.

The cost model of the major components

You cannot reason about cost without a rough mental model of what each component charges you for. Cloud pricing is deliberately multi-dimensional, and the dimension that dominates your bill is almost never the one you were watching. The table below is the single most useful artifact in this lesson: for each building block, what actually drives the bill.

Compute: the serverless cost cliff

Serverless functions are billed per invocation and per GB-second. For spiky, low-duty-cycle, or unpredictable workloads they are wonderful: you pay nothing when idle and never provision for peak. But there is a cost cliff. Once a workload runs near-continuously, you are renting a fraction of a CPU at a steep per-second markup. A function pinned at high concurrency around the clock can cost several times what an equivalently sized reserved VM or container would. The crossover is roughly where sustained utilization climbs past ~40–60%. The principal move is to know which side of that cliff your workload sits on — and that decision belongs with the containers vs serverless trade-off.

Storage tiers: hot, warm, cold

Not all data is worth the same money to keep instantly available. Object stores expose this directly through storage classes. S3 Standard is priced for frequently accessed data; Standard-IA (infrequent access) is roughly half the storage price but adds a per-GB retrieval fee; Glacier and Glacier Deep Archive drop storage cost by an order of magnitude but charge real money and minutes-to-hours of latency to retrieve. The art is matching data temperature to tier — and never paying hot prices for cold bytes. We cover the mechanics in Object Storage & CDN.

Pitfall A managed database read replica is not "a bit of extra cost for reads" — it is a full second copy of compute and storage. Three read replicas plus a Multi-AZ standby can quietly turn one database into five billed instances. Before adding a replica to scale reads, ask whether a cache in front of the primary would absorb the same load for a fraction of the price.

Data transfer: the silent killer

Egress deserves its own section because it is the cost that hides best. It does not appear as a box on your architecture diagram, it is not provisioned, and you only discover it when the invoice arrives. There are three tiers, and they compound:

  1. Cross-AZ — traffic between availability zones in the same region is typically billed in both directions. A chatty service that hops zones on every call pays a tax on each hop.
  2. Cross-region — replicating data or routing requests between regions is materially more expensive than cross-AZ, and is the dominant hidden cost of going multi-region.
  3. Internet egress — data leaving the cloud to end users is the most expensive of all, often $0.05–$0.09/GB at typical tiers. Ingress is almost always free, which is exactly why egress is where the margin is taken.

Concrete failure modes: a microservice fanned out across AZs that serializes large payloads on every internal call; a database in one AZ with replicas in two others doing synchronous replication across the zone boundary; serving large media directly from object storage to users instead of through a CDN. Each is functionally correct and quietly expensive.

What strong engineers do They draw the data-flow diagram and annotate every arrow that crosses an AZ, region, or the public internet boundary, then multiply by traffic volume. A CDN in front of egress-heavy traffic (see CDN) is frequently the single highest-ROI cost change available — the CDN's egress is cheaper than the origin's, and cache hits eliminate origin egress entirely.

Key trade-offs, framed as cost

The trade-offs you already know from earlier lessons all have a cost face. Reframing them this way is what makes you cost-aware rather than merely cost-conscious.

The cost of each additional "nine"

Availability is bought in increasingly expensive increments. Going from 99.9% (~8.8 hours of downtime/year) to 99.99% (~52 minutes) typically means multi-AZ redundancy, automated failover, and removing single points of failure. Pushing to 99.999% (~5 minutes) usually demands active-active multi-region, which multiplies infrastructure and adds the cross-region egress and replication costs above. Each nine roughly multiplies cost while shrinking the absolute downtime saved. The principal question is never "can we be more available?" but "is the next nine worth its price given our error budget and what an hour of downtime actually costs us?"

Consistency as a cost choice

Strong consistency requires coordination — quorum reads/writes, consensus rounds, cross-region round-trips. That coordination is paid in latency and in the extra replicas and traffic needed to maintain it. Eventual consistency lets you serve from the nearest, cheapest replica with no coordination. Choosing eventual consistency where the domain tolerates it (a like count, a feed) is as much a cost decision as a latency one.

Precompute vs compute-on-read

This is the storage-cost-vs-CPU-cost dial. Precomputing and materializing results (fan-out-on-write feeds, materialized views, denormalized copies) trades cheap storage for expensive CPU at read time — great when reads vastly outnumber writes. Computing on read trades storage for CPU the other way — better when the data is written far more than it is read, or is read so rarely that materializing it wastes storage on results nobody asks for. Neither is free; you are choosing which resource to spend.

Replication factor vs durability vs cost

A replication factor of 3 is the common default because it survives one failure while a second is being repaired. RF=5 buys more durability and read fan-out but costs 5x the storage and the cross-node/cross-AZ traffic to keep copies in sync. Erasure coding (used in object stores and some warm-data systems) achieves comparable durability at far lower storage overhead than full replication, at the cost of CPU to reconstruct and higher latency — a deliberate cost trade for data that is durable-but-cold.

Trade-off Multi-region is the great cost multiplier. It can roughly double your steady-state infrastructure (two of everything) and add ongoing cross-region replication and egress on top. It buys regional-failure survival and lower latency for a global audience. The mistake is going multi-region for an availability target a single well-architected region could meet — paying the multiplier for a nine you didn't need. See Multi-Region & DR.

Concrete cost-reduction techniques

Instinct is necessary but not sufficient; you also need a toolbox of moves whose cost mechanics you can explain on demand.

  • Cache to cut database cost. A read-through cache with a high hit ratio means a cheap RAM lookup absorbs load that would otherwise hit your most expensive tier — the database — deferring or avoiding read replicas and IOPS provisioning entirely. The cache costs RAM; the DB read it prevents costs far more.
  • Batch and compress to cut egress. Coalescing many small messages into fewer larger ones amortizes per-request overhead, and compression directly shrinks the GB you are billed for on every egress arrow. For text and JSON-heavy payloads this can cut transfer cost by 60–80% for the CPU cost of (de)compression.
  • Autoscaling + spot/preemptible instances. Autoscaling kills the "provisioned for peak, idle at night" waste. Spot/preemptible instances run at 60–90% off on-demand pricing in exchange for the cloud reclaiming them on short notice — ideal for stateless workers, batch jobs, and anything fault-tolerant. The design discipline is making the workload tolerate eviction (checkpointing, queue-driven retries) so it can ride the cheap capacity.
  • Data lifecycle & TTL. Most data gets colder with age. Lifecycle policies that transition objects Standard → IA → Glacier on a schedule, and TTLs that expire data nobody will read again, are pure savings — you stop paying hot prices for cold or dead bytes. The cheapest byte is the one you deleted.
  • Right-size the storage tier. Match temperature to class deliberately rather than defaulting everything to Standard. The same discipline applies to compute (don't run on the latest premium instance family if a cheaper one meets the SLO) and databases (don't provision IOPS you never consume).
  • Tail-latency vs cost. Driving p99.9 down often requires aggressive over-provisioning, redundant requests (hedging), and excess capacity that sits idle just to absorb rare spikes. Ask whether the user-facing requirement is genuinely p99.9 or whether p99 is fine. Chasing a tail percentile the product doesn't need is one of the most common silent cost sinks.
Pitfall Over-caching has its own cost. Cache RAM is among the most expensive storage you can buy per byte, and a cache sized far beyond its working set is money sitting idle. "Cache everything" is not a cost strategy — cache the hot set, size to the working set, and let cold data fall through to cheaper tiers.

The FinOps mindset: cost as an SLI

FinOps is the practice of bringing the same engineering rigor to cloud cost that SRE brought to reliability. The core shift is from "cost is a monthly surprise" to "cost is a measured, attributed, owned signal."

Cost attribution and tagging

You cannot manage what you cannot attribute. Tagging every resource by team, service, and environment turns a single opaque cloud bill into a per-service P&L. Without it, no one owns any cost and everything is "infrastructure overhead." With it, the team that owns the expensive service sees its own line item and is accountable for it.

Cost per request / per user / per tenant as an SLI

The most powerful idea in this lesson: treat unit cost — cost per request, per active user, per tenant, per transaction — as a service-level indicator you track over time, exactly like latency or error rate (see SLI/SLO). Absolute cloud spend rising is ambiguous (maybe you grew). Cost-per-request rising while traffic is flat is an unambiguous regression that should be investigated like a latency regression. In multi-tenant systems, cost-per-tenant also reveals which customers are unprofitable — a product and pricing signal, not just an engineering one.

Build vs buy, and total cost of ownership

A managed service has a higher sticker price than the open-source equivalent on raw VMs, but the sticker price is not the cost. TCO includes the engineering time to operate, patch, and be paged for self-hosted infrastructure — often the larger number. Build-vs-buy is a TCO decision: buy when the undifferentiated heavy lifting would consume scarce engineering capacity; build when the component is core to your differentiation or when scale makes the managed premium genuinely dominate the bill. The naive comparison ("the managed version costs 3x the EC2 price!") ignores the salaried engineers the managed version replaces.

What strong engineers do They present a design with its cost model attached: "this is roughly $X/month at current traffic, dominated by Y, and scales like Z per additional million requests." They name the unit-cost metric they'd track, they flag the one or two line items that will dominate the bill, and they identify the cheapest acceptable point on each non-functional axis rather than the most impressive one.

Putting it together

Cost-awareness is not a separate phase bolted onto the end of design — it is a lens applied throughout. When you reach for a read replica, ask whether a cache is cheaper. When you propose multi-region, name the nine it buys and the multiplier it costs. When you draw a data flow, annotate the egress arrows. When you set a latency target, confirm the product needs that tail. When you pick a storage class, match it to the data's temperature. None of this means choosing the cheapest option every time — sometimes the expensive option is correct and you should spend confidently. It means that every spend is chosen, defensible, and measured, rather than inherited by accident and discovered on an invoice.

This ties directly into the choices made elsewhere in the course: the compute model, the storage and CDN strategy, the multi-region posture, the reliability targets, and the caching design are all, simultaneously, cost decisions. A principal engineer holds the cost consequence of each in mind as they make it.

Key insight The cheapest design and the best design are usually not the same design — but the best design is one whose cost you understood and chose on purpose. The failure mode is not spending money; it is spending money you didn't know you were spending on a property you didn't need.

Mastery check

  • Can you sketch, for a given component (compute, object storage, managed DB, cache), what actually drives its bill — and name the dimension most teams overlook?
  • Why is data egress so often the largest hidden line item, and how would you find and reduce it in an existing architecture?
  • Can you explain, with rough numbers, the serverless cost cliff — at what utilization does serverless stop being cheaper than a reserved VM, and why?
  • Why does each additional "nine" of availability cost roughly a multiple of the previous one, and how would you decide whether the next nine is worth buying?
  • Can you frame precompute-vs-compute-on-read, consistency, and replication factor each as a cost trade-off, naming which resource you spend in each direction?
  • Why is "cache everything" not a cost strategy, and how do you size a cache so it saves money rather than wasting it?
  • What does it mean to treat cost-per-request as an SLI, and why is unit cost a more useful signal than absolute cloud spend?
