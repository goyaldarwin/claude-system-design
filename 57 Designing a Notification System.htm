Designing a Notification System

A multi-channel fan-out platform — push, SMS, email, in-app — that must be reliable, idempotent, preference-aware, and survive flaky third-party providers at billions of messages a day.

The bottleneck Latency & partial failure. fan-out delivery across providers, paid in retries, dedup, and per-channel rate limits.

Requirements & scale estimation

A notification system accepts trigger events from upstream services (a new message, an order shipped, a price drop, a marketing blast) and delivers the right message to the right user over the right channel. The interesting engineering is almost entirely in the non-functional requirements.

Functional

  • Multiple channels: push (APNs/FCM) SMS email in-app webhook.
  • Two traffic shapes: transactional (1:1, latency-sensitive — OTP, password reset) and bulk/marketing (1:N fan-out, throughput-sensitive).
  • Templating with localization and variable substitution.
  • User preferences and opt-out, honored per channel and per category.
  • Delivery and read-state tracking, plus analytics.

Non-functional

  • Reliability: at-least-once delivery with no duplicate sends — sending an OTP twice or a bill reminder five times is a reputational and cost problem.
  • Scalability: elastic fan-out; a single marketing campaign may target 50M users.
  • Low latency for transactional: p99 < a few seconds end-to-end; bulk can tolerate minutes.
  • Provider isolation: a degraded SMS vendor must not stall push or email.
Key insightThe single most important framing move: separate the transactional path (low-latency, small fan-out, strict idempotency) from the bulk path (high-throughput, batched, rate-shaped). They share the provider layer but have different SLOs, different queues, and different backpressure behavior. Conflating them is the most common junior mistake.

Back-of-envelope

Assume 100M DAU, average 10 notifications/user/day → 1B notifications/day ≈ 11.6K/s average, with peaks of 5–10× (say 100K/s) during campaign bursts or breaking-news fan-out. If 20% carry a ~1 KB payload for tracking and the rest are references, the metadata store ingests roughly 1B rows/day — squarely a partitioned NoSQL or time-series workload, not a single relational table. See SQL vs. NoSQL and Sharding.

High-level architecture

The backbone is an event-driven pipeline. Producers never call providers directly; they emit an intent, and the platform owns the messy delivery semantics. This is a textbook application of Event-Driven Architecture built on durable logs (Kafka) and worker queues (Queues).

Producer services
      |  emit NotificationRequest {userId|segment, templateId, payload, category, priority, dedupeKey}
      v
[ Ingestion API / Gateway ]  --auth, validation, schema, idempotency check
      |
      v
[ Notification Service ]  --hydrate recipients, check preferences, render template, pick channels
      |
      +--> Kafka topic: notifications.high   (transactional)
      +--> Kafka topic: notifications.bulk    (marketing, lower priority)
      |
      v
[ Channel Workers ]   push-worker | sms-worker | email-worker | inapp-worker
      |   (rate-limited, batched, retry-aware)
      v
[ Provider Adapters ]  APNs | FCM | Twilio | SES | internal WebSocket gateway
      |
      v
[ Delivery callbacks / receipts ]  --> Tracking store + analytics + retry/DLQ feedback

Key decomposition decisions:

  • The Notification Service does recipient hydration, preference filtering, deduplication, and template rendering — the CPU-light but fan-out-heavy work — before anything hits a per-channel queue. By the time a message lands on notifications.high it is a fully-resolved, single-recipient, single-channel unit of work.
  • Channel workers are independent consumer groups. Each scales on its own lag, owns its own retry policy, and is the natural place to enforce per-provider rate limits. A Twilio outage backs up only the SMS worker's lag; push keeps flowing.
  • Separate priority topics (or a priority-aware queue) prevent a 50M-user marketing campaign from starving an OTP. Cheaper than a single queue with reordering logic.

Fan-out on write vs read & the celebrity problem

When a single event targets many recipients (a sports score to 20M fans, a creator posting to 30M followers), you face the same fan-out dilemma as Designing Twitter and Facebook’s Newsfeed.

StrategyHow it worksGood forCost
Fan-out on write (push)At event time, expand the audience into N per-recipient messages and enqueue each.Most notifications — recipients expect timely delivery.Write amplification; a 20M fan-out is 20M queue messages.
Fan-out on read (pull)Store the event once; recipients materialize their feed of pending in-app notifications on open.In-app inboxes for very-high-fan-out, low-open-rate content.Read-time cost; useless for push/SMS which must be pushed.
PitfallThe celebrity / thundering-herd problem: naively expanding a 30M-follower fan-out inline blocks the producer for minutes and floods queues in one burst. Async, chunked expansion is the fix — the ingestion API records the intent and returns immediately; a dedicated fan-out service reads the audience in pages (e.g. 10K recipients per chunk), enqueues each chunk as a sub-job, and applies a token-bucket so the burst is smeared over time. This also makes the job resumable: store a cursor so a worker crash mid-fan-out resumes rather than re-sends from the start.

For physical push (APNs/FCM/SMS), fan-out on write is effectively mandatory — you cannot "pull" a push notification. Fan-out on read only applies to the in-app inbox channel, where you can store one event and let clients query it. A strong design often uses a hybrid: push a lightweight "you have updates" signal eagerly, but materialize the rich in-app list on read.

Provider abstraction & third-party integration

Every channel terminates at a third party you do not control: APNs (Apple), FCM (Android/web), Twilio/MessageBird (SMS), SES/SendGrid (email). They differ in auth (APNs JWT/p8 tokens vs FCM OAuth), payload limits (APNs 4KB), rate limits, error taxonomies, and async vs sync receipts. Hide all of it behind a uniform adapter interface.

interface ProviderAdapter {
    SendResult send(RenderedMessage msg);   // maps to provider-specific call
    // classifies provider errors into a normalized enum:
    //   PERMANENT  (invalid token, unsubscribed, bad number)  -> drop + mark device dead
    //   TRANSIENT  (429, 5xx, timeout)                        -> retry with backoff
    //   THROTTLED  (provider rate limit)                       -> slow down, requeue
}
  • Normalize errors. The retry logic must never see provider-specific codes. An APNs 410 Unregistered and an FCM NotRegistered both map to PERMANENT — delete the device token, never retry.
  • Multi-provider failover. For SMS/email, keep two vendors behind the adapter. If the primary trips a circuit breaker (error rate over threshold), shift traffic to the secondary. This also enables least-cost routing for SMS by country.
  • Async receipts. Most providers report final delivery via webhook/callback, not the send response. The send call only confirms acceptance. Reconcile the true delivered/bounced state from the receipt stream into the tracking store.
  • Connection management. APNs uses long-lived HTTP/2 connections; pool and reuse them — churning TLS handshakes per message destroys throughput.

Reliability: retries, dead-letter, idempotency

The contract is at-least-once delivery, exactly-once effect. Queues give you at-least-once for free (a worker can crash after sending but before ack, causing redelivery). The hard half is suppressing the duplicate visible send.

Idempotency

Every notification carries a deterministic idempotency / dedupe key — ideally supplied by the producer (e.g. order-4821-shipped), otherwise derived as hash(userId, templateId, contentHash, timeWindow). Before dispatching, the worker does an atomic check-and-set against a fast store:

// SETNX with TTL in Redis (or a conditional insert in a dedup table)
key = "notif:dedupe:" + dedupeKey
if (redis.set(key, channel, NX, EX=86400)) {
    dispatch(msg);          // first time we've seen this key -> send
} else {
    metrics.inc("dedupe.suppressed");   // already sent (or in flight) -> drop
}
PitfallThe check-and-send is not truly atomic with the external provider call — you can SETNX, then crash before the provider accepts, leaving a key set for a message never sent. Mitigate by writing the dedupe key only after the provider returns acceptance, and accepting that the window between SETNX-on-attempt and confirmation is the residual duplicate risk. For high-value sends, store dispatch state (ATTEMPTED → SENT) rather than a boolean, and let retries re-send only ATTEMPTED entries. There is no perfect exactly-once across a system boundary you don't control — you minimize, not eliminate.

Retries & dead-letter

  • Exponential backoff with jitter for TRANSIENT/THROTTLED errors, capped at a max attempt count. Don't retry PERMANENT errors — that just burns quota and annoys users.
  • Use a delay queue / retry topic rather than blocking the worker (e.g. publish to notifications.retry.5m consumed after a delay). Tail-latency-friendly and keeps the main consumer draining.
  • After N failed attempts, route to a dead-letter queue (DLQ) with full context. The DLQ is monitored, alertable, and replayable — a poison message or a vendor outage shows up as DLQ growth, not silent loss. See Designing a Distributed Message Queue.

Rate limiting & prioritization

Two distinct rate-limiting concerns, both built on the algorithms from Designing an API Rate Limiter:

LimitWhyMechanism
Per-providerTwilio/APNs enforce hard QPS; exceeding it gets you 429'd or banned.Distributed token bucket in Redis, keyed per provider (and per region/sender ID). Workers acquire a token before sending.
Per-user / frequency capDon't send a user 40 pushes in an hour — they'll uninstall or report spam, hurting sender reputation.Sliding-window counter per (userId, category); drop or coalesce when over cap.

Prioritization: transactional messages must preempt bulk. Implement with separate topics/consumer groups (high vs bulk), giving the high path more workers and reserved provider token budget. Within bulk, a marketing campaign can be deliberately paced — spread 50M sends over 30 minutes — both to respect provider limits and to avoid a self-inflicted traffic spike on your own downstream (e.g. a deep-link landing page).

Trade-offWhen the per-provider token bucket is exhausted, do you drop, delay, or shed by priority? For SMS OTPs you delay briefly and fail fast (a 30s-late OTP is useless — better to error so the user retries). For marketing you delay freely. For breaking news you may drop the tail rather than deliver hours late. The right answer is per-category policy, not a single global setting — surface this explicitly in the design.

Preferences, templating & opt-out at scale

Preferences & opt-out

Users control notifications along several axes: per channel (no SMS, push OK), per category (security alerts always, marketing off), and quiet hours by timezone. Plus legal opt-out (CAN-SPAM unsubscribe, TCPA for SMS, GDPR consent) which is non-negotiable.

  • Store preferences in a read-optimized store keyed by userId; cache hot entries (Caching). The preference check sits on the fan-out hot path, so it must be a single fast lookup, not a join.
  • Evaluate preferences during hydration in the Notification Service, before enqueueing — never generate work that will be discarded downstream.
  • Maintain a global suppression list (hard bounces, unsubscribes, complaint feedback loops from ISPs). A hard bounce or spam complaint must immediately suppress that address — ISP reputation systems punish repeat sends to dead addresses.

Templating

  • Versioned templates with variable interpolation and localization (i18n). Render server-side in the Notification Service so workers handle opaque, finished payloads.
  • Separate content (template + variables) from presentation per channel — the same "order shipped" event renders to a 160-char SMS, a rich push, and an HTML email from one logical template family.
  • Validate at template-publish time, not send time, so a broken template fails for the author rather than silently corrupting a million messages.

Delivery tracking & read state

The lifecycle of a notification is a small state machine: CREATED → QUEUED → SENT → DELIVERED → OPENED/READ (or FAILED/BOUNCED). State transitions arrive from two sources: the send path and async provider receipts.

  • Persist events to a high-write-throughput store (Cassandra/DynamoDB partitioned by userId or notificationId, or a time-series log). At 1B/day this is append-heavy — an LSM-tree store fits far better than a B-tree relational table.
  • Drive analytics (delivery rate, open rate, per-provider failure rate) from a stream pipeline rather than querying the operational store — see Stream Processing & CDC.
  • For in-app read state, store unread counts per user and update on read. This is the channel where fan-out-on-read shines: keep an inbox per user and a badge count, materialized lazily.
  • Real-time in-app delivery rides a persistent connection — WebSockets/SSE through a connection gateway, the same primitive used in Facebook Messenger.
What strong engineers doThey treat delivery receipts as a first-class, eventually-consistent feedback stream — reconciling provider webhooks back into state, feeding bounce/complaint data into the suppression list, and closing the loop so a dead token gets pruned automatically. They don't assume "send returned 200" means "user saw it."

Principal-level mastery check

What strong engineers doThey split transactional and bulk paths early, push all preference/dedup work upstream of the per-channel queues, hide every provider behind a normalized adapter with circuit-breaking and failover, and are explicit that "exactly-once" across a third-party boundary is a best-effort property — they design for at-least-once with aggressive dedup, not a fantasy of perfect delivery. They quantify: queue depth, provider QPS budgets, fan-out chunk sizes, retry caps.

Probing questions to ask engineers:

  • "A creator with 30M followers posts. Walk me through exactly what happens from the API call to the first push landing — and what happens if a fan-out worker crashes halfway."
  • "Twilio starts returning 429 for 20% of requests. What does your system do in the next 60 seconds, and what does the user experience?"
  • "Your worker sends an OTP, then crashes before acking the queue. The message is redelivered. How do you guarantee the user doesn't get two OTPs — and where exactly is the residual duplicate window?"
  • "How do you stop a runaway marketing campaign from delaying password-reset emails by 20 minutes?"
  • "A user reports they're still getting marketing SMS after opting out. Where in the pipeline could the opt-out have been missed, and how do you make that check authoritative?"
  • "You're at 100K notifications/sec peak. Which component breaks first, and what's your scaling lever — and which part of this is genuinely a strong-consistency problem versus eventually-consistent?"
