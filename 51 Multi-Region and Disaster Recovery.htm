Multi-Region and Disaster Recovery

Surviving the loss of an entire region — and proving you can — without quietly buying yourself a split-brain or a six-figure replication bill.

The bottleneck Partial failure & latency. surviving a whole region, paid in a consistency/latency tax and a cost multiplier.

Why a region disappears

A region is a cluster of physically separate data centers (availability zones) close enough to share single-digit-millisecond links. Multi-AZ redundancy — the subject of Redundancy and Replication — protects you from a rack, a switch, or a whole building burning down. It does not protect you from the events that take a region offline as a unit:

  • Correlated infrastructure failure: a fiber cut, a power-grid event, or a cooling failure that hits multiple AZs at once.
  • Control-plane outages: the region's API, IAM, or DNS layer fails so you cannot even launch capacity — the most common real-world “region down” scenario.
  • Bad deploys and poisoned data: a config push or schema migration that replicates instantly to every AZ. Note this is a logical disaster — a hot standby that faithfully replicated the corruption is no help.
  • Regional capacity exhaustion during a large-scale event affecting many tenants simultaneously.
Key insightMulti-region is not “more redundancy.” It is a categorically different problem because the failure unit (the region) now has wide-area latency — 60–150 ms round-trip between continents — which makes synchronous replication across regions a non-starter for most write paths. Everything downstream is a consequence of that latency.

RPO and RTO drive everything

Before drawing any boxes, a principal pins down two numbers per workload. They are the contract that dictates topology and cost.

  • RPO (Recovery Point Objective): how much data you can afford to lose, measured in time. RPO = 0 means not a single committed write may be lost. RPO = 5 min means losing the last five minutes of writes is acceptable.
  • RTO (Recovery Time Objective): how long the service may be down before it must be serving again.
WorkloadRealistic RPORealistic RTOImplied architecture
Payment ledger0< 1 minSynchronous quorum or active-active with conflict-free design
User-facing SaaS appseconds–1 min1–5 minWarm standby, async replication
Analytics / reportinghourshoursPilot light or backup-restore

Trade-offRPO and RTO are bought with money and complexity, and the cost curve is steep near zero. Going from RPO = minutes (async replica) to RPO = 0 (synchronous quorum) often means accepting cross-region write latency on every transaction — see PACELC below. The principal's job is to push back on a blanket “everything needs RPO 0” and tie each target to actual business loss.

Active-passive vs active-active

Active-passive (failover)

One region serves all traffic; a second region holds a replica that is idle (or serves only reads). On disaster you promote the standby and redirect traffic. Simpler to reason about — there is exactly one writer, so no cross-region conflict resolution — but you pay for capacity you rarely use, and the standby is exercised only during the rare, high-stress moment you can least afford surprises.

Active-active

Both (or all) regions serve live traffic concurrently. Capacity is utilized, latency is low because users hit the nearest region, and a region loss removes only its share of traffic. The cost is brutal: with more than one writer you must solve cross-region write conflicts. Your options are (a) partition the keyspace so each region owns a disjoint set of write keys (sticky routing / cell-based, no real conflicts), (b) a globally-consistent store that runs consensus across regions (Spanner-class, paying the latency — see Consensus and Raft), or (c) CRDTs / last-writer-wins for genuinely commutative data.

Pitfall“Active-active” with naive bidirectional async replication on a normal RDBMS is a trap. Two regions accept conflicting writes to the same row, replication carries both, and you silently corrupt data or deadlock. Active-active demands a conflict strategy by design, not bolted on. If you cannot name yours, you have active-passive with extra steps.

Cross-region replication and the consistency tax

The unavoidable physics: a packet between, say, us-east and eu-west takes ~70–90 ms one way. Synchronous replication across that link means every write waits a full round trip for the remote ack — 150+ ms added to every commit. Asynchronous replication acks locally and ships the change in the background, keeping writes fast but creating replication lag — the window of data you lose if the primary dies mid-flight. That window is your RPO.

This is exactly PACELC stated operationally: Partition → choose Availability or Consistency; Else (normal operation) → choose Latency or Consistency. Cross-region replication forces the “E” clause into your face on the steady-state path, not just during failures.

ModeWrite latencyRPO on region lossConflict risk
Sync (quorum across regions)High (+WAN RTT)0None (single agreed order)
Semi-sync (1 remote ack)Medium~0 if remote survivesLow
Async (log shipping)Low= replication lagMedium (on failover)

Patterns that get used in practice (covered in depth in Replication Deep Dive): a single global write leader with regional read replicas (low write availability, simple); leaderless quorum tuned with R + W > N spanning regions; or change-data-capture streaming (Stream Processing and CDC) onto a durable log like Kafka with MirrorMaker replicating topics region-to-region.

Key insightDecouple replication of the durable log from replication of materialized state. If your source of truth is an append-only log replicated cross-region, every regional store is a deterministic projection you can rebuild. This converts “restore the database” into “replay from the last offset,” which is far more testable and bounds RPO to log-replication lag.

Global routing: GeoDNS and anycast

Surviving a region is useless if traffic keeps arriving at the dead one. Two mechanisms steer it:

  • GeoDNS / latency-based DNS (Route 53, NS1, Cloudflare): the DNS resolver returns a region-specific IP based on the client's location or measured latency, with health checks that withdraw a region's records when it fails. Cheap and simple, but bounded by DNS TTL and client/resolver caching — a 60 s TTL still leaves stragglers for minutes, so DNS failover RTO is rarely better than a few minutes.
  • Anycast: the same IP is announced via BGP from multiple regions; the network routes each client to the topologically nearest one. Failover is near-instant (BGP withdrawal) and there is no TTL problem, but you need control of the IP space / a provider that offers it, and in-flight TCP connections still break on reroute. This is how global load balancers and CDNs front their edges.

In a real stack these compose: anycast (or a global LB) at the edge for fast steering, GeoDNS for coarse region selection, and regional load balancers behind that. Health-check thresholds matter more than people expect — too sensitive and a transient blip triggers a needless, risky regional failover; too lax and you bleed errors for minutes.

The four DR strategies

The canonical spectrum, ordered by decreasing RTO/RPO and increasing cost. Map each workload's targets to the cheapest tier that meets them.

StrategyWhat's running in DR regionTypical RTOTypical RPOCost
Backup & restoreNothing; backups in cross-region object storageHoursHours (last backup)$
Pilot lightCore data replicating; everything else off, infra as code ready to scale upTens of minMinutes$$
Warm standbyScaled-down full stack, live but smallMinutesSeconds–min$$$
Hot / multi-site (active-active)Full capacity serving live trafficNear-zeroNear-zero$$$$

A mature organization rarely picks one tier for everything. The payment ledger is hot/multi-site; the user app is warm standby; the data warehouse is pilot light; internal tooling is backup-restore. Tiering by RPO/RTO is how you keep the DR bill sane while still being able to honor your SLAs.

PitfallBackups are not DR until you have restored from them, cross-region, on a clean account, and measured how long it took. Untested backups have a habit of being unrestorable: missing encryption keys, IAM roles that only exist in the primary region, or a restore that takes 9 hours when your RTO is 1. The restore path is the deliverable, not the backup.

Failover orchestration and split-brain

Failover is where most DR plans die. The hard part is not promoting the standby — it is deciding whether to, automatically, under uncertainty, without creating two primaries.

The split-brain problem

A network partition between regions looks identical to a dead primary from the standby's point of view. If the standby promotes itself while the original primary is actually alive but unreachable, you now have two writers diverging. When the partition heals you have irreconcilable history. This is the single most dangerous failure mode in multi-region.

Defenses, in order of robustness:

  1. External arbiter / quorum witness: place a tiebreaker in a third region. Promotion requires majority agreement, so a partitioned minority cannot promote itself. This is the consensus answer — see Leader Election and Coordination and Distributed Locking Service.
  2. Fencing / STONITH: before the new primary accepts writes, the old one must be conclusively isolated (revoke its DB credentials, withdraw its routes, fence its storage). A fencing token (monotonic epoch) attached to every write lets storage reject a stale, deposed leader.
  3. Human-gated failover for the highest-stakes systems: automation gathers evidence and stages everything, a human pulls the trigger. Slower RTO, but eliminates the “flapping health check triggered a 3 a.m. unnecessary failover” class of self-inflicted outages.
def maybe_failover(primary_health, witness):
    # Never promote on local opinion alone.
    if primary_health.is_unreachable():
        if not witness.confirms_primary_down():   # 3rd-region arbiter
            return STAY_PASSIVE        # likely a partition, not a death
        if not fence(old_primary):     # revoke creds, withdraw routes
            return STAY_PASSIVE        # cannot guarantee single writer
        epoch = bump_epoch()           # monotonic fencing token
        promote_standby(epoch)
        repoint_traffic()              # DNS/anycast/global LB
        return PROMOTED
    return STAY_PASSIVE
Key insightFailback is the forgotten second half. After the original region recovers it is now behind and may contain writes that never replicated. You cannot just flip traffic back. You need a controlled resync (often a full reseed) and a re-validated data reconciliation before the old primary rejoins. Plan failback at design time or you will improvise it during an incident.

Data residency and sovereignty

Geography is no longer just a latency knob; it is a legal constraint. GDPR (EU), and analogous laws in India, China, Brazil, and others, restrict where personal data may be stored and processed. This collides head-on with naive multi-region replication: replicating EU customer PII into a US region for DR may itself be the violation.

  • Data partitioning by residency: shard such that an EU user's data lives only in EU regions, with its own intra-EU DR pairing (e.g. eu-west ↔ eu-central). The DR topology must respect the residency boundary — you get a separate DR domain per jurisdiction.
  • Right to erasure complicates backups: a user requesting deletion must be erased from backups and replicas too, which is hard with immutable backup snapshots. Crypto-shredding (per-user keys you can destroy) is a common pattern.
  • Control vs data plane: often you can run a global control plane while keeping the data plane pinned per region — a cell-based architecture aligns naturally with this.

Testing DR: game days

A DR plan that has never been exercised is a hypothesis, not a capability. The discipline is game days — scheduled exercises that actually trigger the failure.

  • Regional evacuation drills: deliberately fail a region out of rotation (block its traffic, fence it) during business hours and confirm the others absorb the load within RTO. Netflix's Chaos Kong popularized killing an entire region on purpose.
  • Restore drills: restore production backups into an isolated account on a cadence and time it against RPO/RTO. Automate it — a green check on a dashboard is the only proof that matters.
  • Failback drills: the half everyone skips. Rehearse the resync-and-return path.
  • Dependency mapping: game days surface hidden coupling — the “independent” DR region that secretly calls a service still living only in the primary.

Tie the outcomes into your observability and error budget practice: a failed game day spends budget on hardening before it spends it on an outage. Also revisit Resilience Patterns (bulkheads, circuit breakers) — multi-region only helps if a region's failure does not cascade through shared dependencies before you can evacuate it.

PitfallBeware the shared global dependency that quietly defeats your whole design: one DNS provider, one IAM control plane, one shared message bus, one global config service. If a single component spans all regions and it fails, your beautiful multi-region topology fails as one unit. Map every global singleton and ask “what is its blast radius?”

Principal-level mastery check

What strong engineers doThey start from RPO/RTO numbers tied to business impact and let those derive the topology — never reaching for “active-active everywhere” reflexively. They immediately name the cross-region latency tax and which write paths can or cannot pay it. They proactively raise split-brain and propose a third-region arbiter plus fencing rather than waiting to be prompted. They distinguish physical disasters (region loss) from logical ones (bad deploys, data corruption) and note that replication does not protect against the latter. And they treat failback and DR testing as first-class deliverables, not afterthoughts.
  • Your standby promotes itself during a network partition while the primary is actually alive. Walk me through exactly how your design prevents the resulting split-brain — and how it reconciles state when the partition heals.
  • Marketing pushes a config that corrupts a key table; it replicates to every region in 200 ms. Your hot standby is now corrupted too. What in your architecture saves you, and what is your real RPO for logical disasters?
  • You're told payments need RPO 0 and RTO under a minute across continents. Quantify the steady-state write-latency cost of synchronous cross-region commit, and propose an alternative that meets the intent without it.
  • GDPR says EU user data cannot leave the EU. How does that constraint reshape your DR topology, your backups, and your handling of a right-to-erasure request?
  • What is the difference between failover RTO with GeoDNS versus anycast, and what specifically determines each? When would you still gate failover behind a human?
  • You've had clean multi-region for two years and never failed over for real. How confident are you it works, and what concrete evidence would you produce to a board after an audit?
