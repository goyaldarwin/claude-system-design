Redundancy and Replication

Replication keeps copies of your data on multiple machines — for availability, read scaling, and durability. The price is that copies drift apart, and the moment a leader dies you face the hardest question in the field: who is allowed to accept writes? Get it wrong and a network blip silently corrupts your data.

The trade-off Availability, read scale, and durability in exchange for staleness, conflicts, and the risk of split-brain. Extra copies let you survive node loss, serve reads from many machines, and avoid data loss. In return copies lag, concurrent writes conflict, and a partition during failover can leave two leaders corrupting the dataset.

What replication is

Redundancy is keeping spare copies of a component so a failure isn't fatal; replication is keeping copies of the data on multiple machines, kept in sync. It buys three things: availability (a replica takes over when one dies), read scaling (fan reads across replicas), and durability (the data survives a single machine's loss).

The hard part is that the copies don't stay identical. Three questions decide everything: who can accept writes, how conflicting writes reconcile, and what happens the instant a leader dies — and those determine your durability, consistency model, and whether a partition corrupts your data.

The one-line frameRedundancy never removes the single point of failure — it moves it (from the node, to the failover mechanism, to the quorum). The whole game is pushing that point until the remaining failure probability is acceptable — and never letting two nodes think they're both the leader.

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Scenario 1 — the leader crashes with writes in flight Your leader acknowledges writes to clients immediately and ships them to a follower asynchronously. It's lagging the follower by 800 ms of writes when it suddenly dies. What happens to those 800 ms of "successful" writes, and what's your real data-loss window?
Scenario 2 — the user can't see their own post A user updates their profile (write goes to the leader), then immediately reloads (read goes to a lagging replica). The page shows the old profile. Why, and how do you guarantee they see their own writes without making every read hit the leader?
Scenario 3 — the partition heals into chaos A 43-second network blip makes your failover system think the leader died, so it promotes a follower. But the old leader was only partitioned, not dead — and kept taking writes. The network heals. What state is your data in now, and what should have prevented it?

Scenario 1 is the async data-loss window. Scenario 2 is replication lag / read-your-writes. Scenario 3 is split-brain — the lethal one.

Replication is defined by four decisions

Decision 1 — Who is allowed to accept writes? (topology)

Where writes are accepted dictates the conflict-resolution burden you inherit:

TopologyWrite pathConflictsExamples
Single-leaderAll writes to one leader → followersNone by construction (total order at the leader)Postgres/MySQL primary-replica, MongoDB replica set, Kafka partition
Multi-leaderWrites at 2+ leaders (often per-region)Concurrent writes to one key must be mergedActive-active MySQL, CouchDB
LeaderlessClient writes to many replicas; quorumSurfaced via version vectors / read-repairDynamo, Cassandra, Riak

Single-leader is the default for a reason: one ordering point makes read-after-write tractable. The moment you accept writes in more than one place, you have concurrency — which is undefined order — and must pick a deterministic merge rule.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — write conflicts Adopt multi-leader or leaderless without a real merge strategy and two concurrent writes to the same key collide. Last-write-wins (LWW) is the seductive default and it's lossy — it silently discards one of two genuine updates and depends on clock skew you don't control. The principled fixes: version vectors to detect concurrency precisely, or CRDTs (OR-Set, PN-Counter) whose merges are commutative/associative/idempotent so order stops mattering. Only take on multi-master when you truly need it — the conflict tax is real.

Decision 2 — Synchronous or asynchronous? (the durability dial)

The single most consequential replication setting — durability vs latency:

  • Synchronous — don't ack the client until a replica has durably persisted the write. Zero data loss on leader failure, but every write pays a cross-node round trip (cross-AZ ~1–2 ms, cross-region tens of ms), and one slow replica stalls all writes.
  • Asynchronous — ack immediately, ship changes in the background. Lowest latency, best availability — but a leader crash loses every in-flight write.
  • Semi-synchronous — ack after at least one replica confirms (rest async). The pragmatic middle (MySQL rpl_semi_sync, Postgres ANY 1 (...)): bound loss to "a replica that also fails" without paying for all-replica acks.
The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the async data-loss window This is Scenario 1. Under async replication, replication lag is your RPO. If the leader is 800 ms of writes ahead of followers and dies, those 800 ms of client-acknowledged writes are gone — the client believes they committed. This is exactly how many MySQL async failovers lose data. State your RPO in terms of worst-case observed lag, not average — and use semi-sync (or sync for ledgers) when the data can't be lost.

Decision 3 — How do reads cope with replication lag?

Async followers are always somewhere in the past. Rather than force expensive global linearizability, mature systems use cheaper per-client guarantees:

  • Read-your-writes — a user sees their own write. Route their reads to the leader briefly, or read only from a replica caught up past the write's log position (LSN/GTID), or pin by sticky session.
  • Monotonic reads — never see time go backwards (read v5, then v3 from a more-lagged replica). Pin each user to one replica via consistent hashing.
  • Consistent-prefix reads — causally related writes seen in order (never the answer before the question).
The failure that happens when you get this wrong — stale reads This is Scenario 2. Send a just-written user's next read to a lagging replica and they see the old value — "I posted but it's not there." Forcing all reads to the leader fixes it but throws away read scaling. The discipline: classify reads by staleness tolerance — route "did my write land" reads to the leader or a caught-up replica (by LSN), and route feeds/analytics/search to replicas. (PACELC frames the latency you trade even with no partition — see CAP.)

Decision 4 — What happens when the leader dies? (failover)

Failover sounds simple — promote a follower — but each step hides a failure mode: (1) detect the leader is dead (a timeout, which can't tell "crashed" from "slow/partitioned"), (2) choose the most up-to-date follower, (3) reconfigure clients and the old leader.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — split-brain This is Scenario 3, and it's the lethal one. If the old leader was only partitioned, not dead, you now have two nodes both accepting writes — the dataset diverges, and on reunion you have unmergeable conflicting histories.
Real incident — GitHub, 21–22 October 2018. A routine network maintenance caused a 43-second connectivity loss between US East Coast and West Coast data centers. The automation (Orchestrator) failed over the database to the West Coast — but the East Coast cluster had also taken writes during the blip. The two sides had diverged, and re-establishing a single consistent history (without losing customer data) took over 24 hours of degraded service. A 43-second blip became a day-long incident — the textbook cost of split-brain.
Defenses are non-optional:
  • Quorum / majority — a node may lead only if it sees a majority; a minority partition can't elect itself. (Why Raft/Paxos use odd counts — see Leader Election.)
  • Witness / tiebreaker — a voting-only node in a third AZ so two-DC setups keep an odd vote count.
  • Fencing (STONITH / fencing tokens) — forcibly stop the old leader, or issue a monotonically increasing token that storage rejects if stale, so a zombie leader's writes are refused.
Timeout tuning is a genuine trade-off: too short flaps on GC pauses (needless failovers, each risking lost async writes); too long extends downtime.

The decision table — pick by need

NeedTopologySync modeFailover guard
OLTP, strong-ish, simpleSingle-leaderSemi-syncQuorum + witness, fencing
Financial ledger (no loss)Single-leaderSynchronousQuorum, fencing tokens
Multi-region writesMulti-leader / leaderlessAsync + merge (CRDT)Per-key conflict resolution
Read-heavy scalingSingle-leader + read replicasAsyncRoute by staleness tolerance
How the bytes move, and the read-scaling ceilingPhysical/WAL shipping replicates the storage log byte-for-byte (cheap/exact, but identical engine version only); logical/row-based replicates decoded change events (enables cross-version upgrades, partial replication, CDC to Kafka). Avoid statement-based (non-deterministic NOW()/RAND() diverge replicas). And note the ceiling: every read replica still applies 100% of the write volume, so replicas relieve read load but never write load — past that point you must shard, not just replicate.

Self-check — can you reconstruct the three scenarios?

  • Scenario 1 (data loss): Why is async replication lag your RPO, and how do sync/semi-sync change the data-loss window?
  • Scenario 2 (stale read): Why does a just-written user see old data on a replica, and how do read-your-writes / monotonic reads fix it cheaply?
  • Scenario 3 (split-brain): How does a brief partition during failover create two leaders, what did GitHub's 43-second blip cost, and which three defenses prevent it?
  • Single-leader vs multi-leader vs leaderless — who handles write conflicts, and why is LWW dangerous?
  • Physical vs logical replication — when do you need logical?
  • Why do read replicas never reduce write load, and what do you do at that ceiling?

The shape to remember: replication is 4 decisions — who writes (topology), sync vs async, reads under lag, failover — with failures: conflicts, async data loss, stale reads, split-brain.

Famous implementations

PostgreSQL / MySQL (single-leader)

  • Topology. Primary takes writes; replicas stream the WAL/binlog. Sync, async, or semi-sync configurable.
  • Key idea. One ordering point = simple consistency; read replicas scale reads.
  • The failure to watch. Async failover loses in-flight writes; needs an external orchestrator + fencing to avoid split-brain (see GitHub 2018).

Cassandra (leaderless)

  • Topology. Every replica accepts reads/writes; quorum (R + W > N) for consistency.
  • Key idea. No leader = no single point to fail and no failover step; tunable per-query consistency.
  • The failure to watch. Concurrent writes conflict; LWW can lose data, so model for it or use it only where loss is OK.

Spanner (synchronous, global)

  • Topology. Paxos groups per shard; synchronous quorum commit across regions.
  • Key idea. Strong consistency everywhere via TrueTime commit-wait — the rare system that's PC/EC.
  • The failure to watch. You pay cross-region commit latency on every write — don't buy it unless you need global strong consistency.

Build it yourself

Design a safe failover

Your leader stops responding. Walk the sequence that promotes a new leader without losing data or creating two leaders. What prevents split-brain?

Reveal the approach

Detect via timeout, but require a majority quorum to act — a node promotes itself only if it can see >half the cluster, so a minority partition (including a partitioned old leader) can't elect anyone. Choose the most up-to-date follower (highest log position) to minimize loss. Fence the old leader before the new one serves writes: power-fence it, or hand the new leader a higher fencing token that storage checks — any write from the old leader carrying a stale token is rejected. Reconfigure clients to the new leader. The two non-negotiables: quorum (no minority leader) and fencing (no zombie leader).

Implement read-your-writes

A user posts, then reads. Guarantee they see their own post without sending every read to the leader.

Reveal the approach

On write, capture the leader's log position (LSN/GTID) and hand it back to the client (cookie/session). On the next read, either route to the leader for a short window after a write, or pick a replica and check it has applied up to that LSN — if not, wait briefly or fall back to the leader. Alternatively pin the user to one replica via consistent hashing (also gives monotonic reads). The cheap version — "read from leader for N seconds after a write" — covers most user-facing cases without sacrificing replica read-scaling for everyone else.

Design-it-yourself

  • Multi-region active-active. Accept writes in two regions and reconcile. Which data can use CRDTs, which must stay single-leader? → CRDTs, Multi-Region & DR.
  • Classify every read in an app. Take a product; route each read to leader vs replica by staleness tolerance. → CAP.
