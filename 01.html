The Computer, the Network, and the Bottlenecks They Impose

The first-principles foundation of the entire course: why this whole body of knowledge exists, derived from two physical machines and the unavoidable bottlenecks they impose. Every later lesson is a technique for relieving one of these bottlenecks.

It all reduces to one computer

Strip away every diagram, every brand name, every framework, and a system is a set of physical computers talking to each other over wires. Each box you ever draw — a web server, a database, a cache, a queue, a load balancer — is a real computer sitting in a rack somewhere, with a CPU that executes a bounded number of instructions per second, a memory hierarchy (registers → cache → RAM → SSD → disk, each tier roughly 10–100× slower and cheaper than the one above it), a finite amount of storage, and a network interface. And like any physical object, it can fail — a disk dies, a power supply pops, a kernel panics — at any moment, without warning.

That is the whole substrate. If you understand the limits of one such machine and the wire between two of them, you can derive almost everything else in this course from first principles rather than memorizing it. So let us do exactly that.

The wall: a single computer is finite and mortal

For decades the answer to "I need more" was simply "buy a bigger computer" — vertical scaling. It works until it doesn't, and it always stops working, for two independent reasons:

  • Finite capacity. There is a biggest CPU, a maximum amount of RAM, a fastest disk that money can buy — and the cost of getting there grows super-linearly. Meanwhile demand — data volume, request rate — can grow without bound. At some scale, no single machine is big enough or fast enough, at any price.
  • Mortality. A single computer has a non-zero probability of failing. One machine means one point of failure: when it dies, your system is down. No amount of money makes a single component perfectly reliable.
The pivotThese two facts — not big enough and not reliable enough — are the entire reason system design exists. If one computer could be made arbitrarily fast, arbitrarily large, and immortal, this field would evaporate. You would just buy the box. System design is what you are forced to do because that box does not exist.

The only escape is many computers — which creates a deeper problem

Since you cannot make one machine big enough or reliable enough, you are forced into the only alternative: use many machines — horizontal scaling. Spread the load across many computers so their capacity adds up; keep copies on several computers so the system survives any one of them dying.

This solves the wall. But the instant work spans more than one machine, the binding constraint changes. It is no longer the CPU, the RAM, or the disk of any single box. It becomes the network between the boxes — and the network is a far crueler master than any local resource. This is the hinge of the entire field, so it is worth stating plainly:

The central tradeYou adopt many computers to escape the limits of one. In doing so you trade a capacity-and-reliability problem you cannot solve for a coordination-over-an-unreliable-network problem you can only manage. Every technique in this course is a tactic in that managed war.

The network is the real bottleneck

Two facts about the network are not engineering choices you can optimize away — they are physics:

1. The speed of light is a hard latency floor

Information cannot travel faster than light: ~300,000 km/s, and slower through fibre. New York to London is ~5,500 km, so a round trip is at minimum ~37 ms before a single line of your code runs. No CDN, no clever cache, no faster CPU changes that floor. This is why reading from local RAM (~100 ns) and calling a service on another continent (~tens of milliseconds) differ by five to six orders of magnitude. Distance is latency, and latency is physics.

2. The network is unreliable and has finite bandwidth

Messages are dropped, delayed, duplicated, and reordered. Links saturate. A request you send may never arrive, or its reply may never come back, and you cannot tell which. Bandwidth between machines is finite and often the scarcest resource of all (cross-region data transfer is both slow and, on a cloud bill, expensive).

ConsequenceOnce your system is distributed, "just call the other service" is never free and never guaranteed. Latency and partial failure are not edge cases — they are the default condition of every line that crosses a machine boundary.

The deepest constraint: no shared truth

There is one more constraint, and it is the one engineers most often miss because it is not about hardware at all — it is almost logical. It is also not a separate root: it falls out of the two above. Because state is physically separated and the network is unreliable:

  • There is no global clock. Two machines cannot agree on what time "now" is to the precision needed to order events. Each has its own drifting clock.
  • You cannot distinguish "slow" from "dead." If a machine stops responding, you have no way to know whether it crashed, whether the network ate your message, or whether it is just busy and the reply is still coming. They look identical from the outside.

From these two innocent-sounding facts flows the hardest part of the entire field: getting independent machines to agree on anything — who is the leader, what order writes happened in, whether a transaction committed. This is the root beneath consensus (Raft, Paxos), beneath the CAP theorem and PACELC, beneath logical and vector clocks, beneath the famous FLP impossibility result. It is not additional to "the computer and the network" — it is their theorem.

So, is anything more fundamental than the Computer and the Network?No. There are three forces: the Computer (finite, mortal), the Network (slow, unreliable), and the Workload (the external, growing, bursty demand that pushes you into both). The Computer and Network are the constraints; the Workload is the force. The famous difficulty of agreement is derived from physically separated state on an unreliable network — a consequence, not a peer. Everything in this course sits on top of these.

The numbers that dictate every decision

The abstract argument becomes concrete the moment you internalize the relative costs of operations. The ratios here are what force every design decision in the field — commit them to instinct:

OperationApprox. timeRelative to L1
L1 cache reference~1 ns1×
Main memory (RAM) reference~100 ns~100×
SSD random read~16 µs~16,000×
Datacenter network round trip~500 µs~500,000×
Disk (HDD) seek~2–10 ms~10,000,000×
Same-region network round trip~1–5 ms—
Cross-continent network round trip~100–150 ms~150,000,000×

Read this table as a hierarchy of cost. The entire discipline can be summarized as: keep work as far up this table as possible; when you cannot, hide the cost or tolerate it. That single sentence generates the techniques:

  • Caching = move data up the hierarchy, closer to the CPU. (04)
  • Replication / CDN = put a copy physically closer to the reader, or somewhere that survives a failure. (09, 65)
  • Sharding = the data will not fit in fast memory on one box, so split it across many. (05)
  • Queues / async = stop waiting on the slow network round trip; do it later. (08)
  • Consensus = agree on truth despite no shared clock, paying a majority round trip per decision. (30)

The six bottlenecks — and the map of this course

Here is the unifying idea. Each of the three forces above imposes one or more bottlenecks — unavoidable costs you must pay to build something that appears more capable and more reliable than any single computer. Every technique in this course is a method of relieving one bottleneck — and crucially, each technique incurs a cost of its own. Once you see the bottlenecks, the entire field organizes itself:

This is not a metaphor imposed on the material — it is the material's actual structure. Every single lesson in this course addresses one of these six bottlenecks. To prove it to yourself, look at the small The bottleneck line at the top of every other lesson: it names which bottleneck that technique addresses and what relieving it costs you in return.

The one principle: there are no free lunches, only trade-offs

If you remember one sentence from this entire course, make it this: you can never escape a bottleneck for free — you can only choose which cost to pay to relieve it. Every technique that relieves one bottleneck incurs a cost somewhere else. This is the principal engineer's core instinct, and it is why "it depends" is the honest answer to almost every design question.

  • A cache pays down latency — and bills you in staleness and invalidation complexity.
  • Replication pays down partial failure — and bills you in consistency (the copies can disagree) and cost.
  • Sharding pays down finite capacity — and bills you in lost joins, harder transactions, and hot-shard skew.
  • Strong consistency pays down "no shared truth" — and bills you in latency and reduced availability under partition (this is literally what PACELC formalizes).
  • A saga pays down the blocking of distributed transactions — and bills you in lost atomicity, forcing you to write compensations.
What strong engineers doThey never present a technique as a win. They name the bottleneck it relieves and the cost it incurs, then argue that for this workload the swap is worth it. "We shard by user ID, which costs us cross-user joins — acceptable here because our access pattern is single-user." That sentence — technique, bottleneck relieved, cost incurred, justified by workload — is the whole job.

How to use this course

  1. Hold this lesson in your head as you read every other one. When you meet a technique, first ask: which bottleneck is this relieving, and what does it cost in return? The The bottleneck line at the top of each lesson answers it in one line; the lesson body is the detail.
  2. Build from the bottom. Read the fundamentals (02–13) and the framework (14) first — they are the building blocks every later design composes.
  3. Attempt before you read. For the design problems (15–29, 53–62, 66, 71), sketch your own design first, then read — especially the "Principal-level deep dive," which surfaces the bottlenecks the naive design ignores.
  4. Go deep where it matters. The distributed-systems theory (30–36) and data-intensive lessons (37–42) are where the "no shared truth" bottleneck is confronted in full. They are the difference between assembling components and understanding them.
  5. Self-test. Use the Principal-level mastery check at the end of each lesson, and the Self-Assessment, to confirm you can name the bottlenecks rather than just recognize the technique.

Master the two machines and the bottlenecks they impose, and the seventy lessons that follow stop being a list to memorize and become a single idea, applied over and over.
