Choosing a Message Broker

How to pick between queues and logs — SQS, RabbitMQ, Kafka, Kinesis, Pulsar — by ordering, replay, throughput, and delivery needs.

The core dichotomy: queue vs log

Almost every "which message broker?" decision collapses to one question first: do you want a queue or a log? They look similar — producers write messages, consumers read them — but their storage model and consumption semantics are fundamentally different, and that difference dictates what they are good at.

A queue treats a message as a unit of work that is consumed and destroyed. The broker holds the message, hands it to a consumer, waits for an acknowledgement, and then deletes it. Each message is delivered to one consumer in a group; the broker tracks per-message state (in-flight, acked, redelivered). This is the model of SQS and RabbitMQ. It gives you per-message acknowledgement, dead-letter handling, and trivially elastic competing consumers — but there is no replay: once acked, the message is gone.

A log treats a message as an immutable, ordered event appended to a file and retained for a configured window (or forever). Consumers are not handed messages; they pull from a position and track their own offset. Multiple independent consumer groups read the same data at their own pace, and any of them can rewind and replay. This is the model of Kafka, Kinesis, and Pulsar. The broker is "dumb" (it just stores and serves bytes by offset); the intelligence lives in the consumer. See Queues, Kafka & Log Messaging, and Designing a Distributed Message Queue for the internals.

Mental modelA queue answers "who does this work?" A log answers "what happened, in what order, and who needs to know?" When you find yourself wanting both per-message retry semantics and replay across many consumers, that tension is exactly what Pulsar and Kafka-with-DLQ patterns try to resolve.

Decision dimensions that actually matter

Once you know whether you lean queue or log, the choice between specific brokers is driven by a handful of dimensions. A principal engineer names the two or three that dominate their workload rather than reciting all of them.

  • Ordering guarantees. None / best-effort, per-key (partition), or strict global? Global ordering across a whole topic is rare and expensive — it forces a single partition and caps throughput. Almost always you want per-key ordering (same user, same account) and partition by that key.
  • Replay & retention. Do downstream consumers need to reprocess history — rebuild a search index, backfill a new service, recover from a bad deploy? If yes, you need a log. If a message is a one-shot command, retention is wasted cost.
  • Throughput. Thousands/sec is comfortable for any broker. Hundreds of thousands to millions/sec per topic pushes you to partitioned logs (Kafka, Pulsar) where parallelism scales with partition count.
  • Delivery semantics. At-least-once is the default everywhere and demands idempotent consumers. Exactly-once exists (Kafka transactions, idempotent producer) but only end-to-end within the Kafka ecosystem and at a latency/complexity cost. Treat "exactly-once" claims skeptically.
  • Partitioning & parallelism. In logs, the partition is the unit of both ordering and parallelism — consumer concurrency is capped at the partition count. In queues, you scale consumers freely but lose ordering.
  • Fan-out model. One message to one worker (competing consumers) vs one event to many independent subscribers (pub/sub). Logs make multi-subscriber fan-out free; queues need routing or duplicate queues.
  • Latency. RabbitMQ and Redis Streams offer sub-millisecond hops; Kafka favors throughput and sits in the low single-digit ms; SQS standard adds tens of ms and is variable.
  • Operational cost. Managed (SQS, Kinesis) means no brokers to babysit but per-request/shard pricing and vendor lock-in. Self-hosted Kafka/RabbitMQ means clusters, rebalancing, and on-call — real headcount cost.
  • Ecosystem. Kafka Connect, Streams, ksqlDB, Schema Registry; RabbitMQ plugins; cloud-native triggers (Lambda from SQS/Kinesis). The surrounding tooling often decides the pick more than the core broker. See Event-Driven Architecture.

Broker by broker: sweet spots & when to avoid

Amazon SQS — the managed default queue

Fully managed, effectively infinitely scalable, pay-per-request. Standard queues give at-least-once delivery with no ordering and possible duplicates; FIFO queues give exactly-once processing and strict ordering within a message group, capped around 3,000 msg/s with batching. Visibility timeout + DLQ handle retries cleanly.

Sweet spot: decoupling AWS services, background jobs, task buffering, smoothing spiky load — when you want zero operational burden and don't need replay or high ordered throughput.

Avoid when: you need to replay history, fan a stream out to many independent consumers, or push millions/sec through one ordered stream.

RabbitMQ — the flexible smart broker

A mature AMQP broker with rich routing: exchanges (direct, topic, fanout, headers) decide which queues a message lands in. It is a "smart broker, dumb consumer" design — routing logic lives in the broker. Excellent per-message control, priorities, TTLs, and low latency.

Sweet spot: complex routing topologies, RPC-style request/reply, work queues where you want fine-grained acks and per-message priorities, and moderate throughput (tens of thousands/sec).

Avoid when: you need event replay, very high sustained throughput, or long retention — RabbitMQ is a queue, not a log, and deep queues degrade performance.

Apache Kafka — the high-throughput log

The de facto standard distributed commit log. Topics split into partitions replicated across brokers; ordering is guaranteed per partition; retention is time- or size-based (or compacted to keep the latest value per key). Scales to millions of messages/sec and petabytes retained. Rich ecosystem: Connect, Streams, Schema Registry, exactly-once via transactions.

Sweet spot: event streaming, the central event backbone/log, CDC (see Stream Processing & CDC), metrics/log pipelines, and any case needing replay plus high throughput plus many consumer groups.

Avoid when: you have a handful of messages/sec, need complex per-message routing, or lack the operational maturity to run a cluster (or budget a managed offering like MSK/Confluent).

Amazon Kinesis — managed Kafka-like on AWS

A managed log with the same shard/partition + offset model. Capacity is provisioned in shards (1 MB/s in, 2 MB/s out each); ordering is per shard; retention up to 365 days. Tight integration with Lambda, Firehose, and Analytics.

Sweet spot: AWS-native streaming where you want log semantics and replay without operating Kafka — clickstreams, IoT telemetry, real-time analytics feeding S3/Redshift.

Avoid when: you need Kafka's ecosystem/portability, very large fan-out (read throughput per shard is limited without enhanced fan-out), or you object to shard-based provisioning and AWS lock-in.

Apache Pulsar — segmented storage & multi-tenancy

A log that separates serving (brokers) from storage (BookKeeper), so you scale them independently and rebalance without copying data. Natively supports both queue and streaming subscription modes (exclusive, shared, failover, key-shared), tiered storage to object stores, and first-class multi-tenancy with namespaces.

Sweet spot: platform/multi-tenant environments needing both queue and log semantics in one system, geo-replication, and independent compute/storage scaling.

Avoid when: you want the largest community/ecosystem and operational know-how (Kafka still wins there), or the extra moving part (BookKeeper) outweighs the flexibility for your scale.

Redis Streams — the lightweight log

An append-only log data type inside Redis with consumer groups and offset tracking. Sub-millisecond latency, trivial to run if you already operate Redis. Durability is bounded by Redis persistence and memory.

Sweet spot: low-latency, modest-volume streaming where Redis is already in the stack — real-time notifications, ephemeral event fan-out, simple job streams.

Avoid when: you need durable long-term retention, very large datasets that exceed memory economics, or strong cross-broker replication guarantees.

Trade-offThe recurring tension is broker-managed per-message state (queues: easy retries, easy elastic consumers, no replay) versus consumer-managed offsets (logs: replay and free fan-out, but you own idempotency, DLQ, and partition-bounded parallelism). Picking the wrong side means either bolting replay onto a queue or hand-rolling per-message retry on a log — both are painful. Choose the model that matches your dominant access pattern.

A decision guide in prose

Walk it top-down, stopping at the first constraint that binds:

  1. Do you need replay or multiple independent consumers of the same data? If yes → a log (Kafka / Kinesis / Pulsar / Redis Streams). If a message is a one-shot command consumed once and discarded → a queue (SQS / RabbitMQ).
  2. Do you need strict ordering per key? Logs give it per partition for free — partition by your ordering key. With queues, use SQS FIFO message groups or RabbitMQ single-consumer-per-key; accept the throughput cap that ordering imposes.
  3. What is the throughput target? Up to ~tens of thousands/sec → any broker works; pick on ergonomics. Hundreds of thousands to millions/sec → a partitioned log (Kafka or Pulsar), and your parallelism is bounded by partition count, so size partitions for peak consumer concurrency.
  4. Managed or self-hosted? On AWS with no platform team → SQS for queues, Kinesis (or MSK) for logs. Willing to operate or running multi-cloud → RabbitMQ for routing-heavy queues, Kafka for the event backbone, Pulsar when you need both models and multi-tenancy.
  5. Any special needs? Complex routing → RabbitMQ. Sub-ms latency on an existing Redis stack → Redis Streams. Independent storage/compute scaling and geo-replication → Pulsar. A rich streaming ecosystem (Connect, Streams, schema governance) → Kafka.

A common, defensible end state at scale: Kafka as the durable event backbone (replay, fan-out, CDC, analytics) with SQS/RabbitMQ at the edges for command-style work queues that need per-message retry and elastic workers. Don't force one broker to do both jobs.

Principal-level mastery check

What strong engineers doThey start by classifying the workload as queue-shaped or log-shaped before naming a product, and they justify the choice from the dominant dimension (replay, ordering, throughput) rather than personal familiarity. They are explicit that "exactly-once" almost always means "at-least-once + idempotent consumer," and they bound parallelism by partition count out loud.

Probing questions to ask engineers:

  • "You picked Kafka. A new service needs to backfill from six months ago — walk me through what changes versus if you'd chosen SQS."
  • "Your consumer needs strict per-user ordering but also 500k msg/s. How do partitioning and consumer concurrency interact here?"
  • "You said 'exactly-once.' Define precisely where the boundary is and what happens on a consumer crash mid-batch."
  • "When would you deliberately choose RabbitMQ over Kafka, and when is that choice a mistake?"
  • "This is a one-shot 'send welcome email' command. Why might a log be the wrong tool, and what does it cost you if you use one anyway?"
  • "How would you handle a poison message in Kafka versus in SQS, and which gives cleaner semantics?"
