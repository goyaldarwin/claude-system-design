Designing a Payment System

Move money correctly — charge a card, credit a merchant, never lose or duplicate a cent — across your services and external processors that can time out at the worst moment. Correctness, not throughput, is the whole game: a double charge or a lost payment is a real-world, regulator-visible failure. The two pillars are idempotency (a retry must never charge twice) and a double-entry ledger (an immutable, always-balanced record of every money movement).

The crux Correct, auditable, exactly-once money movement — in exchange for idempotency everywhere, a strongly-consistent ledger, and reconciliation against external processors you don't control. Get money movement provably right and auditable. You pay by making every operation idempotent (retries are inevitable and must be safe), keeping an immutable double-entry ledger as the source of truth, and reconciling with banks/processors whose async, sometimes-failing responses you must absorb.

1. Requirements

Functional: charge a customer (card / wallet), credit a merchant, support refunds and reversals; record every money movement; integrate with external payment service providers / PSPs (Stripe, Adyen, card networks, banks); produce an auditable history.

Non-functional: correctness above all — no double charge, no lost payment (exactly-once effect); strong consistency for balances (no eventual-consistency on money); durable & auditable (immutable record, regulatory compliance); secure (PCI, see Security); available, but correctness wins ties — better to reject a payment than to process it wrong.

2. Capacity estimate

Say 10,000 payments/sec at peak, each touching multiple ledger entries, retained 7+ years (regulatory). Work it:

  • Throughput: 10k payments/sec is modest by web standards — this is not a throughput problem. Consequence: optimize for correctness and consistency, not raw QPS; a strongly-consistent SQL database easily handles this.
  • Ledger writes: each payment writes ≥2 ledger entries (debit + credit), often more (fees, taxes) — say ~5 entries → ~50k ledger writes/sec, all of which must be atomic and balanced. Consequence: the ledger needs ACID transactions; you can't tolerate a partial write that leaves money unaccounted.
  • Storage & audit: billions of immutable entries over 7 years — large but cheap; the entries are append-only and never updated (corrections are new compensating entries). Consequence: design the ledger as immutable, which also gives you the audit trail for free.

3. The API

POST /charges   { amount, currency, source, idempotencyKey }  -> { chargeId, status }
POST /refunds   { chargeId, amount, idempotencyKey }          -> { refundId, status }
GET  /charges/{id}                                            -> { status, ledgerRefs }

The idempotency key is mandatory on every mutating call — it is the single most important field in the whole API (Stripe requires it). status is explicit (pending → succeeded/failed) because external processing is asynchronous and an outcome may be momentarily unknown (sub-problem 3).

4. High-level design

A payment service receives the charge, deduplicates on the idempotency key, writes a pending record + ledger entries in a local transaction, then calls the external PSP (Stripe/Adyen/card network) to actually move money. The PSP responds (or via webhook) with success/failure; the payment service updates the status and finalizes ledger entries. A double-entry ledger (its own strongly-consistent store) is the source of truth for all balances. A reconciliation process continuously compares the ledger against the PSP's/bank's settlement reports to catch any discrepancy. The database is the coordination point: commit state locally before the external call so there's always a durable record to recover from.

5. Data model — the double-entry ledger

The heart is a double-entry ledger: every money movement creates at least two entries — a debit from one account and a matching credit to another — and the sum of all entries is always zero. Money is never created or destroyed, only moved between accounts. Entries are immutable (append-only); a correction is a new compensating pair, never an edit.

ConceptMeaning
AccountA balance bucket (customer, merchant, fees, cash, payable)
TransactionA group of entries that must balance to zero, applied atomically
Entry(account, debit/credit, amount, currency) — immutable
Idempotency record(key → result) so a retry returns the original outcome

Example charge: debit "customer cash" $100, credit "merchant payable" $97, credit "platform fees" $3 — sums to zero. Balances are derived by summing an account's entries (or kept as a running materialized balance).

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide first. If you know all three cold, skip to the comparison.

Scenario 1 — Stripe's idempotency (real design) A client sends "charge $50," the network times out before the response arrives, so the client — not knowing if it worked — retries. Stripe (and every serious payment API) guarantees the card is charged exactly once regardless. What mechanism makes the retry safe, and why can't you solve this with "exactly-once delivery"?
Scenario 2 — the balance that doesn't add up A bug debits the customer but a crash happens before crediting the merchant — so $100 left the customer's account but never arrived anywhere. The books don't balance; money has effectively vanished. What data model makes this structurally impossible, and how is it enforced?
Scenario 3 — the PSP timed out — did the charge happen? You call the external processor to charge the card, and the call times out. Maybe the charge went through; maybe it didn't. You cannot tell from the timeout. How do you avoid both double-charging (if it succeeded) and losing the payment (if it failed), when the outcome is genuinely unknown?

Scenario 1 is idempotency. Scenario 2 is the double-entry ledger. Scenario 3 is talking to the external processor. Each maps to a sub-problem.

The four hard sub-problems

The flow is simple; correctness is everything. These four are the hard core — each with a nested failure.

Sub-problem 1 — Idempotency (never charge twice)

In a distributed system, retries are inevitable — timeouts make "did it succeed?" unanswerable, so clients will resend. An operation is idempotent if doing it twice equals doing it once. The mechanism: every mutating request carries a client-generated idempotency key; the payment service records (key → result) the first time and, on any retry with the same key, returns the stored result without re-executing the charge.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the double charge This is Scenario 1, the cardinal payment sin. Without idempotency, a retried "charge $50" charges twice — a real customer loses real money and you owe a refund + trust. You cannot fix this with "exactly-once delivery," which is impossible over a network. The fix is the idempotency key: on first receipt, the service atomically records the key and its result (e.g. a unique constraint on the key in the same DB transaction as the charge); a retry with that key finds the existing record and returns the original result, never re-charging. Handle the in-flight case too (a retry arrives while the first is still processing → return "in progress," don't start a second). Scope keys per account, expire them after a window (Stripe: ~24h–30d). This is exactly-once effect via idempotency — the foundation of the whole system.

Sub-problem 2 — The double-entry ledger

The ledger is the source of truth for money, and its structure enforces correctness: every transaction is a set of entries that must sum to zero (debits = credits), applied atomically (all or nothing). Money only moves between accounts; it's never created or destroyed. Entries are immutable; corrections are new compensating entries.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — money created or destroyed This is Scenario 2. A single-entry model (just "subtract from A," "add to B" as independent updates) lets a crash between them leave the books unbalanced — money vanishes or is conjured, and you may not notice for days. The double-entry design makes this structurally impossible: a transaction's entries must sum to zero and are committed in one atomic ACID transaction — so you can never debit without the matching credit; either both land or neither does. Because entries are immutable and append-only, you also get a complete, tamper-evident audit trail (regulators require it) and can always recompute any balance from history. This is why payment systems use a strongly-consistent (often relational) store for the ledger, not an eventually-consistent one — money demands ACID.

Sub-problem 3 — Talking to the external processor

You don't actually move money — an external PSP / card network / bank does, asynchronously, and its responses can time out, arrive late (webhooks), or be lost. You must handle the case where you called it but don't know the outcome, without double-charging or losing the payment.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the unknown outcome This is Scenario 3. A timeout from the PSP is genuinely ambiguous — the charge may have succeeded or failed. The disciplined pattern: (1) persist intent first — write a pending charge + ledger entries locally before calling the PSP, so a crash leaves a durable record to recover from. (2) Pass your idempotency key to the PSP too (Stripe accepts one) — so when you retry the call after a timeout, the PSP recognizes it and doesn't charge twice (it returns the original result). (3) On unknown outcome, don't guess — query the PSP for the charge's status by your key (or wait for its webhook), and only then finalize as succeeded/failed. (4) Finalize ledger entries based on the confirmed outcome. So even a timed-out call is safe: retry with the same key (no double charge), then reconcile to the true result (no lost payment). Never mark succeeded on a timeout, and never blindly retry without an idempotency key the PSP honors.

Sub-problem 4 — Reconciliation & consistency

Even with idempotency and a ledger, your records and the PSP's/bank's can diverge — a webhook was missed, a dispute/chargeback occurred, a settlement differs by a fee. Reconciliation is the continuous process of comparing your ledger against the processor's settlement reports and flagging/fixing discrepancies. And throughout, balances must be strongly consistent — no reading a stale balance and overspending.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — silent drift vs the bank This is the failure of not reconciling. Distributed systems and external processors mean your view and the bank's view will occasionally disagree (missed webhook, chargeback, timing). Without reconciliation, this drift is silent and compounds — you think you're owed X, the bank settled Y, and you discover it weeks later during an audit (or a regulator does). The fix: a scheduled reconciliation job that ingests the PSP's settlement reports and matches every transaction against your ledger, surfacing mismatches for investigation; missing webhooks are caught by also polling the PSP for status (don't rely on webhooks alone — they get lost). On the consistency side, balance checks (e.g. "do they have enough?") must read a strongly-consistent balance and update it atomically (often with optimistic locking / a transaction), never an eventually-consistent read — or two concurrent spends can overdraw. Money is the canonical case where you choose consistency over availability.

At a glance — the design decisions

ConcernMechanism
No double chargeIdempotency key (your API and passed to the PSP)
Books always balanceDouble-entry ledger, atomic, immutable, sums to zero
Unknown PSP outcomePersist intent first; retry with key; query status; finalize on confirmed result

The rule in one lineMake every operation idempotent (the key is everything), record money in an atomic, immutable, double-entry ledger that can't go unbalanced, treat the external PSP outcome as confirmable-not-assumable (persist intent, retry with key, query status), and reconcile continuously against the bank. Correctness beats throughput and availability — this is the one system where you'd rather fail than guess.

  • Scenario 2 (unbalanced books): How does a double-entry ledger make "money vanishing" structurally impossible, and why does the ledger need ACID?

The takeawayYou almost never build the money-movement layer — a PSP (Stripe/Adyen) handles cards, compliance, and bank connections. What you build and own is the correctness layer: the idempotency keys, the double-entry ledger (in an ACID store — or a purpose-built ledger DB like TigerBeetle), and the reconciliation. Purpose-built ledger databases exist precisely because the double-entry + strong-consistency requirements are demanding enough to warrant specialized engines.

Famous implementations

Same facets — what it is, the mechanism, and the failure to watch.

Stripe / Adyen (PSPs)

  • What it is. Payment processors that move money and expose idempotent APIs.
  • Mechanism. Mandatory idempotency keys, immutable double-entry ledgers internally, webhooks + status APIs for async outcomes; ~99.999% consistency targets.
  • The failure to watch. You must still pass idempotency keys, handle webhook loss (poll too), and reconcile your ledger against their settlement reports.

Ledger databases (TigerBeetle)

  • What it is. A database purpose-built for double-entry accounting at high throughput with strict correctness.
  • Mechanism. Transactions of balanced debit/credit entries applied atomically; designed for the exact ledger invariants money needs.
  • The failure to watch. Specialized — you still design account structure and reconciliation; most teams use Postgres/Aurora unless throughput demands a dedicated engine.

Saga / workflow orchestration

  • What it is. For multi-step payment flows (authorize → capture → payout) across services, a saga with compensations.
  • Mechanism. Orchestrator drives each step idempotently; on failure, runs compensating entries (a refund is a compensating ledger transaction, not a delete).
  • The failure to watch. No isolation between steps — intermediate states are visible; design statuses (pending/authorized/captured) accordingly.

Build it yourself

Work each before opening the approach.

Make a charge endpoint exactly-once

Design POST /charges so a client can safely retry after a timeout and the card is charged at most once — including the in-flight-retry edge case.

Reveal the approach

Require an idempotency key per logical charge. On receipt: in one DB transaction, attempt to insert a row keyed by the idempotency key (a unique constraint) in pending state plus the pending ledger entries. If the insert succeeds (key was new): call the PSP passing the same key, then on confirmed success finalize the row + ledger to succeeded and store the result. If the insert fails (key exists): the request is a retry — if the existing row is succeeded/failed, return its stored result (no re-charge); if it's still pending (a concurrent in-flight retry), return "in progress" / wait, don't start a second charge. Because you persisted intent before calling the PSP and passed the key to the PSP too, even a crash or PSP timeout is recoverable: a retry re-finds the row and the PSP recognizes the key. Result: exactly-once effect end to end (Scenario 1 + 3).

Model a charge-with-fee as balanced ledger entries

A customer pays $100; the merchant gets $97 and your platform keeps a $3 fee. Write the ledger transaction and explain why it can't leave money unaccounted.

Reveal the approach

One atomic transaction with entries that sum to zero: DEBIT customer_cash $100, CREDIT merchant_payable $97, CREDIT platform_fee_revenue $3 (−100 + 97 + 3 = 0). All three are written in a single ACID transaction — so it's impossible to debit the customer without also crediting the merchant and fee accounts (Scenario 2 can't happen). To refund, you don't edit these immutable entries — you append a new balanced transaction reversing them (DEBIT merchant_payable $97, DEBIT platform_fee_revenue $3, CREDIT customer_cash $100). Every account's balance is the sum of its entries; the grand total across all accounts is always zero (money is conserved). The immutability gives the audit trail, and the sum-to-zero invariant is checkable — a transaction that doesn't balance is rejected before commit.

Design-it-yourself: the open-ended ones

  • Handle chargebacks & disputes. A customer disputes a charge weeks later. Design the ledger movements and state transitions, and how reconciliation catches it. → Sagas.
  • Multi-currency. Support payments and balances in many currencies with FX. Design how the ledger represents currency and conversion without rounding losses. → Consistency.
  • Reconcile against a PSP. Design the daily reconciliation that ingests settlement files, matches them to your ledger, and surfaces discrepancies. → Job Scheduler, Batch.
