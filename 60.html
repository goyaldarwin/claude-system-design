Designing a Distributed Job Scheduler

A managed cron / workflow service that fires millions of one-time and recurring jobs on time, survives worker crashes, and never silently drops a run.

The bottleneck No shared truth & partial failure. reliable scheduled execution, paid in leader election, leases, and fencing tokens.

Requirements & scope

The problem: "design a distributed job scheduler." That phrase covers everything from a glorified cron to Airflow to AWS EventBridge Scheduler / Temporal. Your first job is to scope it. State the functional surface explicitly:

  • One-time jobs ("run task X at 2026-07-01T03:00Z") and recurring jobs (cron expression or fixed interval).
  • Submit / cancel / pause / update a job; query status and run history.
  • Execution: the scheduler does not run business logic itself — it triggers a target (HTTP webhook, enqueue to a queue, invoke a function, run a container).
  • Delivery semantics: at-least-once by default, with an idempotency contract; optional exactly-once effects.
  • DAG dependencies and priorities as an extension.

Non-functional requirements are where the interesting design lives:

  • Scale: 100M+ registered jobs, 10k–100k fires/sec at peak.
  • Timing accuracy: a stated SLO, e.g. p99 fire within 1–2 s of scheduled time. "On time" is a distribution, not a guarantee — nail this number down.
  • Durability: an accepted job is never lost across crashes, deploys, or AZ failure.
  • No double-fire under normal operation, and bounded duplicates under failure.
  • Multi-tenant fairness: one tenant's 50M jobs cannot starve everyone else.
PitfallIt is tempting to jump straight to "a priority queue keyed by next-run-time." That's the easy 10%. The hard 90% is what happens when a worker grabbing the job dies mid-flight, when 4M jobs are all scheduled for midnight UTC, and when the leader that owns dispatch is partitioned away but still thinks it's the leader.

Back-of-the-envelope

Anchor the design in numbers. Suppose 100M recurring jobs averaging one fire per hour — that's ~28k fires/sec average. Peaks concentrate on round timestamps (top of the minute/hour), so a naive design typically sees 10–100× that load in a 1 s window, and pathological clustering can push it far higher. If 60% of 100M jobs use a cron like 0 * * * *, ~60M fires land in the same second every hour — thousands of times the average. That spike, not the average, sizes your system.

API & data model

POST /jobs
{
  "name": "nightly-report",
  "schedule": { "type": "cron", "expr": "0 3 * * *", "tz": "America/New_York" },
  "target":   { "type": "http", "url": "https://svc/run", "method": "POST", "body": {...} },
  "policy":   { "retries": 5, "backoff": "exponential", "timeout_s": 300,
                "misfire": "fire_once", "overlap": "skip" },
  "idempotency_key_template": "report-{{run_id}}",
  "priority": 5
}
POST   /jobs/{id}/pause | /resume | /cancel
GET    /jobs/{id}/runs?from=...&to=...

Two tables matter most. Jobs holds the durable definition. Runs (a.k.a. executions) is the per-fire record — this is the source of truth for "did run R for job J at time T happen?" and is what makes the system idempotent and auditable.

jobs(  job_id PK, tenant_id, schedule, target, policy,
       next_run_at, state[ACTIVE|PAUSED], version, owner_shard )

runs(  run_id PK,            -- deterministic: hash(job_id, scheduled_time)
       job_id, scheduled_at, state[PENDING|LEASED|RUNNING|SUCCEEDED|FAILED|LOST],
       lease_owner, lease_expires_at, fence_token, attempt, last_error )
Key insightMake run_id a deterministic function of (job_id, scheduled_time), not a random UUID. Then "insert this run" becomes an idempotent upsert: two dispatchers that both decide job J should fire at T produce the same run_id and the second insert is a no-op (unique-constraint conflict). This single decision collapses a whole class of double-fire bugs at the data layer. For ID strategy see Designing a Unique ID Generator.

High-level architecture

Separate the three concerns that people conflate: storing jobs, deciding what should fire now, and executing the trigger.

  • API / control plane — validates and persists job definitions, computes the first next_run_at, fronted by load balancers. Stateless.
  • Durable store — sharded SQL (Postgres/CockroachDB) or a wide-column store, partitioned by time bucket. This is the system of record. See Sharding and SQL vs NoSQL.
  • Dispatcher / scheduler tier — scans for due runs, materializes runs rows, and pushes them onto an internal queue. This tier needs coordination so partitions don't double-scan.
  • Execution queue — a distributed message queue (SQS, Kafka, or a Redis-backed queue) decouples "decide" from "do" and absorbs bursts.
  • Worker pool — pulls leased runs, invokes targets, applies retries/timeouts, writes terminal state back to runs.

The decouple-via-queue step is the key architectural move: the scheduler tier only has to be fast enough to enqueue, and the queue smooths the midnight spike into whatever the worker pool can drain. This is classic event-driven back-pressure.

Time-bucketing & the hot-shard problem

You will not SELECT ... WHERE next_run_at <= now() across 100M rows every second — that's a full hot-index scan. Instead bucket time. Partition the due-index by a coarse time unit (e.g. one bucket per second or per minute), so a dispatcher only reads the handful of buckets that are due. A hierarchical timing wheel (the Kafka / Linux-kernel approach) is the in-memory version: O(1) insert and tick, with overflow wheels for far-future jobs. For durability the wheel is a cache in front of the persistent bucket index, rebuilt on restart.

The classic failure: hot buckets. Cron expressions cluster on round numbers — 0 0 * * * (midnight), 0 * * * * (top of hour), */5. Millions of jobs land in the same second-bucket, and whichever shard owns it gets crushed.

MitigationHow
Jitter on materializationSpread fires across a window: actual = scheduled + hash(job_id) % jitter_window. Only valid if the SLO allows it — "approximately 3am" jobs, yes; financial cutoffs, no.
Shard by job_id, not by timeThe due-index partition key = hash(job_id); each shard independently scans its own slice of the current time bucket. Distributes the midnight spike across all shards.
Pre-materialize earlyGenerate runs rows a few seconds ahead of scheduled time and let the queue hold them, so the fire itself is just a queue dequeue at T.
Smooth the queueThe execution queue + worker autoscaling absorbs the residual burst; timing SLO is measured at enqueue, throughput is bounded by workers.
Trade-offSharding the due-index by hash(job_id) kills the hot-time-bucket problem but means every shard must tick every bucket — more total scan work, more dispatcher nodes. Sharding by time bucket is cheaper to scan but reintroduces hot shards. Most production designs shard by job_id and accept the fan-out, because predictable load beats cheap-but-spiky.

Leader election & the dispatcher

If N dispatcher nodes all scan the same bucket, they'll all try to fire the same jobs. Two safe designs:

Option A — partitioned ownership (preferred at scale)

Assign each due-index shard to exactly one dispatcher via a coordination service (ZooKeeper / etcd / Consul). Use consistent hashing over the shard ring so adding/removing a dispatcher reshuffles only 1/N of shards. When a dispatcher dies, its ephemeral lock expires and its shards are reassigned. This is horizontally scalable — no single leader is a bottleneck. The mechanics of leases, ephemeral nodes, and herd avoidance are covered in Leader Election and Coordination and Designing a Distributed Locking Service.

Option B — single leader

One elected leader (Raft — see Consensus and Raft) owns all scanning and hands work out. Simpler reasoning, but the leader is a throughput ceiling and a single point of latency. Fine for thousands of jobs, not millions.

PitfallLeader election does not by itself prevent double-fire. A leader can be partitioned, fail to receive heartbeats, and a new leader gets elected — while the old leader, not yet aware it lost the lease, fires a job. This is the split-brain window. The defense is not "better election," it's idempotency at the run layer plus fencing tokens at the side-effect layer. Never rely on a lock alone to guarantee single execution.

Exactly-once, leases & fencing

Be precise: exactly-once delivery is impossible over an unreliable network (the Two Generals problem). What you can build is at-least-once delivery + idempotent effects = "effectively-once." Choose at-least-once over at-most-once: a duplicate fire is recoverable if effects are idempotent; a dropped fire of a payroll job is not.

Worker leasing & visibility timeout

A worker doesn't "take" a run, it leases it: atomically set state=LEASED, lease_owner=W, lease_expires_at=now+T, fence_token=monotonic++. The worker must heartbeat to extend the lease while running. If the worker crashes, the lease expires (the queue's visibility timeout), the run reverts to PENDING, and another worker reclaims it. This is exactly the SQS visibility-timeout pattern.

# reclaim loop (runs on dispatchers)
UPDATE runs SET state='PENDING'
WHERE state IN ('LEASED','RUNNING') AND lease_expires_at < now();

# worker claim (atomic, idempotent)
UPDATE runs SET state='LEASED', lease_owner=:w,
               lease_expires_at=now()+:T, fence_token=:tok
WHERE run_id=:r AND state='PENDING';   -- 0 rows affected => someone beat us

Fencing tokens

The lease-expiry reclaim creates a danger: the original worker may have only been slow (GC pause), wake up, and complete its side effect after a second worker already started. Guard the downstream with a monotonically increasing fence token. The target (or a dedup layer) records the highest token it has seen for a given run_id and rejects any write with a lower token. This is the canonical defense Kleppmann describes — a stale lease holder is fenced out at the resource, not at the lock. See the same pattern in Distributed Locking Service.

End-to-end idempotency

Pass the deterministic idempotency_key (derived from run_id) to the target. The target deduplicates on it. For HTTP targets this is a header; for queue targets it's the message dedup key. Combined with deterministic run_id and fence tokens, a duplicate fire becomes a no-op rather than a double payment. See Resilience Patterns for retry/backoff/dead-letter handling and Designing a Payment System for why idempotency keys are non-negotiable on money-moving targets.

Trade-offShorter visibility timeout => faster recovery from dead workers, but more false reclaims of slow-but-alive workers (more duplicates). Longer timeout => fewer duplicates, slower failover (a fire stuck behind a dead worker waits the full timeout). Heartbeat-based lease extension lets you keep the timeout short and tolerate long-running jobs — the right answer for variable job durations.

Backfill & missed runs

The scheduler is down (deploy, outage, partition) from 02:55 to 03:10 and a job was due at 03:00. What now? This is a misfire policy and it must be explicit per job, not a global behavior:

  • Fire once on recovery — coalesce all missed occurrences into a single catch-up fire. Right for "send the daily digest" (you don't want three digests).
  • Fire all (backfill) — replay every missed occurrence in order. Right for batch ETL where each interval is a distinct data partition. See Batch & Big Data Processing.
  • Skip / fire-next-only — drop the missed window entirely; only run the next scheduled occurrence. Right for "publish current price snapshot" where stale data is worthless.

Implementation: because run_id = hash(job_id, scheduled_time), the scheduler can on recovery enumerate every scheduled_time in the gap and decide per-policy which runs to materialize. The deterministic ID means re-running the recovery scan twice is safe — it produces the same run rows. Track last_materialized_time per job so you know the exact gap boundary. Airflow's catchup flag and max_active_runs are this policy made concrete.

PitfallNaive backfill of a job that was paused for a week with a 1-minute cron generates 10,080 runs at once and stampedes the workers. Always cap backfill (max_catchup_runs) and feed it through the same rate-limited queue, not a tight loop. Relate this to API rate limiting.

DAGs, dependencies & priority

Dependency scheduling

Beyond time triggers, jobs depend on other jobs: "run aggregate only after extract and transform both succeed." Model the workflow as a DAG of tasks. A task becomes eligible when all upstream edges are in a satisfied terminal state. Two execution models:

  • Event-driven — on each task completion, emit an event; a coordinator decrements the unmet-dependency count of downstream tasks and enqueues those that hit zero. Scales well; needs durable event handling.
  • Polling / sweep — a scheduler periodically evaluates which tasks are now eligible. Simpler, higher latency. (Airflow's scheduler loop.)

Store the DAG topology and per-run task states durably; a DAG run is itself a row tree so you can resume from the failure point rather than re-running succeeded upstream tasks. Cycle detection at submit time is mandatory.

Priority & multi-tenant fairness

A single FIFO queue lets a low-priority bulk job block a latency-sensitive one. Use priority queues (multiple queues drained weighted, or a score-ordered structure). For multi-tenancy, layer weighted fair queueing per tenant so one tenant's 50M-job backfill can't monopolize workers — cap concurrent runs per tenant and round-robin across tenant queues. This is the same fairness concern as in Queues.

Principal-level mastery check

What strong engineers doThey separate delivery semantics from execution semantics early, and state plainly that exactly-once delivery is impossible — then build at-least-once + idempotent effects + fencing tokens to get effectively-once. They make run_id deterministic so idempotency falls out of a unique constraint. They identify the hot-time-bucket problem unprompted and pick a sharding strategy with eyes open about the fan-out cost. They treat leader election as necessary-but-insufficient and never claim a lock alone prevents double-fire. They tie timing accuracy to a concrete SLO measured at enqueue, and they make misfire/backfill an explicit per-job policy rather than hand-waving "we'll just re-run it."
  • Your SLO is "p99 fire within 1s of schedule." Walk me through every place latency accumulates between the scheduled instant and the target receiving the trigger — and where you'd measure it.
  • A worker leases a run, GC-pauses for 40s, the lease expires, a second worker runs the job, then the first worker wakes and POSTs to the target. Trace exactly what prevents a double side-effect. What if the target has no fencing support?
  • 60% of 100M jobs use 0 * * * *. At 12:00:00 your design must not melt. Show me the load distribution across shards and where the burst is absorbed. What changes if jitter is disallowed by the SLO?
  • The scheduler tier is down for 12 minutes during a deploy. For a 1-minute-cron ETL job, a daily-digest job, and a price-snapshot job — what fires on recovery, and how do you stop a backfill stampede?
  • How do you guarantee a job submitted via the API is durable before you return 200, while keeping submit latency low at 10k submits/sec? Where exactly does the write land and what's your replication/consistency model? (See CAP, PACELC.)
  • How would you support exactly-once effects for a target that moves money but exposes no idempotency key? What do you push back on, and what compensating design (ledger, two-phase, dedup table) would you propose?
