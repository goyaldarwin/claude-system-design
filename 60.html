Designing a Distributed Job Scheduler

Run jobs at a time (cron at 2am), after a delay (send this in 1 hour), or on a recurring schedule — reliably, at scale, across a fleet of workers where any node can crash. The deceptively hard parts: making sure a job runs exactly once (not zero times if a worker dies mid-run, not twice if two workers grab it), and finding "what's due now" efficiently among millions of scheduled jobs.

The crux Reliable time-based and recurring execution across many workers — in exchange for solving exactly-once execution (despite crashes), efficient due-job lookup, and worker coordination. A durable scheduler runs jobs even when workers fail. You pay by handling the at-least-once vs at-most-once dilemma (a crashed job must rerun, but mustn't double-run), indexing jobs by run-time so "what's due" is cheap, and coordinating so two workers don't run the same job.

1. Requirements

Functional: schedule a job to run at a specific time, after a delay, or on a recurring (cron) schedule; cancel/update a scheduled job; jobs are units of work handed to workers; track status (scheduled/running/succeeded/failed).

Non-functional: reliable (a scheduled job will run, even if a worker crashes), exactly-once execution (or at-least-once + idempotent — never silently zero), scalable (millions of scheduled jobs, thousands running concurrently), reasonably timely (run near the scheduled time, not minutes late), no single point of failure.

2. Capacity estimate

Say 100M scheduled jobs outstanding, peak 10,000 jobs/sec becoming due. Work it:

  • Storage: 100M jobs × ~1 KB metadata = ~100 GB — fits in a database, but you can't scan it to find due jobs. Consequence: jobs must be indexed by run-time so "what's due" is a range query, not a scan.
  • Due rate: 10,000 jobs/sec become due at peak. The picker must claim and dispatch them faster than they arrive, or the schedule falls behind. Consequence: dispatch is the throughput-critical path; parallelize pickers, but coordinate so they don't claim the same job.
  • Skew danger: humans schedule things on round numbers — midnight, the top of the hour. So 2am UTC might have a huge spike of jobs all due at once. Consequence: design for thundering-herd spikes at common times, and spread recurring jobs with jitter.

3. The API

schedule({ jobType, payload, runAt | delay | cronExpr, idempotencyKey }) -> jobId
cancel(jobId)
getStatus(jobId) -> { state, lastRunAt, nextRunAt, attempts }

runAt/delay/cronExpr cover the three timing modes; idempotencyKey supports exactly-once at the work level (sub-problem 2). The job payload describes the work; workers know how to execute each jobType.

4. High-level design

Three parts: (1) a durable job store (database) holding all scheduled jobs, indexed by next-run-time. (2) Picker/dispatcher nodes that periodically query "jobs due now," atomically claim a batch (so two pickers don't grab the same job), and push them onto a work queue. (3) Worker pools that pull from the queue, execute the job, and report success/failure; on success, recurring jobs are re-scheduled (compute next run, write it back). A coordination/leader mechanism ensures the picker role is owned without conflict. The queue between pickers and workers decouples discovery from execution and absorbs the due-time spikes.

5. Data model — jobs indexed by run-time

FieldPurpose
jobId, jobType, payloadIdentity & work
nextRunAt (indexed)The key index — "jobs due" is WHERE nextRunAt ≤ now
state, lockedBy, lockExpiryClaim/lease so one worker owns it
cronExpr, attempts, idempotencyKeyRecurrence, retry count, dedup

The index on nextRunAt is the heart: it turns "find due jobs among 100M" into an efficient range scan of just the soon-due ones. For very high scale, jobs are sharded (by time bucket or job id) so pickers parallelize without contending.

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide first. If you know all three cold, skip to the comparison.

Scenario 1 — the worker that crashed mid-job A worker claims "send invoice email," starts running it, and crashes before reporting success. If you re-run it, the customer gets two emails; if you don't, they get none. Which is correct, how do you decide a job is really dead (not just slow), and how do you make the re-run safe?
Scenario 2 — two pickers grabbed the same job Two picker nodes both query "due jobs," both see job X, and both dispatch it — so it runs twice simultaneously on two workers. How do you ensure exactly one picker/worker owns a given job, even with many pickers running in parallel?
Scenario 3 — 2am, and 5 million jobs are all due at once Tons of recurring jobs were scheduled "daily at midnight," so at 00:00 a massive spike of due jobs hits the picker and the downstream systems they call — a self-inflicted thundering herd. Why does this happen, and how do you smooth it?

Scenario 1 is worker failure & recovery (+ exactly-once). Scenario 2 is exactly-once execution / claiming. Scenario 3 is recurring jobs & the herd. Each maps to a sub-problem.

The four hard sub-problems

The pipeline is the easy part. These four are where schedulers are hard — each with a nested failure.

Sub-problem 1 — Finding what's due (the scan problem)

With millions of scheduled jobs, "which are due now?" must be cheap. The answer is the nextRunAt index: a range query WHERE nextRunAt ≤ now AND state = scheduled returns just the due ones. Pickers poll this every few seconds (or use a time-bucketed structure). The index is what makes the scheduler scale.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — scanning everything Without an index on run-time, finding due jobs means scanning all 100M rows every polling interval — crippling, and it gets worse as jobs accumulate. The nextRunAt index turns it into an efficient range read of only the soon-due rows. Two refinements: time-bucketing (group jobs into buckets like "jobs due in minute M" so you query one small bucket) reduces index pressure and contention; and for far-future or huge-volume jobs, a tiered design (a database for the long tail, an in-memory priority queue / timer wheel for the imminent ones) keeps the hot path fast. Polling a flat unindexed table is the classic scheduler scaling failure.

Sub-problem 2 — Exactly-once execution (claiming a job)

Two distinct ways a job runs wrong: it runs twice (two pickers/workers grab it) or zero times (everyone assumes someone else has it). Preventing double-claim needs an atomic claim: a picker claims a job with a conditional update — UPDATE jobs SET lockedBy=me, lockExpiry=now+T WHERE jobId=X AND state=scheduled — which atomically succeeds for exactly one picker (the database serializes it). Or claim via a queue's at-least-once-with-visibility semantics.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — double-run or no-run This is Scenario 2. If pickers read-then-write without atomicity, two can both see "scheduled" and both claim → the job runs twice. The fix is an atomic conditional claim (compare-and-set on state, in one DB statement, or a distributed lock with a fencing token) so exactly one picker wins; losers see the row already claimed and skip it. But "exactly-once execution" across crashes is ultimately impossible to guarantee at the dispatch layer alone — so combine atomic claiming (prevents concurrent double-run) with idempotent jobs (the job uses its idempotencyKey so even if it is dispatched twice across a crash, the effect happens once). Atomic claim + idempotency = exactly-once effect; relying on either alone leaves a gap.

Sub-problem 3 — Worker failure & recovery (leases)

A worker can claim a job and then die — leaving the job "running" forever (orphaned) unless you detect it. The mechanism is a lease: the claim sets lockExpiry = now + T; the worker must renew the lease (heartbeat) while running. If the lease expires without renewal, the job is presumed dead and becomes claimable again (re-dispatched to another worker).

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the orphaned job (and the slow-vs-dead dilemma) This is Scenario 1. Without a lease/timeout, a worker that crashes mid-job leaves it stuck in "running" forever — it never completes and never retries (a silent zero-run). With a lease + heartbeat, an expired lease signals "worker probably dead" and the job is reclaimed. The hard judgment is slow vs dead: set the lease too short and you reclaim (and double-run) jobs from workers that are merely slow (a GC pause); too long and recovery from a real crash is sluggish. Size the lease above your worst expected job duration + pause, require heartbeats for long jobs, and — because reclaim can double-dispatch a slow-but-alive worker's job — make jobs idempotent so the re-run is safe (back to Scenario 1: re-run is fine if the job is idempotent, which is why the customer gets one email, not two). A fencing token ensures the reclaimed worker's write wins over the zombie's.

Sub-problem 4 — Recurring jobs & clock issues

Recurring (cron) jobs must compute their next run after each execution and write it back. And they introduce timing hazards: clock differences between nodes, clock drift/skew, daylight-saving/timezone arithmetic, and the round-number spike where everyone schedules "at midnight."

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — skew, drift & the scheduling herd This is Scenario 3. Several traps: (1) The herd — humans schedule on round times, so 00:00 gets a giant spike of simultaneously-due jobs that overwhelms pickers and the systems those jobs call (a thundering herd). Fix: add jitter — spread recurring jobs over a window (run "daily" jobs at a random minute within the hour, not all at 00:00). (2) Clock skew — if pickers use their own wall clocks and those disagree, a job can run early/late or (with a backward NTP correction) twice. Fix: drive timing from one source of truth (the job store's time, or tightly-synced clocks), and treat the stored nextRunAt as authoritative. (3) Timezone/DST — "9am local daily" across DST changes is genuinely tricky; compute next-run in the user's timezone with a real calendar library, not naive UTC offset arithmetic. The jitter fix is the big one — it turns a 5-million-job spike into a smooth flow.

At a glance — the design decisions

ConcernMechanism
Find due jobs cheaplyIndex on nextRunAt + time-bucketing (+ timer wheel for imminent)
One owner per jobAtomic conditional claim (CAS on state) / distributed lock + fencing
Survive worker crashLease + heartbeat; reclaim on lease expiry

The rule in one lineStore jobs indexed by run-time, have pickers atomically claim due jobs onto a queue, run them on workers under a lease + heartbeat (reclaim on expiry), make jobs idempotent so a reclaim/re-run is safe (exactly-once effect), and jitter recurring jobs to avoid the round-number herd.

The takeawayManaged schedulers (EventBridge Scheduler, Cloud Scheduler, Cloud Tasks) handle the durable store, the due-time lookup, and at-least-once dispatch for you — so you mostly provide idempotent jobs and a target. For complex, long-running, stateful jobs with retries and compensation, workflow engines (Temporal, Step Functions) are the modern answer — they bake in the lease/heartbeat and exactly-once-effect machinery.

Famous implementations

Same facets — what it is, the model, and the failure to watch.

Cron / Quartz

  • What it is. Classic time-based schedulers — Unix cron (single node) and Quartz (clustered, JVM).
  • Model. Quartz clustering uses a database with row locks so only one node fires a given trigger.
  • The failure to watch. Single-node cron is a SPOF; clustered Quartz relies on DB locking that can contend at scale; no built-in idempotency — jobs must be safe to re-run.

DB-backed job queues (Sidekiq / Celery)

  • What it is. Job queues with scheduled/delayed support, backed by Redis or a database.
  • Model. Jobs in a sorted set by run-time; workers pop due jobs atomically; retries with backoff.
  • The failure to watch. At-least-once by default (duplicate runs possible) — jobs must be idempotent; visibility/lease tuning for crash recovery.

Temporal / Cadence

  • What it is. Durable workflow engines that handle scheduling, retries, and exactly-once-effect for long-running jobs.
  • Model. Workflow state is durably persisted and replayed; activities have timeouts/heartbeats and automatic retry; the engine guarantees progress.
  • The failure to watch. More operational surface; you still design activities to be idempotent for the at-least-once activity execution.

Build it yourself

Work each before opening the approach.

Claim a due job exactly once across many pickers

Many picker nodes poll for due jobs. Design the claim so exactly one picker dispatches a given job, even under concurrency, and a crashed picker/worker's job is recovered.

Reveal the approach

Pickers query WHERE nextRunAt ≤ now AND state='scheduled' (using the run-time index). To claim, issue an atomic conditional update: UPDATE jobs SET state='running', lockedBy=:me, lockExpiry=now()+lease WHERE jobId=:id AND state='scheduled' — the database serializes this, so exactly one picker's update affects a row; losers get 0 rows updated and move on (Scenario 2 solved). The claimer sets a lease (lockExpiry) and, for long jobs, the worker heartbeats to extend it. A recovery sweep reclaims jobs whose state='running' but lockExpiry < now (crashed worker, Scenario 1) by resetting them to scheduled. Because a reclaim can re-dispatch a slow-but-alive worker's job, the job itself is idempotent (uses idempotencyKey), and a fencing token (e.g. the lease version) ensures a zombie worker's late write is rejected. Atomic claim + lease + idempotency = exactly-once effect.

Smooth the midnight herd

Millions of "daily" jobs are scheduled at 00:00 and overwhelm the system at midnight. Redesign so the load is smooth without changing what users asked for.

Reveal the approach

Add jitter: when materializing a recurring job's next run, don't use the exact boundary — spread it across a window. For "daily" jobs that don't need a precise minute, assign each a stable random offset within an hour (e.g. hash the jobId into 0–3600s) so the 5M jobs fire smoothly across 00:00–01:00 instead of all at 00:00 (Scenario 3 solved). For jobs that must run at exactly midnight, you can't jitter the trigger — so instead rate-limit/queue the dispatch (pickers pull due jobs at a bounded rate onto the work queue) and ensure downstream systems the jobs call are protected (rate limiting, backpressure). The principle: spread the load at the source (jitter) where semantics allow, and meter it at dispatch where they don't — never let a round-number spike become a self-inflicted thundering herd.

Design-it-yourself: the open-ended ones

  • Tiered timing. Design a two-tier store — a database for far-future jobs, an in-memory timer wheel for imminent ones — and how jobs migrate between tiers. → Indexes.
  • Workflow with dependencies. Jobs that depend on other jobs (a DAG). Design ordering and failure/retry across the graph. → Sagas, Batch.
  • Scheduled notifications. Combine this with a notification system to send "in the user's 9am." Design the timezone handling. → Notification System.
