Microservices & Service Mesh

Splitting one application into many independently-deployable services lets teams ship without stepping on each other and scale hot paths in isolation. But it converts every in-process function call into a network call — and the network can be slow, fail, and lie. Microservices trade local simplicity for organizational scale, and the bill comes due in distributed-systems complexity. Knowing when not to split is as important as knowing how.

The trade-off Independent deployment, scaling, and team autonomy — in exchange for network latency/failure, distributed data, and operational complexity on every call that used to be a function. You gain the ability for many teams to build and ship in parallel and scale services independently. You pay by turning every method call into an unreliable network hop, losing easy transactions, and taking on service discovery, observability, and failure handling everywhere.

What microservices are — and the cost of the network

Microservices decompose an application into small, independently-deployable services, each owning a slice of the business capability and (ideally) its own data, communicating over the network. The opposite is a monolith: one deployable unit where modules call each other in-process. The promised benefits are real — independent deployment (teams ship on their own cadence), independent scaling (scale only the hot service), fault isolation, and technology freedom per service.

But the defining fact, the one everything else follows from: a microservice call is a network call. What was a nanosecond, always-succeeds function invocation becomes a millisecond, sometimes-fails, sometimes-slow, sometimes-lies remote call. Every distributed-systems problem you'd otherwise avoid — partial failure, latency, distributed transactions, consistency — now lives inside your own app.

The one-line frameMicroservices trade distributed-systems complexity for organizational scale. The real driver is team autonomy, not technology — you adopt them when coordination between teams on one codebase costs more than the network/operational tax of splitting. Below that threshold, a monolith is the right answer, and "we should use microservices" is often the wrong default.

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide first. If you know all three cold, skip to the comparison.

Scenario 1 — Amazon Prime Video reverses course, 2023 (real case) Prime Video's video-quality monitoring service was built as distributed microservices — AWS Step Functions orchestrating Lambdas, passing frames through S3. It hit a hard scaling limit at ~5% of the target load, and the orchestration + storage costs were enormous. The team collapsed it back into a single process (one ECS task) and cut costs ~90%. What did the microservice/serverless decomposition cost them here, and what does this say about when to split?
Scenario 2 — the deploy that requires deploying everything You have 12 "microservices," but to ship a feature you must change and deploy 5 of them together in a specific order, and they all read the same shared database tables. A schema change in one breaks three others. What have you actually built, and which two decisions did you get wrong to end up here?
Scenario 3 — one slow service takes down the site Your checkout service calls the recommendations service synchronously to show "you might also like." Recommendations gets slow (GC, overload). Checkout threads block waiting on it; the thread pool fills; checkout stops responding; the failure spreads upstream to the API gateway. A non-critical feature just took down checkout. What is this failure called, and what three patterns would have contained it?

Scenario 1 is whether to split at all. Scenario 2 is boundaries + data ownership. Scenario 3 is sync communication and cascading failure. Each maps to a decision below.

The five decisions that define a microservices design

Designing (or avoiding) microservices is these five choices. Each has a named failure nested under it.

Decision 1 — Should you split at all?

The first and most-skipped decision. Microservices solve an organizational problem — many teams contending on one codebase — at the price of distributed-systems complexity. If you have one or a few teams, a monolith (or a modular monolith: clean module boundaries, one deployable) ships faster and is far easier to operate. Split when team-coordination cost clearly exceeds the network/ops tax, and split incrementally (extract the service that most needs independent scaling or ownership).

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — premature decomposition This is Scenario 1. Splitting before you have the scale or team pressure to justify it buys you all the cost (network hops, orchestration, distributed data, ops) and none of the benefit. Prime Video's monitoring pipeline paid for Step Functions state transitions and S3 round-trips on every frame — costs that vanished when the work ran in one process. The lesson isn't "microservices bad," it's "match the architecture to the actual problem": that team had one tightly-coupled, high-throughput workload, which a monolith serves better. Start monolith, extract services when a real seam appears.

Decision 2 — Where are the service boundaries?

If you split, boundaries should follow business capabilities / bounded contexts (Orders, Payments, Inventory) — cohesive areas that change together — not technical layers or CRUD nouns. Conway's law says your architecture will mirror your org chart, so align service boundaries with team boundaries: one team owns a service end-to-end. A good boundary minimizes the calls and data that must cross it.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the distributed monolith This is half of Scenario 2. Boundaries drawn at the wrong seams (by entity, by layer, or arbitrarily) produce services so chatty and interdependent that you must deploy them together in lockstep — a distributed monolith: all the operational pain of microservices, none of the independence. The tell: you can't deploy one service without others, a change ripples across many, and a single user action fans out to a dozen synchronous calls. Fix by redrawing boundaries around things that change together (merge over-split services), so each can deploy alone.

Decision 3 — Who owns the data?

The rule that makes services truly independent: each service owns its data and no other service touches that database directly — you go through the owning service's API. This lets a service change its schema without coordinating, but it means cross-service queries and transactions are now distributed: you can't JOIN across services or wrap them in one ACID transaction, so you use sagas, the outbox/CDC, and accept eventual consistency.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the shared database This is the other half of Scenario 2. If multiple services read and write the same tables, you've coupled them at the deepest level: one service's schema migration breaks the others, and nobody can evolve independently — the database is the integration point, recreating the monolith's coupling with worse ergonomics. Each service must own its store and expose data through its API (or via events). The cost — no cross-service joins/transactions — is real and is the point: it forces the boundaries to be honest.

Decision 4 — Synchronous or asynchronous communication?

Services talk either synchronously (request/response: REST/gRPC — simple, but the caller waits and is coupled to the callee's availability) or asynchronously (events/messages — the caller fires and forgets, decoupled in time, but you get eventual consistency and harder debugging). The principle: use async/events for cross-service workflows wherever you can tolerate eventual consistency, and reserve synchronous calls for genuine request/response needs — and even then, protect them.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the latency/failure cascade This is Scenario 3. A synchronous call chain couples availability: if A calls B calls C synchronously, A is only as available as B×C, and a slow dependency is worse than a dead one — callers block threads waiting, pools exhaust, and the stall propagates upstream until the whole chain falls over (a non-critical service taking down a critical one). Contain it with: timeouts (never wait forever), circuit breakers (stop calling a failing dependency, fail fast), and bulkheads (isolate thread pools so one slow dependency can't consume all of them) — plus making non-critical calls async/optional. Full treatment in Resilience Patterns.

Decision 5 — Where do cross-cutting concerns live? (the service mesh)

Every service needs the same plumbing: retries, timeouts, load balancing, mutual TLS, tracing, circuit breaking. You can put it in a shared library (linked into each service) or push it into a service mesh — a sidecar proxy (e.g. Envoy) deployed alongside each service that intercepts all traffic and applies these policies, controlled centrally, with zero application code.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — library drift across polyglot services The shared-library approach works until you have services in multiple languages (you must reimplement the library N times) or many versions deployed (a fix to the retry logic requires every team to upgrade and redeploy — library drift, where services run inconsistent, stale networking behavior). A service mesh moves this into the sidecar/network layer: one place to set timeouts, mTLS, and traffic policy, applied uniformly regardless of service language or version. The trade-off is the mesh's own operational complexity and per-call sidecar latency — worth it past a certain scale, overkill below it.

The rule in one lineStart with a (modular) monolith. Adopt microservices when team-coordination cost exceeds the network/ops tax — then split by bounded context, give each service its own data, prefer async communication, protect the sync calls, and use a mesh for cross-cutting concerns once you're polyglot/large.

The takeawayManaged meshes (Anthos/Istio, App Mesh) give you mTLS, retries, traffic shaping, and tracing without per-service code — but they add a control plane and sidecar to operate. Below a handful of services in one language, a shared library or framework features are simpler; the mesh earns its keep when you're polyglot and at scale (Decision 5).

Famous implementations

Patterns made concrete. Same facets — what it is, the mechanism, and the failure to watch.

Istio / Linkerd (service mesh)

  • What it is. A mesh that injects a sidecar proxy (Istio: Envoy; Linkerd: a lightweight Rust proxy) next to each service.
  • Mechanism. Sidecars intercept all traffic; a central control plane configures mTLS, retries, timeouts, traffic splitting (canary), and tracing — uniformly, no app code.
  • The failure to watch. Operational complexity and per-hop sidecar latency/resource overhead; Linkerd trades some features for simplicity/lower overhead vs Istio.

The strangler-fig migration

  • What it is. The standard way to break up a monolith incrementally — not a big-bang rewrite.
  • Mechanism. Put a proxy/router in front of the monolith; extract one capability at a time into a service and route its traffic to the new service, leaving the rest in the monolith until it's gradually "strangled" away.
  • The failure to watch. The migration stalls half-done (you now run both forever); pick high-value seams first and actually finish, or stop deliberately at a stable hybrid.

The modular monolith

  • What it is. One deployable, but with strict internal module boundaries (clear interfaces, separate schemas per module) — microservice discipline without the network.
  • Mechanism. Enforce boundaries in code (module visibility, no cross-module DB access) so you could later extract a module into a service cheaply.
  • The failure to watch. Boundaries erode without enforcement; but for most teams this is the right default and the answer to Scenario 1's lesson.

Build it yourself

These click when you draw the boundaries. Work each before opening the approach.

Draw service boundaries for an e-commerce app

You have Users, Products, Carts, Orders, Payments, Inventory, Shipping, Reviews. Group them into services, decide data ownership, and identify which cross-service calls should be async.

Reveal the approach

Group by bounded context, not entity: e.g. Catalog (Products, Reviews), Ordering (Carts, Orders), Payments, Fulfillment (Inventory, Shipping), Identity (Users). Each owns its data — no shared tables. Synchronous where you need an immediate answer (checkout → Payments authorize). Asynchronous/events for workflows that tolerate eventual consistency: "OrderPlaced" event triggers Fulfillment to reserve inventory and Shipping to schedule, via a saga, not a distributed transaction. The test of a good boundary: a typical feature touches one service, and a service can change its schema without coordinating. If "place order" fans out to 8 synchronous calls across 8 services, you've over-split (distributed monolith) — merge.

Plan a strangler-fig migration off a monolith

Your monolith handles everything; the Payments code is the riskiest and needs independent scaling and a dedicated team. Plan extracting it without a big-bang rewrite.

Reveal the approach

Put a router/proxy in front of the monolith. Build a new Payments service with its own database; migrate/replicate the payment data it needs. Route payment endpoints to the new service while everything else still hits the monolith. Use the outbox/CDC to keep any shared data in sync during the transition, and an anti-corruption layer so the monolith and new service can evolve independently. Cut over incrementally (shadow traffic → small % → full), with the ability to route back on failure. Once Payments is fully served by the service and the monolith no longer touches payment tables, remove the old code. Finish the extraction — a permanently half-migrated system is the worst of both.
