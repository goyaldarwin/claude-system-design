Choosing a Database

The single most expensive-to-reverse decision in most systems. Data has gravity: once terabytes and a hundred services depend on a store's query semantics, migrating off it is a multi-quarter project. So you choose by walking a fixed checklist of dimensions — never by preference.

The trade-off Which store's trade-offs fit this workload — decided by dimensions, in order, not by familiarity. Every store wins its niche by giving up something (usually query generality). The job is to name what you're willing to sacrifice for this workload, and defend it by walking the dimensions — not "we already run Postgres."

Why this choice is hard to reverse

Choosing a database is the most expensive-to-reverse decision in most systems, because data has gravity: once a few terabytes and a hundred services depend on a store's query semantics, migrating off it is a multi-quarter project. A principal engineer treats the choice as a structured trade-off across a fixed set of dimensions, not a preference — and walks every candidate store through the same checklist.

The one-line frameEvery store wins its niche by giving up something — almost always query generality. Relational and NewSQL keep generality but pay in write-scaling effort or latency. There is no free lunch: you are choosing which flexibility to sacrifice, not whether to sacrifice any. Default to relational and justify every move away from it.

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide your answer first — the rest of the lesson is the answer. If you already know all three cold, skip to the worked example.

Scenario 1 — "I'll use MongoDB, I know it" In an architecture review, an engineer proposes a datastore by naming it first: "let's use MongoDB — I know it well." What's the one question that exposes whether this is a real decision or a comfort choice — and what order should the reasoning have gone in?
Scenario 2 — the query you didn't plan for You chose Cassandra "for scale" and modeled your tables around today's queries. Six months in, product asks for a new query that doesn't include the partition key. What happens to that query, and why was this decided the day you picked the store?
Scenario 3 — the fourth datastore Your system already runs Postgres, Redis, and Elasticsearch. A team wants to add a graph database for one feature. What's the real cost of saying yes, and what's the bar that addition must clear?

Scenario 1 is the familiarity trap. Scenario 2 is why access pattern is decided up front (and the partition-key test). Scenario 3 is the cost of polyglot sprawl. Each maps below.

The decision dimensions

Walk every candidate through the same checklist. The dimensions resolve in a rough order — and the order matters as much as the list, because it's what stops you starting from "what we already run."

DimensionWhat to askWhy it dominates
Data model fitRelational, blob-by-id, nested document, wide sparse row, graph, free text, or time series?A mismatch makes you fight the engine forever — joins emulated in app code, or denormalized copies to keep in sync
Access patternRead- or write-heavy? Point lookup, range scan, full-text, or analytical aggregation?Storage engines are optimized for one shape (LSM vs B-Tree)
ConsistencyRead-your-writes, linearizable, or eventual?Strong consistency costs latency and availability under partition (CAP / PACELC)
ScaleRead-scale via replicas, or write-scale via sharding?Read replicas are cheap; write scaling means a partition key on day one
Latencyp99 sub-ms, single-digit ms, or seconds-OK analytics?In-memory beats on-disk beats cross-region quorum by orders of magnitude
DurabilityCan you lose the last second of writes? Sync vs async, fsync policy?Durability and write latency trade directly
TransactionsMulti-row ACID, snapshot isolation, serializability?Multi-key transactions across shards are expensive and not universal
Operational maturityCan your team run it? Managed offering? Backup, upgrade, observability?The best-fit engine you can't operate at 3 a.m. is the wrong choice

First: model fit + access pattern (eliminate most engines)

Data model is the first filter because it's the hardest to work around. Map your dominant entity and its access shape to a family — relational for entities-with-relationships and ad-hoc queries, key-value for O(1) get-by-key, document for self-contained aggregates, wide-column for write-heavy keyed-by-partition, graph for multi-hop traversals, search for free text, time-series for append-only metrics. These two dimensions alone usually eliminate all but a couple of candidates.

Then: consistency + scale (narrow the survivors)

Among the survivors, do you need read-your-writes or linearizability, and how big does throughput get? Strong consistency narrows you toward CP/relational/NewSQL stores; write-scale needs sharding and a partition key chosen up front.

Real migration — Uber, Postgres → MySQL (2016) A famous example of the scale dimension forcing a move — and notably not "Postgres is bad." Uber hit a wall on write amplification: in Postgres's MVCC model, every row update writes a new physical tuple, and every secondary index must be updated to point at it — even for indexes whose columns didn't change. Worse, Postgres replication is physical: it ships every on-disk change, so a small logical update became a large, bandwidth-heavy replication stream across datacenters.
The lesson, in framework terms. At Uber's write volume and multi-datacenter scale, that amplification didn't scale — so they moved to MySQL/InnoDB, which updates rows in place where possible and replicates logically (the compact statement of the change), and built a sharding layer ("Schemaless") on top. The point for you: the move was driven by a specific dimension (write pattern × scale × cross-DC replication), defensible in one sentence — exactly the framework, not a preference. Most systems never hit this wall; a single Postgres primary plus replicas is the right default until a dimension like this proves otherwise. (Source: Uber Engineering blog, 2016.)

Then: latency, durability, transactions (break ties)

p99 target, acceptable data-loss window, and multi-row transaction needs separate the remaining candidates.

Last: operational fit (the final veto — never the first filter)

Can your team actually run it? This is a legitimate veto at the end — but starting here ("we already run Postgres") is the familiarity trap dressed up as pragmatism.

Sequence mattersModel fit + access pattern eliminate; consistency + scale narrow; latency, durability, transactions break ties; operational fit is the final veto. Resolve them in that order, every time.

Famous implementations — the family-by-family guide

For this lesson the "famous implementations" branch is the family guide: the real stores grouped by data-model family, with what each nails, when to use it, and — most importantly — when to avoid it. The "avoid" line is where the real judgment lives.

Relational — PostgreSQL, MySQL

  • Nails: ad-hoc queries, joins across normalized entities, multi-row ACID, invariants via constraints.
  • Use when data is relational and you don't yet know every query — the default for most OLTP systems until a dimension proves otherwise. Postgres (JSONB, full-text, PostGIS, partitioning) often postpones needing a second engine.
  • Avoid when one table's write throughput exceeds a single primary + read replicas and the working set no longer fits one node — then you need sharding or NewSQL.

Key-value — Redis, DynamoDB

  • Nails: sub-millisecond point reads/writes by a known key; predictable horizontal scale.
  • Use when access is purely "value for this key" — sessions, caches, feature flags, rate-limit counters (Redis), or high-scale partition-keyed workloads (DynamoDB).
  • Avoid when you must query by non-key attributes or join. DynamoDB punishes unplanned access patterns; Redis is memory-bound (not a system of record unless persistence and cost are acceptable).

Document — MongoDB

  • Nails: aggregates read/written as a whole (a product with variants, an order with line items) with a flexible evolving schema.
  • Use when the unit of access is one self-contained document and patterns rarely cross document boundaries — content, catalogs, profiles.
  • Avoid when data is highly relational and you emulate joins in app code. "Schemaless" doesn't mean no schema — it pushes enforcement into your application.

Wide-column — Cassandra, HBase

  • Nails: enormous write volume, linear horizontal scale, multi-region availability with tunable consistency.
  • Use when write-heavy append-style at scale — event logs, sensor data, feeds — and you model the table around the exact query (partition + clustering key). Cassandra is AP-leaning with tunable quorums.
  • Avoid when you need ad-hoc queries, joins, or strong multi-row transactions. One table per query pattern; an unplanned query may need a full scan.

Graph — Neo4j

  • Nails: deep multi-hop traversals over densely connected data, relationships first-class.
  • Use when the questions are about connections — fraud rings, social graphs, recommendations — where SQL would mean many self-joins of unknown depth.
  • Avoid when connections are shallow (1–2 hops); a relational join table is simpler, cheaper, and easier to operate. Graphs are also harder to shard.

Search — Elasticsearch

  • Nails: full-text relevance ranking, faceting, fuzzy matching, flexible aggregation over an inverted index.
  • Use when users type queries expecting ranked, typo-tolerant results — almost always a secondary index fed via CDC, not the source of truth.
  • Avoid when you need it as a primary transactional store — near-real-time refresh delay, weak multi-document transactions, not built for system-of-record duty.

Time-series — InfluxDB, Prometheus

  • Nails: high-volume append-only timestamped measurements, time-window scans, downsampling, retention.
  • Use when data is metrics/events indexed by time — monitoring, IoT, financial ticks — with cheap rollups and TTL expiry.
  • Avoid when the pattern isn't time-centric, or you mutate historical rows. Watch cardinality: high-cardinality labels (per-user/per-request IDs) blow up the in-memory index — their classic failure mode (see Metrics & Monitoring).

NewSQL — Spanner, CockroachDB

  • Nails: relational model + SQL plus horizontal write scaling and strong (often serializable) consistency across shards.
  • Use when you genuinely need transactional integrity at relational richness and scale beyond one primary, often multi-region — financial ledgers, global inventory.
  • Avoid when a single Postgres primary + replicas would do. NewSQL adds cost and cross-region commit latency (a consensus round-trip per write, plus 2PC for multi-shard txns). Don't pay for distributed transactions you don't need.

The failure: the familiarity trap

The most common failure mode in real architecture reviews is choosing by familiarity — "I'll use MongoDB because I know it," or reaching for Cassandra because it sounds web-scale. This is Scenario 1.

The tell, and the cure The tell: an engineer who names the database before describing the access pattern. The inverse failure is resume-driven design — picking the trendiest store to learn it on the company's dime. Both skip the framework.

The cure is the same for both: state the access pattern, consistency need, and scale first; let those eliminate engines; then let familiarity and managed-service availability break the remaining tie. Familiarity is a legitimate tiebreaker under operational fit — it must never be the first filter. A principal can defend the choice by walking the dimensions, not by citing comfort.
Polyglot sprawl — Scenario 3's costPolyglot persistence (each service owns the store that fits it) aligns with microservice boundaries, but isn't free: every engine is a new thing to operate, monitor, back up, patch, and staff; derived stores (search, caches) need CDC pipelines with their own lag; cross-store transactions don't exist (you fall back to sagas). The bar for adding a store: an existing one cannot meet a dimension that actually matters for that service. Three well-run engines beat seven half-understood ones.

Self-check — can you reconstruct the three scenarios?

You read the answers above. Close the page and see if you can rebuild them.

  • Scenario 1 (familiarity): What's the tell of a comfort-driven choice, and what's the correct order of reasoning that ends — not starts — with familiarity?
  • Scenario 2 (unplanned query): Why does a query without the partition key fail or full-scan on Cassandra, and why was that fate sealed when you chose the store?
  • Scenario 3 (fourth datastore): What's the operational cost of polyglot, and what bar must a new store clear before you add it?
  • Recite the dimension order and what each phase does (eliminate / narrow / tie-break / veto).
  • Why is relational the right default, and what specific dimension justifies moving off it?
  • Pick any NoSQL family and name exactly what query generality it sacrifices for its niche.

The shape to remember: choosing a DB is dimensions resolved in order — model+access eliminate, consistency+scale narrow, latency/durability/txns tie-break, operational fit vetoes — and the failure is the familiarity trap: naming the DB before the access pattern.

Build it yourself

This skill is judgment under questioning. Work each one before opening the approach.

Defend a choice by walking the dimensions

Someone proposes MongoDB for a feature. Without saying "I prefer X," argue for or against it purely by the dimensions. What's the single question that settles whether a document store beats Postgres+JSONB here?

Reveal the approach

The settling question: "What is the unit of access — do reads and writes stay within one self-contained document, or do they cross document boundaries?" If the natural access is "fetch/update one aggregate by id" and the schema evolves freely, a document store fits. If you find yourself emulating joins across documents or duplicating data you then struggle to keep consistent, the data is relational and Postgres (with JSONB for the nested parts) is the better default. Notice you never mention preference — you name the access pattern, and it picks the engine. That's a defensible choice.

Find the query that breaks the store (the partition-key test)

You're reviewing a Cassandra design. In one question, expose whether a future product ask could become an impossible query. What do you ask?

Reveal the approach

Ask: "What's your partition key, and what happens to a query that doesn't include it?" Cassandra routes a query to a node by hashing the partition key; a query without it can't be routed and degrades to a cluster-wide scan (or is simply disallowed). Because you model one table per query pattern, any access pattern not designed up front is expensive or impossible — that's Scenario 2. The same test generalizes: for any store, ask "what query would be slow or impossible here, and what will you do when product asks for it?"

Design-it-yourself: the open-ended ones

No single right answer — reason it out, then follow the link.

  • Pick stores for a product. Take a ride-hailing app: choose stores for the trip ledger, driver location, surge pricing, and trip history — each justified in one sentence by its dominant dimension. → Uber Backend.
  • Justify (or kill) a NewSQL choice. A team wants Spanner/CockroachDB. Argue when that's right vs when a single Postgres primary + replicas wins, naming the exact dimension that flips it. → Consensus & Raft.
  • Design the CDC pipeline for a derived store. You add Elasticsearch as a search index over Postgres. Keep it in sync without dual writes. What feeds it, and what's the consistency lag? → Stream Processing & CDC.
