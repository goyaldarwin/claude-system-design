SLI, SLO, SLA & Error Budgets

"Make it reliable" is meaningless until you can measure it and agree on how much reliability is enough. SLIs, SLOs, and SLAs turn reliability into numbers; the error budget turns those numbers into a control system that arbitrates the eternal fight between shipping features and staying up. The counterintuitive core: 100% reliability is the wrong target, and a service that's too reliable is a problem.

The trade-off An explicit, agreed reliability target that lets you ship fast inside it — in exchange for accepting (and budgeting for) a measured amount of failure rather than chasing perfection. Defining an SLO gives you a shared, data-driven line: above it you ship features freely; below it you stop and stabilize. You pay by giving up the comfortable fiction of "100% uptime" and committing to act on a budget — which only works if everyone honors it.

What these terms are

Three nested terms, plus the budget they imply:

  • SLI — Service Level Indicator. A measured number about your service's behavior: the ratio of good events to valid events. E.g. "proportion of HTTP requests served in <300ms," "proportion of requests that return non-5xx." It's a fraction, almost always expressed as a percentage.
  • SLO — Service Level Objective. The target for an SLI over a window: "99.9% of requests succeed over 28 days." This is your internal reliability goal — the line you manage to.
  • SLA — Service Level Agreement. A contract with customers that includes consequences (refunds, credits) if you miss a threshold. Always looser than your SLO — you alert yourself (SLO) well before you breach the promise (SLA).
  • Error budget. The flip side of an SLO: if your SLO is 99.9%, your error budget is 0.1% — the amount of failure you're allowed to spend per window. It's permission to fail that much.
The one-line frameSLIs/SLOs trade the fantasy of perfect reliability for an explicit, measured budget you can spend. The error budget reframes reliability from "never fail" to "fail no more than X" — and that remaining X becomes currency: spend it on faster feature releases, or save it by slowing down. The whole system only works because the target is deliberately below 100%.

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide first. If you know all three cold, skip to the nines.

Scenario 1 — Google's Chubby planned outages (real practice) Google's Chubby lock service became so reliable that teams started assuming it would never go down and stopped designing for its failure — building fragile, hidden dependencies on it. Google's response was startling: in any quarter Chubby exceeds its availability target, they deliberately take it down to burn the remaining error budget and flush out those bad assumptions. Why is a service being "too reliable" actually a problem, and what does this say about the right reliability target?
Scenario 2 — the SLO that says "fine" while users suffer Your dashboard shows "average response time: 180ms — well within target." Yet support is flooded with complaints about slowness. It turns out 1% of requests take 8 seconds, and that 1% includes your biggest customers' heaviest queries. Why did the average hide the pain, and what should the SLI have measured instead?
Scenario 3 — the budget that's almost gone, mid-quarter Three weeks into a 28-day window, a series of incidents has burned 90% of your error budget — you have 10% left for the remaining week. Product wants to ship three risky features. What should the error budget do in this moment, and who does it let you say "no" to without it being a political fight?

Scenario 1 is why 100% is the wrong target. Scenario 2 is choosing a good SLI (percentiles, not averages). Scenario 3 is the budget as a control system. Each maps to a decision below.

The four decisions that define a reliability target

Setting up reliability targets is these four choices. Each has a named failure nested under it.

Decision 1 — What do you measure? (choosing the SLI)

A good SLI measures what users actually experience, as a good-events / valid-events ratio. The canonical categories: availability (fraction of requests that succeed), latency (fraction served faster than a threshold), quality/correctness (fraction returning correct data), and for pipelines, freshness/throughput. Pick a small number of SLIs that map to user happiness — not internal metrics like CPU that users never feel.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the average that hides the pain This is Scenario 2. Measuring average latency (or any mean) hides the tail: a 180ms average can contain a 1% of requests taking 8 seconds — and that slow tail is exactly what users notice and complain about, often your biggest/heaviest customers. Define latency SLIs as percentiles ("99% of requests < 300ms"), not averages, so the SLI tracks the worst experiences. (Computing global percentiles correctly needs mergeable histograms, never averaged per-host percentiles.) And measure as close to the user as possible (at the load balancer / client), not deep in a backend that misses upstream failures.

Decision 2 — How high do you set it? (the SLO, and the cost of nines)

The SLO target is a business decision, not "as high as possible." Each additional nine (99% → 99.9% → 99.99%) costs exponentially more in engineering and infrastructure while delivering diminishing user-perceptible value. Set the SLO at the point where users stop noticing improvement — usually well below 100%. A user on a flaky mobile network can't even tell the difference between 99.9% and 99.99% backend availability.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — chasing 100% This is Scenario 1. 100% is the wrong target for essentially everything. First, it's unachievable and absurdly expensive — each nine multiplies cost. Second, and subtler: a service that's more reliable than its SLO trains its dependents to assume it never fails, so they stop handling its failure — turning it into a hidden single point of failure for everyone. Google deliberately takes Chubby down when it's been too reliable, precisely to break those unsafe assumptions. The target reliability should match what users need and no more; the leftover unreliability (the error budget) is a feature, not a bug.

Decision 3 — How does the error budget govern velocity?

The error budget turns the reliability number into a control system arbitrating the structural conflict between dev teams (want to ship features — risk) and SRE/ops (want stability). The policy: budget remaining → ship freely; budget exhausted → feature freeze, all hands on reliability until the budget recovers. This makes the reliability/velocity trade objective and automatic instead of a recurring argument.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the budget nobody honors This is Scenario 3. An error budget only works if there's an agreed, enforced error-budget policy: when 90% is burned with a week left, the budget's job is to halt risky releases and redirect effort to stability — and crucially, it lets engineering say "no" to that risky launch as a data-driven decision, not a political fight ("we're out of budget" beats "I think it's too risky"). If leadership routinely overrides the budget to ship anyway, the SLO is theater — you've measured reliability but built no mechanism to act on it. The budget must have teeth (a freeze) that everyone, including product, has pre-agreed to honor.

Decision 4 — How do you alert on it? (burn-rate alerting)

You don't want to be paged the instant a single request fails, nor find out you blew the budget only at month-end. Burn-rate alerting watches how fast you're consuming the budget: a burn rate of 1 means you'll exactly exhaust the budget by window's end; a burn rate of 10 means you'll exhaust it 10× too fast. Alert on fast burn (page now — a major incident eating the budget in hours) and slow burn (ticket — a chronic low-grade issue that'll exhaust it over days), typically with multi-window checks to avoid false alarms.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — alerts too slow, too noisy, or both Naive alerting fails two ways. Threshold-on-instantaneous-errors pages on every transient blip (noise → alert fatigue → ignored pages). Alert only when the SLO is already breached and you find out too late to act. Multi-window, multi-burn-rate alerting fixes both: a high burn rate over a short window (e.g. 14× over 1h) pages immediately for fast emergencies; a moderate burn rate over a long window (e.g. 3× over 6h) opens a ticket for slow leaks; requiring a short and long window to agree suppresses transient false positives. The goal: every page is real, urgent, and budget-relevant.

The rule in one lineMeasure user-perceived SLIs as percentiles, set the SLO at the fewest nines users actually need (not 100%), spend the resulting error budget on velocity via an enforced policy, and alert on burn rate (multi-window) so pages are timely and real. Each extra nine roughly 10×es cost for shrinking user-visible benefit.

Famous implementations

The practice made concrete. Same facets — what it is, the mechanism, and the failure to watch.

Google SRE error budgets

  • What it is. The originating framework (the SRE book): SLOs + error budgets as the contract between dev velocity and reliability.
  • Mechanism. Budget remaining → ship; budget exhausted → freeze; and the Chubby practice of synthesizing outages when a service is too reliable, to flush hidden dependencies.
  • The failure to watch. A budget with no enforced policy is theater; an SLO measured on averages or internal metrics tracks the wrong thing.

Multi-window burn-rate alerting

  • What it is. The standard way to page on SLO consumption (SRE workbook).
  • Mechanism. Combine a fast-burn alert (high rate, short window → page) and a slow-burn alert (moderate rate, long window → ticket); require short+long windows to agree to suppress blips.
  • The failure to watch. Single-window threshold alerts are either noisy or too late; tune burn-rate thresholds to your budget and window.

SLAs & their consequences

  • What it is. The external contract (e.g. "99.9% monthly uptime or service credits").
  • Mechanism. Set the SLA looser than your internal SLO so your alerts fire and you remediate well before you owe customers credits.
  • The failure to watch. An SLA equal to or tighter than your SLO leaves no margin — you breach the contract the moment you miss your own goal.

Build it yourself

These click when you write the actual numbers. Work each before opening the approach.

Define an SLI and SLO for a checkout API

Your checkout API must feel fast and reliable. Define an availability SLI, a latency SLI, an SLO for each over a window, and explain how you'd measure them.

Reveal the approach

Availability SLI: (count of requests with non-5xx, non-timeout responses) / (count of valid requests), measured at the load balancer (closest to the user, captures backend failures). Latency SLI: proportion of requests served in < 300ms, as a percentile from a histogram — e.g. "p99 < 300ms" — never an average (Scenario 2). SLOs: "99.9% availability over 28 days" and "99% of requests < 300ms over 28 days." Pick 28 days (rolling) so the window is stable and matches a billing cycle. Exclude invalid requests (4xx from bad client input) from the denominator — they're not your failures. The error budget is the 0.1% (≈40 min/month) you may spend on releases.

Design burn-rate alerts for that SLO

For the 99.9%/28-day availability SLO, design alerts that page for a fast emergency but only ticket for a slow leak, without paging on every transient blip.

Reveal the approach

Compute burn rate = (observed error rate) / (allowed error rate, 0.1%). Fast-burn page: burn rate ≥ ~14 sustained over both a 1-hour and a 5-minute window — at 14× you'd exhaust the whole 28-day budget in ~2 days, clearly an emergency; requiring two windows to agree filters out 1-minute blips. Slow-burn ticket: burn rate ≥ ~3 over a 6-hour and 30-minute window — a chronic leak that'd exhaust the budget in ~9 days; ticket, don't page. This multi-window/multi-burn-rate scheme (from the SRE workbook) makes every page urgent and real while catching slow erosion before it blows the budget — avoiding both alert fatigue and finding out too late (Scenario's Decision 4).

Design-it-yourself: the open-ended ones

No single right answer — reason it out, then follow the link.

  • Write an error-budget policy with teeth. Draft the policy that everyone (incl. product) pre-agrees to: what happens at 50%/90%/100% burn, who can override, and how the freeze lifts. → Resilience Patterns.
  • Pick SLOs for a dependency chain. Service A depends on B and C. Given their SLOs, compute A's achievable SLO and decide where to add resilience. → Microservices.
  • Flush hidden dependencies (the Chubby move). Design a safe way to periodically prove your service's dependents handle its failure, without causing a real incident. → Chaos engineering.
