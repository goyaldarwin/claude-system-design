Designing a Metrics and Monitoring System

Build a time-series metrics & monitoring system (Prometheus/Datadog-class) — ingestion at scale, rollups, cardinality, and the query path.

The bottleneck Finite capacity. time-series at huge write volume, paid in cardinality limits, rollups, and tiered retention.

Requirements & scope

We are building the backbone of an observability platform: a system that ingests numeric time-series metrics from tens of thousands of hosts and services, stores them durably with tiered retention, powers near-real-time dashboards, and evaluates alerting rules. Think Prometheus, Datadog, InfluxDB, M3, or Amazon CloudWatch — the metrics pillar specifically, not logs or traces (covered in Observability).

Functional requirements:

  • Ingest the three core metric types: counters (monotonic, e.g. requests_total), gauges (point-in-time, e.g. memory_bytes), and histograms/summaries (latency distributions exposed as bucketed counters).
  • Serve dashboard queries with single-digit-second latency over recent windows, and longer-range queries over downsampled data.
  • Evaluate alerting rules continuously and fire/resolve notifications.
  • Apply retention policies with automatic downsampling.

Non-functional requirements — and the explicit trade-offs we accept:

  • Very high write throughput is the dominant constraint. Reads are a tiny fraction of writes.
  • Lossy and late data are tolerable. Monitoring is statistical, not transactional — dropping 0.01% of datapoints under load is acceptable; blocking ingestion to guarantee every point is not. This is the opposite stance from a payment system.
  • Availability over consistency. A monitoring system must stay up precisely when the rest of the infra is on fire. It is firmly AP.
  • Bounded query cost. A single bad query must not be able to OOM a storage node.

Back-of-envelope estimation

Assume 10,000 hosts. Each host emits roughly 1,000 active time series (host metrics, plus the services it runs and their per-endpoint histograms). That is 10 million active series. With a 10-second scrape interval, each series produces 0.1 datapoints/sec, so:

  • Write rate: 10M series × 0.1 dp/s = 1M datapoints/sec sustained. Large fleets push this to 10–100M dp/s.
  • Raw storage (naive): a datapoint is timestamp (8 B) + value (8 B) = 16 B. 1M dp/s × 16 B = 16 MB/s = ~1.4 TB/day raw. Over 15 days of raw retention that is ~20 TB — before replication.
  • After compression: good TSDB encoding reaches ~1.3 bytes/datapoint (see Gorilla below). That collapses 16 B to ~1.3 B — a ~12× reduction, bringing raw retention to ~1.7 TB. This is why compression is not optional; it is the difference between a viable and an unviable system.
Key insightThe system is write-dominated and the working set is enormous, but the data is highly compressible because consecutive timestamps and values change predictably. The entire storage design exists to exploit that regularity. Reads, by contrast, are bursty and unpredictable — a dashboard refresh can fan out to thousands of series at once.

The time-series data model & cardinality

A time series is uniquely identified by a metric name plus a set of key-value labels (tags). The label set is the identity:

http_requests_total{service="checkout", method="POST", status="500", region="us-east-1", instance="10.2.3.4:9090"}
   → (timestamp, value) (timestamp, value) ...

Each unique combination of label values is a distinct series with its own append-only chunk on disk. This leads directly to the single most important challenge in the whole design.

The cardinality explosion

Cardinality is the total number of distinct series. It is the product of the cardinalities of every label. Add one label with high cardinality — user_id, request_id, email, a raw URL with query params — and the series count multiplies catastrophically:

LabelsDistinct valuesSeries created
service × status × region50 × 10 × 52,500 (fine)
… add endpoint× 200500,000 (watch it)
… add user_id× 1,000,000500 billion (system dies)

Cardinality is the enemy because the in-memory inverted index (label → series IDs, used to resolve queries) grows with the number of series, not the number of datapoints. Memory, not disk, is usually the first thing to fall over. A single misbehaving service tagging metrics with a unique ID can take down ingestion for everyone on a shared cluster.

The cardinality bombThe classic outage: an engineer adds user_id or a full request path as a label "just for debugging." Series count jumps from 1M to billions, the index blows past RAM, the ingester OOMs, and the monitoring system goes dark during the incident it was supposed to observe. Defend with per-tenant series limits, label allow-lists, and active-series alerts on the monitoring system itself.

Estimating distinct-label-value counts for limit enforcement is itself a streaming-cardinality problem — you cannot keep an exact set per tenant cheaply, so use HyperLogLog sketches to approximate distinct series per tenant in bounded memory. See Probabilistic Data Structures for HLL and Bloom filters, the latter useful for "does this series exist?" checks during ingestion.

API surface

# Ingestion (push model, line-protocol style)
POST /v1/write
  metric=http_requests_total
  labels={service:"checkout",status:"500"}
  samples=[(t0,v0),(t1,v1),...]      # batched, gzip-compressed

# Pull model: target merely exposes a scrape endpoint
GET /metrics            → text/plain exposition of all current series

# Query (PromQL-style)
GET /v1/query?query=sum(rate(http_requests_total{status="5.."}[5m])) by (service)&time=...
GET /v1/query_range?query=...&start=...&end=...&step=15s

# Alerting rules CRUD
POST /v1/rules   { expr, for, labels, annotations }

The query language is the product. rate() over a counter, histogram_quantile() over bucketed histograms, and aggregation operators (sum/avg/max by (label)) are the primitives dashboards and alerts are built from.

Ingestion pipeline: push vs pull

The first architectural fork is how datapoints arrive.

Pull (Prometheus scrape)Push (StatsD / Datadog agent)
MechanismServer scrapes /metrics on a scheduleClient/agent sends to a collector
Target discoveryServer-side service discovery; knows what should be upTargets must know where to send
Liveness signalFree — a failed scrape = target downAbsence is ambiguous (down vs. silent)
Short-lived jobsAwkward (need a pushgateway)Natural fit
Firewall/NATServer must reach targetsTargets reach out (easier across boundaries)

Large platforms run a hybrid: pull for infrastructure where service discovery is rich, push for serverless/edge/batch jobs. The key principal point is that the scrape or receive layer is stateless and horizontally shardable — shard targets across collectors by a consistent hash of the target identity (see Consistent Hashing) so adding collectors rebalances minimally.

Buffering through a stream

To decouple ingestion spikes from storage and to tolerate downstream slowness, write into a durable log rather than straight to the TSDB:

  • Collectors append batches to Kafka, partitioned by hash(series_id) so all points for a series land on one partition — preserving per-series order and giving consumers locality.
  • TSDB ingesters consume partitions, build in-memory chunks, and flush. If an ingester falls behind or restarts, Kafka retains the backlog; nothing is lost during a deploy.
  • A stream processing layer (Flink/Kafka Streams) computes pre-aggregated rollups (e.g. per-service request rate) on the fly, so common dashboard queries hit a small materialized series instead of fanning out over millions.
Trade-offThe buffering log adds end-to-end latency (typically a few seconds) and operational cost, but converts a tightly-coupled, back-pressure-fragile pipeline into one where storage can be restarted, scaled, or briefly unavailable without dropping data. For a system that must survive infra incidents, that decoupling is worth the latency.

Storage: TSDB internals & compression

A purpose-built time-series database beats a general KV or relational store here because the access pattern is so specific: append-heavy, time-ordered, queried by label and time range.

LSM-based, time-bucketed layout

Recent data lives in an in-memory head block (a memtable-equivalent). Periodically it is flushed to immutable, time-partitioned blocks on disk — e.g. one block per 2-hour window, later compacted into larger blocks (1-day, then 1-week). This is an LSM-tree specialized for time: writes are always appends to the head, and old blocks are never mutated, only compacted and eventually deleted. A write-ahead log protects the head block against crashes before it is flushed.

Time-bucketed partitioning makes retention a metadata operation: dropping data older than 15 days is just deleting whole blocks — no per-row tombstones, no compaction debt. Range queries touch only the blocks overlapping the query window.

Columnar compression: delta-of-delta & Gorilla

Within a block, timestamps and values are stored in separate columns and compressed aggressively (the Facebook Gorilla scheme):

  • Timestamps — delta-of-delta: scrapes are near-periodic, so the gap between consecutive timestamps is nearly constant. Store the delta of the delta, which is usually 0 and encodes in a single bit.
  • Values — XOR encoding: consecutive float values are often identical or close. XOR the current value with the previous; for slow-moving gauges the result is mostly leading/trailing zeros, encoded in a handful of bits.

Together these reach ~1.3 bytes/datapoint on real workloads — the ~12× win from the estimation section.

Hot / warm / cold tiers

TierMediumHoldsServes
HotRAM + local NVMeLast few hours, full resolutionLive dashboards, alert evaluation
WarmLocal/SSD blocksDays–weeksRecent investigation
ColdObject storage (S3/GCS)Months–years, downsampledLong-range trend queries

Shipping compacted blocks to object storage (the Thanos/Mimir/M3 pattern) decouples storage cost from compute and makes the cluster effectively stateless — ingesters hold only the head block. See Object Storage & CDN.

Rollups, downsampling & retention

Nobody queries 10-second resolution for data from six months ago, and storing it would be ruinous. Downsampling pre-aggregates older data into coarser intervals, preserving the aggregates that queries actually need (count, sum, min, max — from which avg and rate are derivable):

ResolutionRetentionRationale
Raw (10s)15 daysIncident debugging needs fine detail
5-minute rollup90 daysWeekly/monthly trends, capacity planning
1-hour rollup1 year+Year-over-year, long-term trends

Rollups are computed during compaction (a background job over immutable blocks) and stored as separate, lower-cardinality series. The query layer picks the coarsest resolution that satisfies the requested step. Storing min/max alongside the mean is what preserves the ability to see spikes that an average would smooth away.

The query path

A query like sum(rate(http_requests_total[5m])) by (service) executes roughly as:

  1. Parse & plan: resolve the label matchers against the inverted index to get the set of matching series IDs.
  2. Fan-out: the query frontend splits the request across the storage shards that own those series (and across the time-block tiers). This is a scatter-gather — the slowest shard bounds latency, so hedged requests and per-shard timeouts matter.
  3. Per-shard execution: each shard decodes the relevant chunks, applies rate() per series, and partially aggregates.
  4. Merge: the frontend combines partial results, finishes the sum by (service), and returns.

Three things keep this fast and safe: a query frontend that splits long ranges into parallel sub-queries and caches results; pre-aggregated rollups so common dashboard panels never touch raw series; and hard resource limits (max series touched, max samples, query timeout) so one expensive query cannot exhaust a storage node. Caching is layered — see Caching for the result/fragment cache patterns reused here.

Alerting pipeline

Alerting is a continuous query loop, separate from ad-hoc dashboards:

  1. Rule evaluation: a stateless evaluator runs each alert expression (e.g. job:errors:rate5m > 0.05) on a fixed interval against the TSDB. To scale, shard rules across evaluators by rule group.
  2. For-duration: a rule fires only after the condition holds for a configured for window (e.g. 5m), which suppresses flapping on transient spikes.
  3. Alert manager: receives firing alerts and handles deduplication (the same alert from replicated evaluators counts once), grouping (collapse 500 per-host alerts into one "region down" notification), silencing/inhibition (a node-down alert inhibits the 50 service alerts it caused), and routing to channels (PagerDuty, Slack, email).

Alerting rules are where this system connects to SLI/SLO & Error Budgets: SLO burn-rate alerts (fast/slow multi-window) are just metric expressions evaluated by this same pipeline, and the metrics here are the SLIs. Run the evaluator and alert manager replicated and independent of the main storage path so alerting survives a storage outage.

Failure modeNaive per-host alerting causes alert storms — one rack failure pages the on-call 300 times. Grouping and inhibition are not nice-to-haves; without them the alerting layer becomes noise and engineers start ignoring pages, defeating the system's entire purpose.

Bottlenecks & failure modes

  • Cardinality limits (the #1 limit): active-series count, not datapoint volume, is what kills the index in RAM. Enforce per-tenant series quotas at ingestion, reject/relabel high-cardinality labels, and alert on series growth. Everything else is secondary to this.
  • Write amplification: LSM compaction rewrites data multiple times as blocks merge from 2h → 1d → 1w. Tune compaction so it does not starve the ingestion path of disk IO during write peaks.
  • Hot shards: if one service emits vastly more series than others, its shard becomes a hotspot. Shard by series identity (consistent hashing) rather than by metric name to spread load; resharding is the painful operation, so over-provision partitions up front.
  • Late & out-of-order data: a host with clock skew or a delayed batch arrives after its block was flushed. Allow a bounded out-of-order window into the head block; drop or route to a separate block anything older. Tolerating late data is explicitly in scope.
  • Query blast radius: an unbounded query ({__name__=~".+"} over a year) can scan everything. Hard limits and a query frontend that rejects over-broad plans are mandatory.
Compare & contrastThis problem is a close cousin of Ad Click Aggregation: both ingest a high-volume event stream, both pre-aggregate via stream processing, and both accept approximate/lossy results for speed. The difference is the query model — ad-click aggregation answers a handful of known aggregation queries, whereas a metrics system must serve arbitrary label-matcher queries, which is exactly why the inverted index and its cardinality limits dominate this design.

Principal-level mastery check

What strong engineers doThey identify cardinality as the central challenge within the first few minutes and structure the whole design around protecting the index — rather than getting lost in compression tricks. They explicitly state that the system is write-dominated, AP, and tolerant of loss, and they treat the monitoring system's own reliability (it must survive the incidents it observes) as a first-class requirement. They separate the ingestion, storage, query, and alerting planes so each scales and fails independently.

Probing questions to ask engineers:

  • "An engineer adds user_id as a label and your active series jumps from 1M to 5B. Walk me through exactly what breaks first and how you prevent it."
  • "You quoted 1.3 bytes per datapoint. Where does that compression come from, and what kind of metric would compress badly?"
  • "A dashboard query for the last 6 months and one for the last 10 minutes hit the same API. How does the system serve each differently?"
  • "A rack of 300 hosts fails. How many pages does the on-call get, and why?"
  • "Your storage cluster is down for 10 minutes during a deploy. What happens to in-flight datapoints, and what happens to alert evaluation?"
  • "Why a purpose-built TSDB instead of putting this in Cassandra or Postgres? Be specific about the access pattern."
