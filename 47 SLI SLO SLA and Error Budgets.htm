SLI, SLO, SLA and Error Budgets

The vocabulary that turns "is the service reliable?" into a number you can measure, alert on, and trade against feature velocity.

The vocabulary: SLI, SLO, SLA

These three terms come from Google's SRE practice and are constantly confused, including by senior engineers. Getting them straight is the price of admission to any serious reliability conversation.

  • SLI — Service Level Indicator: a measurement. A quantitative metric of some aspect of the service, almost always expressed as a ratio of good events to total events over a window. Example: "the proportion of HTTP requests served in under 300 ms." An SLI is a number between 0 and 100%.
  • SLO — Service Level Objective: a target for an SLI. "99.9% of requests served in under 300 ms over a rolling 28-day window." The SLO is an internal commitment the team holds itself to. It has no direct external consequence.
  • SLA — Service Level Agreement: a contract with a customer that includes consequences — service credits, refunds, penalties — if the objective is missed. An SLA is a business and legal artifact, not an engineering one.
Key insightThe relationship is a strict hierarchy: SLA < SLO < the reliability you actually deliver. Your internal SLO is deliberately tighter than the SLA you sign, so that when you start trending toward an SLO breach you still have a buffer of weeks before you owe anyone money. If your SLA is 99.9% and your SLO is also 99.9%, you have no margin and will be writing refund checks the first time the SLO wobbles. A common rule of thumb: set the SLO one extra nine tighter, or at least add a comfortable gap.

One subtle but important point: not every service needs an SLA, but every user-facing service should have an SLO. Internal services frequently have SLOs and no SLA at all — the "contract" is just the expectation other teams build against. Conversely, signing an SLA without an internal SLO to back it is how organizations sleepwalk into penalty payments.

Choosing good SLIs

An SLI is only useful if it correlates with what users actually experience. A CPU-utilization graph is a fine operational metric but a terrible SLI — users do not care about your CPU, they care whether their request succeeded and was fast. Good SLIs measure the user-visible boundary of the service. The canonical SLI categories:

  • Availability: the fraction of valid requests that succeed. For request-driven services this is the workhorse SLI.
  • Latency: the fraction of requests served faster than a threshold. Almost always the most user-impactful SLI for interactive systems.
  • Throughput / freshness: for pipelines and streaming systems, how much data is processed and how stale the output is. See Stream Processing and CDC and Batch and Big Data Processing.
  • Error rate: the inverse framing of availability — the fraction of requests that fail with 5xx, timeouts, or malformed responses.
  • Correctness / durability: the fraction of operations that return the right answer, or the fraction of stored objects not lost. Often the hardest to measure but the most damaging to violate. S3 advertises "eleven nines" of durability, which is a correctness SLI, not an availability one.

The good-events / valid-events ratio

The most durable way to specify an SLI is as a ratio with carefully defined numerator and denominator:

SLI = good events / valid events

availability_sli = (count of requests with status < 500 and not timeout)
                 / (count of all requests, excluding client errors we don't own)

latency_sli      = (count of requests faster than 300ms)
                 / (count of all valid requests)

The hard, interesting work is in the definitions. What counts as "valid"? A 400 caused by a malformed client request is usually not your fault and should be excluded from the denominator — otherwise a buggy client tanks your SLO. A 429 from your own rate limiter is arguably a success (the system worked as designed) and might be excluded too. Where you measure also matters: at the load balancer, you miss failures that never reached it; at the client, you capture the true experience but conflate it with the user's own network. Principal engineers debate the denominator as hard as the numerator.

PitfallMeasuring availability at the server misses the failures that matter most: the requests that timed out before your server logged anything, or that failed at a CDN/load balancer hop you don't instrument. The infamous failure mode is a service reporting "99.99% availability" from its own logs while users see a hard outage, because the requests never arrived to be counted. Where you measure is a design decision, not a detail.

Setting SLOs: percentiles, not averages

The single most important SLI discipline is to specify latency as a percentile, never as an average. The mean is actively misleading for latency because the distribution is heavily right-skewed: a handful of multi-second requests barely move the mean while ruining the experience for real users.

StatisticWhat it tells youWhy it misleads
Mean (average)Central tendencyA 10 ms median and a 5 s tail can average to a "fine" number; hides the tail entirely
p50 (median)Typical experienceSays nothing about the unhappy half-percent
p99Tail latency — the slow 1%The right target for most user-facing SLOs
p99.9Worst-case tailWhere retries, GC pauses, cold caches, and tail amplification show up

Why p99 (and p99.9) matter so much: in a system that fans out a single user action into many backend calls — a typical microservices request hitting dozens of services — the user waits for the slowest of those calls. This is tail latency amplification: if each backend has a 1% chance of being slow, a request touching 100 backends has roughly a 63% chance of hitting at least one slow one. So the median user experience is governed by your backends' p99, not their median. Jeff Dean's "The Tail at Scale" is the canonical reference. This is also why resilience patterns like hedged requests and timeouts exist — to cut the tail.

What strong engineers doThey never say "the SLO is 200 ms latency." They say "the SLO is p99 latency under 200 ms, measured at the edge, over a rolling 28-day window, excluding requests that 4xx for client reasons." Every clause — the percentile, the threshold, the measurement point, the window, the exclusions — is a deliberate choice that changes what the number means.

The measurement window

SLOs are defined over a window, typically a rolling 28 days (four weeks captures weekly cyclicality and is divisible cleanly). A rolling window is preferred over a calendar month because it avoids the perverse incentive of "we already blew the budget on the 3rd, so reliability doesn't matter for the rest of the month." With a rolling window, every bad day ages out 28 days later, so the budget continuously replenishes.

Error budgets as a control system

The error budget is the inverse of the SLO. If your SLO is 99.9% availability, your error budget is 0.1% — the amount of unreliability you are allowed to spend. Over a 28-day window:

error budget = (1 - SLO) × total events

For 99.9% over 28 days at 1000 req/s:
  total requests   = 1000 × 86400 × 28  ≈ 2.42 billion
  budget (0.1%)    ≈ 2.42 million failed requests allowed

Expressed as time (a simplification, see warning below):
  99.9%  over 28 days  →  ~40 minutes of downtime
  99.99% over 28 days  →  ~4 minutes
  99.999% over 28 days →  ~24 seconds
Pitfall"Minutes of downtime" is a lossy way to express an error budget. A request-ratio budget and a time budget diverge under partial failure: if 5% of requests fail continuously, you are never "down" but you burn budget steadily. Prefer the request-success ratio as the source of truth and treat time-based figures as intuition pumps only.

How the budget arbitrates velocity vs. reliability

This is the real reason error budgets exist, and the part most often misunderstood. The eternal tension is between product teams (who want to ship features fast) and SRE/ops (who want stability). Without a shared number, this is a religious war settled by whoever shouts loudest. The error budget makes it a data-driven contract:

  • Budget remaining: the team ships freely. Risky changes, faster rollouts, more experiments — the budget is there to be spent. An unused error budget is a signal you are being too conservative and shipping too slowly.
  • Budget exhausted: a pre-agreed policy kicks in — typically a feature freeze. All engineering effort redirects to reliability work until the budget recovers. No debate, no escalation, the policy was agreed in advance when everyone was calm.

The genius is that it aligns incentives: the product team now owns reliability, because their feature velocity is gated by it. They stop treating outages as "ops' problem." This is why deployment strategies like canary and progressive rollout matter — they let you spend budget in small, observable increments rather than betting it all on a big-bang release. The budget also funds deliberate risk: planned failover drills and chaos experiments are legitimate budget expenditures.

Trade-offAn error budget makes the velocity/reliability trade-off explicit but it is only as good as the policy attached to it and the leadership willingness to enforce a freeze. The most common failure is a budget that everyone watches and no one acts on — "we're over budget but we have a launch, so we'll ship anyway." A budget with no teeth is just a dashboard.

Burn-rate alerting

Naively, you might alert "fire when the 28-day SLO is breached." That is useless — by the time the rolling window crosses the threshold, you have already been failing for a long time. The modern approach, from the Google SRE Workbook, is multi-window, multi-burn-rate alerting.

The burn rate is how fast you are consuming the error budget relative to the rate that would exhaust it exactly at the end of the window. A burn rate of 1 means you'll spend the whole budget over 28 days. A burn rate of 14.4 means you'll exhaust the entire 28-day budget in 2 days.

burn_rate = (observed error rate) / (1 - SLO)

# Example: SLO 99.9%, so budget error rate = 0.001
# If current error rate = 0.014, burn_rate = 14
# That burns the full 28-day budget in 28/14 = 2 days

You configure multiple alerts at different burn rates and time windows, trading off how much budget you're willing to lose before paging against how many false pages you tolerate:

Burn rateLong windowShort window (guard)Budget consumed before pageSeverity
14.4×1 hour5 min~2%Page immediately
6×6 hours30 min~5%Page
1×3 days6 hours~10%Ticket (no page)

The short window acts as a guard: the alert only fires if both the long window and the recent short window are burning hot. This stops a long-window alert from continuing to page for an hour after the incident has already been fixed (good for fast reset) and stops a brief blip from tripping the slow-burn alert. Fast burns page a human; slow burns open a ticket. Tying alerts to budget burn rather than raw thresholds is what keeps on-call humane and meaningful — see Observability for how these signals are collected and surfaced.

Key insightBurn-rate alerting solves the classic alerting dilemma. Threshold alerts ("error rate > 1%") are either too sensitive (page on every blip) or too slow (miss a sustained slow bleed). Symptom-based, budget-relative alerting pages proportional to user harm per unit time, which is exactly what you want a human woken up for.

The fallacy of 100% and the cost of each nine

The most important reliability insight, and the one that separates principal engineers from the rest: 100% is the wrong target for almost everything. The reasons are both economic and physical.

  • The user can't tell. Between the service and the user sits the user's WiFi, their ISP, DNS, their phone's flaky radio, and the public internet — a chain that is itself maybe 99.9% reliable. Pushing your service from 99.99% to 99.999% is invisible to a user whose own connection is the bottleneck. You are spending fortunes on reliability the user physically cannot perceive.
  • Each nine costs roughly an order of magnitude more. Going from 99.9% to 99.99% typically means redundant everything, multi-AZ then multi-region, automated failover, and far more sophisticated resilience patterns. The next nine after that may require active-active across regions, removal of every shared dependency, and a doubling of operational maturity.
  • 100% forbids change. You cannot deploy, patch, or experiment if you have zero budget for error. A 100% target is a velocity freeze in disguise. The error budget exists precisely to spend on the risk of shipping.

What strong engineers doThey ask "what reliability does this service actually need?" before quoting a number. A payment authorization path (payment system) may justify five nines and the cost that comes with it; an internal analytics dashboard is fine at 99%. They right-size the target to the user impact and the cost of the next nine, rather than reflexively chasing 100%. They also distinguish criticality within a service — the read path and the write path often warrant different SLOs.

Principal-level mastery check

What strong engineers doStrong engineers treat reliability as a quantified, negotiable budget rather than a vibe. They define SLIs as good/valid ratios with explicit numerators, denominators, and measurement points; they always specify latency SLOs as percentiles with a window; they connect the error budget to a concrete velocity policy with leadership buy-in; and they reflexively question whether more nines are worth the cost. They know the difference between an internal SLO and a customer SLA, and they design the gap between them deliberately.
  • A team tells you their service is "99.99% available" based on their server access logs, but customers report frequent outages. Walk me through every reason those two facts can both be true, and how you'd fix the measurement.
  • Your service fans out each user request to 30 backend microservices. Each backend has a p99 of 50 ms. What is the user-facing latency distribution, and what techniques would you use to control the tail?
  • You're signing an SLA with a customer for 99.9% availability. What internal SLO would you set, why the gap, and how would you structure burn-rate alerts to give your team time to react before you owe service credits?
  • The product team has blown through the error budget two weeks into a 28-day window but has a major launch scheduled. The pre-agreed policy is a feature freeze. What do you actually do, and how do you keep the budget from becoming a dashboard nobody respects?
  • How would you define SLIs for a system that isn't request/response — say a Kafka-based pipeline or a job scheduler? What replaces availability and latency?
  • Leadership wants to "add another nine" to a service. How do you estimate the cost, identify which dependency is the limiting factor, and decide whether it's worth it?
