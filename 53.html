Multi-Region & Disaster Recovery

An entire cloud region can disappear — a fire, a fiber cut, a botched config, a natural disaster. Surviving that means running in more than one region, which is the hardest reliability problem there is: it forces you to confront CAP across continents, replicate data over high-latency links, fail over without splitting your brain, and prove it all works before the disaster. Two numbers — how much data and how much time you can lose — drive every decision.

The trade-off Survival of a whole-region failure — in exchange for cross-region replication latency, multi-region complexity and cost, and the consistency compromises CAP forces across continents. Multiple regions let you ride out a regional outage and serve users closer to home. You pay with the latency and cost of replicating data across the planet, the operational complexity of failover, and the hard CAP trade-offs that geographic distance makes unavoidable.

What multi-region & DR are — surviving a region loss

A cloud region is a geographic cluster of datacenters. Running across multiple availability zones within one region survives a single-datacenter failure — but not a whole-region event (a regional network/power/config failure, a disaster). Disaster recovery (DR) and multi-region architecture are about surviving that: keeping a copy of your system and data in another region so you can continue (or recover quickly) when one region goes dark.

Everything is driven by two numbers you must set with the business before designing:

  • RPO — Recovery Point Objective: how much data you can afford to lose, measured in time. RPO = 0 means "not a single committed write"; RPO = 5 min means "up to 5 minutes of recent writes may be lost."
  • RTO — Recovery Time Objective: how much time you can afford to be down. RTO = 0 means "no downtime"; RTO = 1 hour means "back within an hour."
The one-line frameMulti-region trades cross-region latency, cost, and complexity for survival of a region-level disaster. The tighter your RPO/RTO (toward zero data loss and zero downtime), the more you pay — synchronous cross-region replication, active-active everywhere, constant failover testing. The numbers are a business decision; the architecture follows from them, and the cruelest failures are the dependencies you forgot were single-region.

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide first. If you know all three cold, skip to the DR strategies.

Scenario 1 — AWS S3 us-east-1, 28 February 2017 (real incident) A typo in an operational command took down a large chunk of S3 in us-east-1 for hours. The damage spread far beyond S3: countless apps that assumed S3 was always there broke, and even AWS's own status dashboard couldn't update — because it depended on the very S3 region that was down. Yet some companies (e.g. Netflix) sailed through. Why did one region's outage break so many "multi-region" systems, and what made the survivors different?
Scenario 2 — the failover that had never been tried You have a passive standby region, replicated and ready. The primary region fails for real — and now you discover the failover runbook is stale, DNS TTLs are too long, the standby's capacity was never scaled for full load, and a config secret was only in the primary. Recovery takes hours instead of minutes. Why do untested failovers fail, and what practice ensures the standby actually works when you need it?
Scenario 3 — both regions think they're primary Your active-passive setup fails over to the standby during a network partition between regions — but the original primary didn't actually die, it was just unreachable. Now both regions accept writes, and they diverge. When the partition heals, you have conflicting data in two places and no automatic way to reconcile. What is this, and how do you fail over without it?

Scenario 1 is RPO/RTO & hidden single-region dependencies. Scenario 2 is testing failover (game days). Scenario 3 is failover & split-brain. Each maps to a decision below.

The four decisions that define a DR design

Designing for region failure is these four choices. Each has a named failure nested under it.

Decision 1 — Set RPO and RTO (and find every dependency)

Before any architecture, the business sets RPO (tolerable data loss) and RTO (tolerable downtime). These two numbers determine everything downstream — a 0/0 target demands active-active with synchronous replication (expensive); a "few hours / few minutes of data" target allows a cheap warm standby. Equally important: inventory every dependency and confirm each is regionally resilient — databases, queues, secrets, config, DNS, and third-party services. A multi-region app is only as resilient as its least resilient dependency.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the hidden single-region dependency This is Scenario 1. The S3 us-east-1 outage broke systems that thought they were resilient because they had an undiscovered hard dependency on that one region — an S3 bucket, a control-plane API, a secret, even AWS's own status dashboard (which depended on the down region). The lesson: a single forgotten single-region dependency makes your whole system single-region. Survivors like Netflix had genuinely region-independent architectures and could shift traffic to healthy regions. You must enumerate dependencies and verify each fails over — including control-plane calls (e.g. some global services' control planes live in us-east-1, so don't depend on them during a us-east-1 failover). Multi-AZ is not multi-region; assuming it covers a regional event is the trap.

Decision 2 — Active-passive or active-active?

Two topologies. Active-passive (failover): one region serves; a standby is kept ready (cold/warm/hot) and takes over on disaster. Simpler, cheaper, but has failover time (RTO) and possible data loss (RPO). Active-active: all regions serve traffic simultaneously, replicating to each other. Lowest RTO (a region loss just sheds its share of traffic) and serves users locally — but you must handle multi-leader write conflicts and the full CAP trade-off, and it's the most complex/costly.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the untested failover This is Scenario 2. An active-passive standby that's never exercised is Schrödinger's DR — you don't know if it works until disaster forces the test, and that's when you discover stale runbooks, too-long DNS TTLs, an under-scaled standby, missing secrets, or replication that silently stopped. The fix is DR game days: regularly and deliberately fail over to the standby (ideally in production, like Netflix's region-evacuation exercises), measure actual RTO/RPO against targets, and fix what breaks — so the real failover is a rehearsed routine, not a first attempt under fire. Active-active sidesteps much of this because the "standby" is always serving real traffic, so it can't silently rot.

Decision 3 — How does data replicate across regions?

Cross-region links have high latency (tens to hundreds of ms) and can partition. Synchronous replication (wait for the remote region to confirm) gives RPO=0 but adds that latency to every write and stalls if the remote is unreachable. Asynchronous replication (confirm locally, ship to the remote in the background) keeps writes fast but means recent writes can be lost on a sudden region loss (RPO > 0). This is the sync-vs-async durability knob stretched across continents, where the latency cost is brutal.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — async-lag data loss (or sync-latency stalls) With async cross-region replication, a region that fails takes its un-replicated recent writes with it — your real RPO equals the replication lag at the moment of failure (could be seconds to minutes of acknowledged writes gone). With sync, you avoid the loss but pay cross-region latency on every write and risk write-unavailability when the remote region is slow/unreachable — CAP biting at continental scale. The resolution is to match the choice to RPO per dataset: synchronous (or quorum across regions, like Spanner) only for data that truly can't lose a write (payments, ledgers); asynchronous for data where seconds of loss is acceptable (most things). You rarely want one global setting — classify by how much each dataset can afford to lose.

Decision 4 — How do you route traffic and fail over safely?

Two routing tools: GeoDNS (DNS returns a region based on the user's location — simple, but DNS caching/TTL slows failover) and anycast (the same IP announced from many locations; the network routes to the nearest — fast failover, used by CDNs/global load balancers). Failover orchestration must decide a region is really dead and promote another — and crucially, must prevent two regions from both acting as primary.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — cross-region split-brain This is Scenario 3. If failover promotes the standby because the primary is unreachable — but the primary is actually alive (just partitioned) — both regions accept writes and diverge (split-brain at continental scale), producing conflicting data that needs painful manual reconciliation on heal (like GitHub 2018). Prevention: use a consensus-based / quorum decision for promotion (a majority — often a third witness region/zone — must agree the primary is gone, so a partitioned primary can't keep its role), fence the old primary (revoke its ability to accept writes, e.g. via a global lock or its replication being cut), and for active-active, design conflict resolution (CRDTs / clear per-region data ownership) up front. Never let "I can't reach it" alone trigger promotion to a second writer.

Famous implementations

The pieces made concrete. Same facets — what it is, the mechanism, and the failure to watch.

Global routing — GeoDNS / anycast

  • What it is. How users reach the nearest healthy region — GeoDNS (location-based DNS answers) and anycast (one IP, nearest network path).
  • Mechanism. Health-checked DNS failover (Route 53) reroutes on region failure; anycast reroutes at the network layer near-instantly (CDNs, Global Accelerator).
  • The failure to watch. DNS TTL/caching delays GeoDNS failover (set short TTLs for failover records); clients caching old IPs slow recovery.

Active-active stores — Spanner / DynamoDB global tables / Cosmos

  • What it is. Databases that natively replicate and serve across regions.
  • Mechanism. Spanner: synchronous Paxos + TrueTime for global strong consistency (RPO≈0, latency cost). DynamoDB global tables / Cosmos: multi-leader async with last-write-wins or custom conflict resolution (low latency, conflict risk).
  • The failure to watch. Strong (Spanner) pays write latency; LWW multi-leader (DynamoDB/Cosmos) can silently drop concurrent writes — know which you have (PACELC).

DR game days (Netflix region evacuation)

  • What it is. Deliberately exercising regional failover — Netflix famously evacuates an entire AWS region as a test.
  • Mechanism. Regularly redirect all traffic away from a region in production, measure real RTO/RPO, and fix what breaks — turning failover into a rehearsed routine.
  • The failure to watch. Skipping this is Scenario 2 — the failover you never test is the one that fails when it counts.

Build it yourself

These click when you reason from the numbers. Work each before opening the approach.

Pick a DR strategy from RPO/RTO

For three systems — (a) a payments ledger, (b) a user-facing web app, (c) an internal analytics warehouse — set RPO/RTO and choose a DR strategy for each, justifying the cost.

Reveal the approach

(a) Payments ledger: RPO ≈ 0 (can't lose a committed transfer), RTO low → active-active or synchronous multi-region (Spanner-style quorum) for the ledger — pay the write latency because a lost or double payment is unacceptable. (b) Web app: RPO seconds–minutes, RTO minutes → warm standby or active-active with async replication — losing a few seconds of non-critical writes on a rare regional disaster is acceptable; prioritize fast recovery and local latency. (c) Analytics warehouse: RPO/RTO of hours is fine (it's derived, rebuildable, not user-facing) → backup-and-restore or pilot light — cheapest, since you can re-derive from sources. The discipline: let the business set RPO/RTO per system, then pick the cheapest strategy that meets it — don't pay active-active prices for data that tolerates hours of loss.

Design a failover that can't split-brain

Active-passive across two regions. Design failover so a network partition between regions never makes both accept writes, while still failing over for a real disaster.

Reveal the approach

Don't let "primary unreachable from the standby" alone trigger promotion — that's how a partitioned-but-alive primary becomes a second writer (Scenario 3). Use a quorum decision with a third site: a witness/arbiter in a third region (or a consensus service) must agree the primary is gone before the standby is promoted — so during a two-region partition, only the side with the witness majority can hold/claim primary; the isolated old primary loses quorum and steps down / fences itself (stops accepting writes). Combine with: a fencing mechanism (the promoted region gets a higher epoch/token; storage rejects the old primary's writes — 36), short DNS TTLs / anycast for fast traffic shift, and ensuring the standby has secrets/capacity ready. For a real disaster (primary truly gone), the witness sees it's gone and promotion proceeds. Net: failover happens for real failures, never for a mere partition — one writer at all times.

Design-it-yourself: the open-ended ones

No single right answer — reason it out, then follow the link.

  • Audit for hidden single-region dependencies. Take a "multi-region" service and hunt for the S3-style traps — buckets, secrets, control-plane calls, DNS, third parties pinned to one region. → Object Storage & CDN.
  • Design a region-evacuation game day. Plan a production exercise that shifts all traffic out of one region, with hypothesis, blast-radius limits, success criteria (meet RTO/RPO), and abort plan. → Resilience Patterns.
  • Choose data residency-aware regions. Some users' data must stay in-country (GDPR/sovereignty). Design multi-region replication that respects residency while still surviving a region loss. → Security.
