Domain-Driven Design

DDD as the bridge between business domains and service boundaries — the discipline behind getting microservice decomposition right.

The bottleneck Operability. drawing boundaries that match the business, so services/teams/transactions align — paid in modeling discipline.

In this lesson

  • Why DDD belongs in a system-design course

  • Context mapping: how contexts relate

Why DDD belongs in a system-design course

Most system-design failures are not about the wrong database or the wrong queue — they are about boundaries drawn in the wrong place. A team splits services along technical layers, or along whatever the org chart looked like that quarter, and ends up with a distributed monolith: every feature requires a synchronized deploy across five services, and a single "order" concept means four different things in four codebases. Domain-Driven Design (Eric Evans, 2003) is the discipline that gives you a principled answer to the question that dominates every architecture review: where do the seams go?

DDD operates at two altitudes. Strategic DDD is about carving the problem space into bounded contexts and deciding how they relate — this is the part that directly produces service and team boundaries. Tactical DDD is the in-the-small modeling toolkit (entities, value objects, aggregates) that determines your transaction boundaries and consistency model. A principal engineer uses the strategic half to defend a decomposition and the tactical half to defend a data model.

Strategic DDD: domains, contexts, language

A domain is the overall problem space your business operates in — ride-hailing, payments, e-commerce. It decomposes into subdomains, and the single most leverage-producing act in strategic DDD is classifying them:

Subdomain typeWhat it isInvestment strategy
CoreYour competitive differentiator — the reason customers choose you (e.g. Uber's matching/dispatch, Netflix's recommendations)Best engineers, deepest modeling, build in-house, never outsource
SupportingNecessary but not differentiating (e.g. a driver-rating workflow)Build pragmatically, lower polish, often a good place for off-the-shelf plus glue
GenericSolved problems everyone needs (auth, billing, notifications)Buy or adopt — Auth0, Stripe, SendGrid; do not reinvent

This classification is a strategic resourcing tool: it tells you where to spend your strongest people and where a SaaS vendor is the right answer. Spending your best modelers on a generic subdomain is a classic waste, and outsourcing your core subdomain is how companies lose their moat.

Bounded contexts — the central idea

The keystone of all of DDD is the bounded context: an explicit boundary within which a particular domain model is defined and internally consistent. The crucial insight is that a model is only valid within its context. The word "Customer" in a Sales context (a lead with a pipeline stage) is a genuinely different concept from "Customer" in a Billing context (an account with a payment method and tax ID) or a Support context (a ticket history). Trying to build one canonical, organization-wide "Customer" model is the anti-pattern DDD was invented to kill — it produces a bloated god-object that no team fully owns and every team is afraid to change.

Within a bounded context the team shares a ubiquitous language: the same terms are used by domain experts, in conversation, in the code, and in the schema. If the business says "settlement," the class is Settlement, not TransactionFinalizer. The point is to eliminate the translation layer between how the business talks and how the code is written, because that translation layer is where bugs and misunderstandings breed.

Key insightA bounded context is a linguistic and model boundary, not a deployment artifact. It maps naturally onto a service, but the boundary exists conceptually even inside a monolith (as a module with its own model). Get the context right first; the service split is a downstream packaging decision.

Context mapping: how contexts relate

Real systems have many contexts that must integrate. A context map documents the relationships and, critically, the power dynamics between teams. The standard patterns:

  • Partnership — two contexts succeed or fail together; teams coordinate planning and releases as peers.
  • Customer–Supplier — an upstream context provides for a downstream one, and the downstream's needs are a prioritized input to the upstream's backlog.
  • Conformist — the downstream simply accepts the upstream's model wholesale (no negotiating power), e.g. integrating with a large external API you cannot influence.
  • Anti-Corruption Layer (ACL) — the downstream builds a translation layer that maps the foreign model into its own clean model, protecting it from upstream changes and leaky abstractions. This is the most important defensive pattern and the standard way to integrate with legacy systems.
  • Shared Kernel — two teams share a small common model/codebase; powerful but creates tight coupling, so it must be tiny and jointly governed.
  • Open-Host Service / Published Language — an upstream exposes a well-documented, stable protocol (a public API with a versioned schema) so many downstreams can integrate without bespoke deals.

Why contexts map to service and team boundaries

Conway's Law states that systems mirror the communication structure of the organizations that build them. DDD turns this from a curse into a design tool (the Inverse Conway Maneuver): if you want a clean service boundary, you give one team full ownership of one bounded context, end to end. Because the team shares a ubiquitous language and a single model, the cognitive load stays bounded and they can evolve their service independently. A bounded context that spans two teams will accumulate coordination overhead and erode; two contexts crammed into one team's service will entangle. This is precisely the decomposition logic behind Microservices & Service Mesh, and it is the lens to apply when reading Architectural Styles & How to Choose — the choice between monolith, modular monolith, and microservices is really a choice about how many contexts share a deployable.

Trade-offA modular monolith keeps bounded contexts as in-process modules with enforced boundaries — you get the model clarity of DDD without the operational tax of a distributed system. Split a module into its own service only when its scaling, deployment cadence, or team-ownership needs genuinely diverge. "One context = one module" is cheap; "one context = one service" buys independence at the cost of network calls, distributed transactions, and operational surface.

Tactical DDD: the building blocks

Inside a single bounded context, tactical patterns shape the model:

  • Entity — an object defined by identity and continuity over time, not its attributes. A User with id 42 is the same user even after they change their name.
  • Value Object — defined entirely by its attributes, immutable, no identity. Money(amount, currency), Address, DateRange. Two value objects with equal fields are interchangeable. Pushing logic into value objects shrinks entities and removes a huge class of bugs.
  • Aggregate — a cluster of entities and value objects treated as a single unit, with one aggregate root as the only entry point. Outsiders reference the aggregate only by the root's id.
  • Repository — the collection-like abstraction for loading and persisting whole aggregates, hiding the storage mechanism behind a domain-shaped interface.
  • Domain Service — stateless domain logic that doesn't naturally belong to any single entity (e.g. a TransferService coordinating two accounts).
  • Factory — encapsulates complex aggregate construction so an aggregate is never created in an invalid state.
  • Domain Event — a record that something meaningful happened in the domain (OrderPlaced, PaymentSettled), expressed in the ubiquitous language and past-tense.

The aggregate as a consistency boundary

The deepest tactical insight, and the one that connects DDD to distributed systems, is this: an aggregate is the unit of transactional consistency. The rule is "one aggregate, one transaction" — a single command modifies exactly one aggregate atomically, and invariants are enforced only within that aggregate. Anything spanning multiple aggregates is updated eventually, not in the same transaction.

This is not bureaucracy; it is what makes a system scalable. If your aggregate boundaries are correct, every write is a local, single-row-or-document transaction — which is exactly why an aggregate maps cleanly onto a row in a relational DB or a document in a document store, and why aggregates shard well. The moment you need a single transaction across two aggregates (or two services), you have left the world of local ACID and entered Distributed Transactions: you reach for the saga pattern, where a business process becomes a sequence of local transactions, each emitting a domain event that triggers the next, with compensating actions to undo on failure. Designing aggregates well is therefore the act of deciding what must be strongly consistent versus what can be eventually consistent — the most consequential decision in the whole data model.

// Aggregate root enforces invariants; outsiders only touch the root.
class Order {                       // aggregate root (Entity)
    private OrderId id;
    private List<OrderLine> lines;  // child entities, no external refs
    private Money total;            // Value Object, recomputed internally
    private CustomerId customerId;  // reference OTHER aggregate by id only

    void addLine(ProductId p, int qty) {
        if (this.status != DRAFT) throw new IllegalState();
        // invariant (e.g. max 50 lines) enforced here, atomically
        lines.add(new OrderLine(p, qty));
        this.total = recomputeTotal();
    }
}
// Repository persists the WHOLE aggregate in one transaction.
orderRepository.save(order);        // single atomic write

Domain events as the integration mechanism

A domain event emitted inside a context (e.g. OrderPlaced) often needs to reach other contexts. The pattern is to translate an internal domain event into an integration event published on a broker, which downstream contexts subscribe to and react to within their own model. This is the bridge from tactical DDD straight into Event-Driven Architecture: events become the loose-coupling glue between bounded contexts, letting each context maintain its own data and react asynchronously rather than calling the source service synchronously. The outbox pattern (write the event to a table in the same local transaction as the aggregate, then relay it) is the standard way to publish events reliably without a distributed transaction.

How DDD drives system design

The throughline of this lesson is a chain of three mappings that turn domain modeling directly into architecture:

  1. Bounded context → service. Each context becomes an engineer service (or a module in a modular monolith) owned by one team, with its own ubiquitous language and model. This is your decomposition.
  2. Aggregate → transaction boundary. Each aggregate is the unit of local ACID consistency. Anything bigger needs a saga. This determines your consistency model and where eventual consistency is acceptable.
  3. Domain event → integration event. Cross-context communication is event-driven and asynchronous, decoupling services in time and at deploy boundaries.

A direct and famous consequence is database-per-service. If a bounded context owns its model, no other context may reach into its tables — shared databases are the back door that re-couples "independent" services and re-creates the distributed monolith. Each context owns its persistence; integration happens only through published APIs and events. This is also why DDD interacts with choosing a database: different contexts may legitimately choose different stores (a graph DB for one, a document store for another) because nothing else depends on their internal schema.

The payoffWhen the three mappings hold, a feature that lives entirely in one context is a single-team, single-service, single-deploy change with a local transaction. That is the operational definition of a "good" boundary — and the thing a distributed monolith can never deliver.

Common misuse and over-engineering

DDD is a force multiplier on complex core domains and pure overhead everywhere else. The dominant failure mode is ceremony on a CRUD app: wrapping a simple admin tool or a thin data-entry form in aggregates, repositories, domain services, and an event bus when the entire "domain logic" is validate-and-save. For genuine CRUD and generic subdomains, a transaction-script or active-record style is faster to build and easier to maintain — reserve the full tactical toolkit for the core subdomain where the business rules are actually rich and changing.

Anti-patterns to avoidAnemic domain models (entities that are bags of getters/setters with all logic in services — the opposite of DDD's intent); aggregates so large they become contention hotspots and serialize unrelated writes; a single "enterprise canonical model" spanning every context; and treating DDD as a tactical-patterns checklist while skipping the strategic context mapping — the strategic half is where the real value lives.

The other common error is starting with tactical patterns. Strategic design comes first: you cannot draw correct aggregates until you know which context you are in, because the same business concept has different invariants in different contexts.

Principal-level mastery check

What strong engineers doThey justify every service boundary by naming the bounded context and its ubiquitous language — "this is the Pricing context, it owns these invariants, it integrates with Catalog via published events." They use aggregates to explain exactly where they need strong consistency and where they accept eventual consistency via sagas, and they classify subdomains (core/supporting/generic) to explain build-vs-buy. They resist premature service splits, defaulting to a modular monolith and splitting only when ownership or scaling diverges.

Probing questions to ask engineers:

  • "You've drawn five services. For each, what is the bounded context and who owns it — and where does the word 'Customer' mean different things?"
  • "Walk me through one write that must be strongly consistent and one that can be eventual. What's your aggregate, and where does a saga begin?"
  • "These two services share a database table. Why is that a problem, and how would you untangle it?"
  • "This is a basic CRUD admin panel — would you still apply aggregates and repositories here? Why or why not?"
  • "How do you keep two bounded contexts from coupling when they clearly need each other's data?"
  • "Your org has three teams but you've proposed eight microservices. How will Conway's Law fight you here?"
