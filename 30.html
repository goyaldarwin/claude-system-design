Consensus & Raft

How do a group of machines agree on one value — the next log entry, who is leader, whether a transaction committed — when any of them can crash and the network can drop or delay messages? That single problem, consensus, sits under almost every reliable distributed system. Raft is the algorithm that finally made it teachable.

The crux One agreed value across many unreliable machines — in exchange for a majority round-trip on every decision and a hard cap on how many failures you can survive. Consensus buys you a single source of truth that outlives any one node. You pay with latency (a quorum must agree before anything is committed) and with a fault ceiling: a cluster of 2f+1 nodes survives only f failures.

What consensus is

Consensus is the problem of getting a set of machines to agree on a single value, even though some may crash and the network may delay, drop, or reorder messages. "Value" is general: it can be the next entry in a replicated log, the identity of the current leader, or the outcome (commit or abort) of a transaction. Agree on a sequence of values and you have a replicated state machine — every node applies the same operations in the same order, so every node ends in the same state.

A correct consensus algorithm must guarantee three things at once:

  • Agreement — no two nodes decide different values. (This is the safety property you must never violate.)
  • Validity — the value decided was actually proposed by some node (you can't agree on garbage).
  • Termination — every non-failed node eventually decides. (This is liveness — the property that can be sacrificed temporarily without corrupting data.)
The one-line frameConsensus trades latency and a fault ceiling for a single, durable source of truth. Every decision waits for a majority; the cluster survives fewer than half its nodes failing. In return you get a value that all survivors agree on and that outlives any individual machine. It is the foundation under leader election, distributed locks, configuration stores, and atomic commit.

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide your answer first — the rest of the lesson is the answer. If you already know all three cold, skip to the numbers.

Scenario 1 — Cloudflare, 2 November 2020 (real incident) A partial switch failure put part of Cloudflare's core network into a half-broken state: some nodes could reach others but not all. Their etcd cluster — which uses Raft — could not elect a stable leader. It kept starting elections forever and committed nothing for hours. The data was safe; the cluster simply made no progress. Which of consensus's three guarantees broke, and why is that the one that's allowed to break?
Scenario 2 — the two-leader fear Your 5-node Raft cluster suffers a partition: 2 nodes on one side, 3 on the other. A new leader gets elected on the 3-node side. Meanwhile the old leader is still alive on the 2-node side and thinks it's in charge. Can both leaders accept writes and corrupt your data? What single mechanism makes that impossible?
Scenario 3 — the durability illusion A client sends a write. The leader copies it to one follower, then both crash before anyone else sees it. Later the cluster recovers from the remaining 3 nodes. Is the write still there? When exactly is a write safe to acknowledge to the client — and why isn't "I copied it to a follower" enough?

Scenario 1 is FLP and liveness — consensus can stall but never lie. Scenario 2 is terms and the majority rule. Scenario 3 is the commit point — what "committed" really means. Each maps to an idea below.

The five ideas to understand

Consensus isn't decisions to configure or sub-problems to build — it's a small set of ideas you must hold correctly, each guarded by a common misconception. Get the idea, avoid the trap.

Idea 1 — The problem, and FLP impossibility

In 1985, Fischer, Lynch and Paterson proved the FLP impossibility result: in a fully asynchronous network (no bound on message delay), no deterministic algorithm can guarantee consensus if even one node may crash. The reason is fundamental: you can never distinguish a crashed node from a merely slow one, so any algorithm can be forced to wait forever.

Real systems escape FLP not by breaking it but by relaxing the model: they add timeouts (a partial-synchrony assumption — messages usually arrive within some bound) and often a dash of randomness (Raft randomizes election timeouts so two candidates rarely clash twice). This buys termination in practice while keeping agreement always.

The misconception to avoid — "FLP means consensus is impossible" This is Scenario 1. FLP says you cannot guarantee termination (liveness) in a purely asynchronous model — it does not say you can't agree. Raft and Paxos never violate agreement (safety); under a nasty partition they may simply stop making progress, which is exactly what Cloudflare's etcd did in 2020 — infinite elections, zero committed entries, but no corrupted data. The deal consensus makes is: I may stall, but I will never lie. A system that kept serving writes during that partition would be the dangerous one.

Idea 2 — Leader election and terms

Raft makes consensus understandable by funneling everything through one elected leader. Time is divided into terms — numbered epochs, each with at most one leader. A node is a follower, a candidate, or the leader. If a follower hears nothing from a leader within its (randomized) election timeout, it bumps the term number, becomes a candidate, votes for itself, and asks the others for votes. A candidate wins only if it collects votes from a majority. Because each node votes for at most one candidate per term, and any two majorities overlap, at most one candidate can win a given term.

The misconception to avoid — "two leaders can coexist and both accept writes" This is Scenario 2. The old leader on the minority (2-node) side still thinks it's leader — but it can't get a majority to accept any new entry, so it commits nothing. The new leader has a higher term number; the moment the partition heals and the old leader sees that higher term, it immediately steps down. The majority-vote rule plus monotonic terms means writes only ever commit on one side of any partition. Two nodes can briefly believe they're leader; only one can ever commit. (A split vote — nobody gets a majority — just triggers a new election with fresh random timeouts.)

Idea 3 — Log replication and the commit point

Clients send commands to the leader. The leader appends each command to its log and sends it to all followers via AppendEntries. Here is the crucial rule: an entry is "committed" only once a majority of nodes have written it to their logs. Only then does the leader apply it to its state machine and reply to the client. The leader tracks a commit index and tells followers about it so they apply entries in the same order.

The misconception to avoid — "replicated to one follower = committed" This is Scenario 3. A write copied to only the leader + one follower (2 of 5) is not committed — if both crash, the remaining 3 form a majority that never saw it, and the entry is correctly discarded. That's not a bug; it's the guarantee. A write is durable only after a majority holds it, because then any future leader (also elected by a majority) is guaranteed to have it — the two majorities must overlap. "Committed" means "a majority has it," and you must not acknowledge the client one moment sooner.

Idea 4 — Safety and the quorum-overlap argument

Why does any of this actually work? Because any two majorities of the same set must share at least one member. A new leader is elected by a majority, and that majority must include at least one node from the majority that committed the last entry — so the new leader is guaranteed to have every committed entry. Raft layers extra rules on top (a candidate can't win unless its log is at least as up-to-date as the voter's; a leader never overwrites or deletes its own entries; a leader only commits entries from its own term directly) but the quorum overlap is the load-bearing idea.

The misconception to avoid — "more replicas always means more durable" Adding nodes raises your fault tolerance only in odd steps, and it slows every write because the majority grows. A 5-node cluster tolerates 2 failures; a 6-node cluster also tolerates only 2 (you need 4 of 6 vs 3 of 5) while being slower and more likely to lose quorum. Always run an odd number of nodes — 3 or 5 is the sweet spot. Beyond ~7, the majority round-trip dominates latency and you should shard the consensus group, not grow it.

Idea 5 — Paxos, Multi-Paxos, and where Raft came from

Paxos (Lamport, 1998) was the first proven-correct consensus algorithm, but its single-decree form decides one value and is famously hard to turn into a working system. Multi-Paxos adds a stable leader to decide a sequence of values efficiently — which is what real systems need. Raft (Ongaro & Ousterhout, 2014) was designed explicitly for understandability: it makes the leader strong, forbids gaps in the log, and separates election from replication. It is equivalent in power to Multi-Paxos, just easier to implement correctly.

The misconception to avoid — "Raft replaced Paxos" Plenty of battle-tested infrastructure still runs Paxos variants — Google's Chubby and Spanner, for instance. Raft won the teaching war and dominates new open-source systems (etcd, Consul), but "which consensus algorithm" is rarely your decision — you pick the system (etcd vs ZooKeeper) and inherit its algorithm. The thing to carry into an interview is that they all rest on the same quorum-overlap guarantee.

At a glance — cluster size and fault tolerance

The one table to memorize. A cluster of N=2f+1 nodes tolerates f failures and needs f+1 (a majority) to commit:

Nodes (N)Majority neededFailures tolerated (f)Notes
110No fault tolerance — not consensus.
321The common minimum for production.
532The sweet spot for critical clusters.
642Worse than 5 — same tolerance, slower, easier to lose quorum. Never use even N.
743Rarely worth it — the majority round-trip starts to hurt latency.
The rule in one lineRun an odd number of nodes (3 or 5). Tolerance is (N-1)/2. Every committed write costs one majority round-trip, so bigger clusters are more available to read but slower to write — which is why you scale consensus by sharding into many small groups, not by growing one group.

Self-check — can you reconstruct the three scenarios?

You read the answers above. Close the page and see if you can rebuild them.

  • Scenario 1 (Cloudflare etcd): Which of agreement / validity / termination broke during the partition, and why is that the one consensus is allowed to sacrifice? What does FLP actually prove?
  • Scenario 2 (two leaders): Why can the old leader on the minority side never corrupt data, even though it still thinks it's leader? What two mechanisms guarantee one committer per term?
  • Scenario 3 (commit point): Exactly when is a write safe to acknowledge, and why is "copied to one follower" not enough? State the quorum-overlap argument.
  • Why must you run an odd number of nodes? What does a 6-node cluster buy you over a 5-node one?
  • What is the difference between Paxos, Multi-Paxos, and Raft — and why is the choice rarely yours to make directly?

The shape to remember: consensus is 5 ideas — FLP (stall, don't lie), leader election by majority + terms, commit = majority holds it, safety from quorum overlap, Paxos/Raft are the same guarantee — each guarded by a misconception.

Famous implementations

The theory made concrete. Same facets for each — algorithm, what it stores, how clients use it, and the failure to watch.

Raft systems — etcd / Consul

  • Algorithm. Raft, exactly as described above (leader, terms, log replication, majority commit).
  • What it stores. A replicated key-value store for small, critical metadata: leader identity, cluster membership, config, distributed locks. etcd is the backing store for all of Kubernetes.
  • How clients use it. Watches (notify me when this key changes), leases (a key that expires if not renewed), compare-and-swap. These are the primitives you build leader election and locks on.
  • The failure to watch. The Cloudflare 2020 case — a partial network failure can cause endless elections and total stall. Keep the cluster small (3 or 5), low-latency, and off the critical path where a stall would take everything down.

Paxos systems — Chubby / Spanner

  • Algorithm. Multi-Paxos with a stable leader.
  • What it stores. Chubby is Google's coarse-grained lock service and small-file store; Spanner runs Paxos per shard (per "Paxos group") to replicate data across datacenters.
  • How clients use it. Chubby: acquire a lock / read a config file. Spanner: ordinary SQL — the consensus is invisible, paid for in TrueTime commit-wait latency.
  • The failure to watch. Same fault ceiling; Spanner sidesteps the "one group is slow" problem by sharding into thousands of independent Paxos groups so no single majority round-trip gates the whole database.

ZAB — ZooKeeper

  • Algorithm. ZAB (ZooKeeper Atomic Broadcast) — a consensus protocol predating Raft, similar in spirit: one leader, majority commit, totally-ordered broadcast of updates.
  • What it stores. A hierarchical namespace of small nodes (znodes) — the original coordination kernel that Kafka, HBase, and Hadoop leaned on.
  • How clients use it. Ephemeral znodes + watches — the classic recipe for leader election and membership (covered in Leader Election & Coordination).
  • The failure to watch. Same odd-cluster, majority-quorum constraints; a minority partition goes read-only.

Build it yourself

Consensus sticks when you reason it through. Work each one before opening the approach.

Size a cluster for a target fault tolerance

Your coordination store must survive any 2 node failures and you want the cheapest cluster that does. How many nodes, and what's the majority? What happens to write latency if you double that to survive 4 failures?

Reveal the approach

Tolerating f=2 needs N=2f+1=5 nodes, majority = 3. To survive f=4 you need N=9, majority = 5 — every commit now waits for the slowest of 5 nodes instead of 3, and you've nearly doubled the gossip and replication traffic. The lesson: fault tolerance grows linearly in nodes but write latency and coordination cost grow with the majority size, so production almost never goes past 5 in one group — it shards into many 3- or 5-node groups instead (see Spanner's per-shard Paxos).

Trace an election through a partition

A 5-node cluster (terms currently at 7) partitions into {A,B} and {C,D,E}, where A was leader. Walk through what each side does, what term numbers result, and what happens when the partition heals.

Reveal the approach

On {A,B}: A stays leader at term 7 but can never reach a 3-node majority, so it commits nothing — client writes time out. On {C,D,E}: they stop hearing A, time out, and one (say C) bumps the term to 8, campaigns, and wins 3 votes — a valid majority. C now commits writes at term 8. On heal: A receives a message with term 8 > 7, recognizes a newer term, and immediately steps down to follower, discarding any uncommitted term-7 entries that conflict with C's log. No data committed on the majority side is ever lost; nothing committed on the minority side ever existed. One committer per term, guaranteed.

Design-it-yourself: the open-ended ones

No single right answer — reason it out, then follow the link.

  • Build leader election on a consensus store. Using only "create-if-not-exists" + an expiring lease + a watch, design leader election so exactly one node leads and a new one takes over within seconds of a crash. → Leader Election & Coordination.
  • Make the stall tolerable. Your metadata store can go unavailable on the minority side during a partition (by design). Architect the surrounding system so a consensus stall degrades gracefully instead of taking the product down. → Resilience Patterns, CAP Theorem.
  • Scale consensus past one group. A single Raft group can't handle your write volume. Design a sharding scheme (many independent groups) and decide what crosses group boundaries — and what that costs you. → Sharding, Distributed Transactions.
