Architectural Styles & How to Choose

Monolith, microservices, event-driven, serverless, batch, stream — the named architectural styles aren't a menu where newer is better; each answers a specific question about how components are split and how they communicate. The principal skill is matching the style to your team size, scale, latency needs, and how the work actually flows — and resisting the gravity toward the trendy option that doesn't fit.

The trade-off An architecture that matches your real constraints — in exchange for resisting the default pull toward whatever's fashionable, and accepting that every style trades simplicity for some flavor of scalability/decoupling. Pick the style your team size and workload actually need and you ship faster and operate more cheaply. The cost is discipline: the trendy choice (microservices, serverless) is usually wrong for small teams, and each step toward decoupling adds distributed-systems complexity.

What an architectural style is

An architectural style is a reusable answer to two questions: how do you split the system into components, and how do those components communicate? That's it. The named styles are points in that space:

  • Monolith — one deployable, in-process calls. Client–server — the basic request/response split.
  • SOA / Microservices — split into services that talk over the network (SOA coarser + an ESB; microservices finer + independent).
  • Event-driven / Pub-Sub — components react to events instead of calling each other directly (async, decoupled).
  • Stream / Batch processing — organized around data flow: continuous (stream) or bounded-and-complete (batch).
  • Serverless / FaaS — functions the platform runs on demand; no servers to manage.
The one-line frameChoosing a style trades simplicity for some specific gain (independent deployment, decoupling, data-flow fit, elastic scale) — and the right choice is dictated by your constraints, not by what's newest. Newer/more-distributed is not better; it's more complex, and only worth it when your team size, scale, or workload shape actually demands it.

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide first. If you know all three cold, skip to the comparison.

Scenario 1 — the 5-engineer startup that "wants microservices" A 5-person team building an MVP insists on microservices "so it'll scale." Six months later they're spending most of their time operating service mesh, debugging cross-service calls, and managing distributed data — instead of building product. What style should they have started with, and what actually drives the move to microservices?
Scenario 2 — Amazon Prime Video collapses serverless to a monolith (real case) Prime Video's video-monitoring pipeline was built serverless (Step Functions + Lambda + S3), hit a hard scaling limit at ~5% of target load, and cost a fortune. They moved it into a single long-running process and cut cost ~90%. Why was serverless the wrong style for that workload, and which dimension should have caught it?
Scenario 3 — the order pipeline drowning in synchronous calls "Place order" makes 8 synchronous service-to-service calls in sequence; one slow service stalls checkout, and adding a new reactor (fraud check, analytics) means modifying the order service every time. What architectural style decouples these so reactors can be added freely and a slow one can't block checkout?

Scenario 1 is team size. Scenario 2 is workload shape + scale/cost. Scenario 3 is sync vs async (event-driven). Each maps to a dimension.

The decision dimensions, resolved in order

Don't start from "which style is cool" — resolve these dimensions in order: eliminate on team size, narrow on communication shape and workload, tie-break on scale and ops appetite.

Dimension 1 — Team & org size (eliminate first)

The most decisive and most-ignored factor. Microservices solve an organizational problem — many teams contending on one codebase — at the cost of distributed-systems complexity. So: few teams → monolith (or modular monolith); many autonomous teams that need to deploy independently → microservices. This single question eliminates whole branches before you consider anything else. (Conway's law: your architecture will mirror your org anyway.)

Dimension 2 — Communication shape: sync or async? (narrow)

Do components need an immediate answer (synchronous request/response — client-server, REST/gRPC between services) or can they react eventually to things that happened (asynchronous events — event-driven / pub-sub)? Async/event-driven decouples producers from consumers in time and identity (add a reactor without touching the producer; absorb bursts) at the cost of eventual consistency and harder debugging. Use sync for genuine request/response, async/events for workflows that tolerate eventual consistency.

Dimension 3 — Workload: request, event, or data-flow? (narrow)

What shape is the work? Request-driven (a user waits for a response) → client-server / microservices. Event-driven (react to things happening) → event-driven / pub-sub. Data-flow → stream processing (continuous, unbounded, low-latency) or batch processing (bounded, complete, exact, latency-tolerant). Matching the style to the workload's natural shape is most of the decision — a data-heavy analytics job wants batch/stream, not a request/response service.

Dimension 4 — Scale & operational appetite (tie-break)

Once the above narrows it, tie-break on scale and how much operational complexity you can run. Serverless for spiky/event-driven/low-steady-state work with small ops appetite (scales to zero, no servers) — but watch the cold-start + cost cliff at high steady load. Microservices/Kubernetes when you have the scale and team to justify the operational surface. Monolith on a PaaS when you want minimal ops. The question: can your team actually operate the style you're choosing?

The failure — cargo-culting the trendy style

The mistake that dominates real architecture decisions The recurring failure (Scenarios 1 & 2) is choosing a style by fashion or résumé, not by constraints: microservices for a tiny team (all the distributed-systems pain, none of the team-autonomy benefit — a distributed monolith), or serverless for a sustained high-throughput workload (the cost cliff — exactly what bit Prime Video). The discipline: start with the simplest style that fits (usually a monolith), and move toward more-distributed styles only when a specific dimension forces it — team size you've actually reached, scale you actually have, a workload shape that genuinely doesn't fit. "We might need to scale someday" is not a reason; it's premature complexity. Newer is not better — appropriate is better.

Famous implementations — the style-by-style guide

Each style as a concrete option. Same facets — what it is, when to reach for it, the failure to watch.

Monolith / modular monolith

  • What it is. One deployable; the modular variant enforces clean internal module boundaries.
  • Reach for it when. Few teams, early product, or any time you don't have a concrete reason to distribute — the right default.
  • Failure to watch. At large team scale, coordination on one codebase slows everyone — that's the signal to extract services (via strangler-fig).

SOA / Microservices

  • What it is. System split into network services; microservices = fine-grained, independently deployable, DB-per-service.
  • Reach for it when. Many autonomous teams need independent deployment + scaling; you have the ops maturity.
  • Failure to watch. Distributed monolith (wrong boundaries / shared DB), premature adoption, cascading failures — see Microservices.

Event-driven / pub-sub / stream / batch

  • What it is. Async, data-flow-oriented styles — event-driven (react to events), stream (continuous), batch (bounded/complete).
  • Reach for it when. Workflows tolerate eventual consistency (event-driven), or the work is fundamentally data-flow (stream for real-time, batch for exact/historical).
  • Failure to watch. Eventual-consistency surprises, poison messages, Lambda dual-codebase drift — match to workload shape.

Serverless / FaaS

  • What it is. Functions run on demand; scale to zero, no servers.
  • Reach for it when. Spiky, event-driven, low-steady-state work or glue; small ops appetite.
  • Failure to watch. Cold starts + the cost cliff at sustained high throughput (Prime Video) — wrong for steady heavy workloads.

Build it yourself

Work each before opening the approach.

Pick a style for three systems

Choose an architectural style for: (a) a 4-person startup MVP, (b) a real-time fraud-scoring pipeline, (c) a 50-team enterprise platform. Justify each via the dimensions.

Reveal the approach

(a) 4-person MVP: modular monolith on a PaaS — dimension 1 (team size) eliminates microservices immediately; ship product, keep clean module boundaries so you can extract services later if the team grows. (b) Real-time fraud scoring: stream processing (+ event-driven ingestion) — dimension 3 (workload = continuous data-flow needing low latency) dictates it; a request/response service or batch job is the wrong shape. (c) 50-team enterprise: microservices (likely on Kubernetes) — dimension 1 (many autonomous teams) plus dimension 4 (scale + ops maturity) justify the distributed complexity; you've genuinely outgrown a monolith. The method: resolve team size first (it eliminates), then workload shape (it narrows), then scale/ops (it tie-breaks) — never start from the trendy answer.

Resist a premature-microservices push

Your team (8 engineers, one product) is told by leadership to "go microservices for scale." Build the argument for the right call and what would change your mind.

Reveal the approach

Frame it on dimension 1: microservices solve a team-coordination problem, and with one 8-person team you don't have that problem yet — so you'd pay all the distributed-systems cost (network calls, DB-per-service, distributed transactions, mesh, observability across services) for none of the benefit, slowing the very velocity leadership wants. Recommend a modular monolith: clean module boundaries (each module owns its data, talks via interfaces) so it's cheap to extract services later via strangler-fig when a real seam appears. What would change your mind: the team growing to several independent teams stepping on each other, a module needing radically different scaling/tech, or a genuine independent-deploy requirement — concrete, present pressures, not "we might scale someday." The honest move is to defer complexity until a dimension actually forces it, while keeping the door open. (Mirror of the premature-decomposition failure.)

Design-it-yourself: the open-ended ones

  • Migrate a monolith incrementally. Your monolith genuinely outgrew its team. Plan the move to microservices without a big-bang rewrite. → Microservices (strangler-fig).
  • Mix styles in one system. Most real systems combine styles (sync APIs + event-driven workflows + batch analytics). Design which parts use which and why. → Event-Driven Architecture.
  • Right-size the substrate. For a chosen style, pick VM / container-PaaS / K8s / serverless by traffic shape and ops appetite. → Containers/K8s/Serverless.
