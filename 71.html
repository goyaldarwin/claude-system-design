Cost-Aware System Design

At scale, the cloud bill is a design output, not an accounting afterthought — and the costs that blow budgets are rarely the obvious compute. They're the silent ones: cross-region data transfer, every extra "nine" of reliability, keeping all data hot forever, and serverless that's cheap at low volume and ruinous at high. Treating cost as a first-class design dimension — a number you estimate and optimize like latency — is a principal-level skill.

The trade-off A system that's cheap to run at scale — in exchange for treating cost as a design constraint from the start and accepting the cost/latency/reliability trade-offs explicitly. Designing with cost in mind keeps the bill sane as you grow. You pay by making cost a number you reason about up front (not after the invoice), and by deliberately trading some performance/reliability/freshness for money where it's worth it.

Why cost is a design dimension

Performance, scalability, and reliability are obviously design concerns — but cost is the fourth, and at scale it's just as architectural. The same functional requirement can be met by designs whose bills differ by 10× or 100×, and the difference is design choices: which compute model, how you tier storage, how data moves between regions, how many nines you buy, what you precompute. The principal-level move is to estimate cost like you estimate latency — up front, as part of the design — and to know which levers actually matter, because the budget-killers are usually not the line item you'd expect.

The FinOps mindset crystallizes this: treat cost as an SLI you measure, attribute, and optimize — cost-per-request, cost-per-tenant — not a quarterly surprise.

The one-line frameCost-aware design trades some performance/reliability/freshness for money, deliberately, and trades upfront cost-estimation effort for a bill that scales sanely. Every reliability nine, every hot byte, every cross-region transfer, every serverless invocation has a price — design is choosing which to pay. The skill is making those trades on purpose, with numbers, before the invoice forces the conversation.

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide first. If you know all three cold, skip to where the money goes.

Scenario 1 — Amazon Prime Video's serverless bill (real case) Prime Video's video-monitoring pipeline ran serverless (Step Functions + Lambda + S3). It was fine at low volume, but at scale the per-invocation and state-transition charges plus S3 calls became enormous — they moved it to a single long-running process and cut cost ~90%. Scenario 2 — the data-transfer bill nobody saw coming A team architects a system spanning two regions (and pulls data out to the internet for analytics). Compute and storage costs look reasonable, but the monthly bill is dominated by a line item they never estimated: data transfer / egress. Why is cross-region and internet egress so often the silent budget-killer, and how do you design around it?
Scenario 3 — paying for five nines on a service that needs three An internal tool is architected for 99.999% availability — multi-region active-active, synchronous replication, the works — at enormous cost, when the business would be perfectly happy with 99.9%. Why does each additional "nine" cost so much more, and how do you decide how many to buy?

Scenario 1 is the compute model. Scenario 2 is data transfer. Scenario 3 is the cost of each nine. Each maps to a lever.

The five cost levers (and their traps)

Five design levers dominate the bill. Each has a trap that quietly inflates cost — nested under it.

Lever 1 — The compute model

How you run code has very different cost curves: serverless (pay per invocation + compute-time — cheap when idle/spiky, scales to zero), containers/VMs (pay for provisioned capacity — cheap when busy and steady), reserved/spot (commit or use spare capacity for big discounts on predictable/interruptible workloads). The right model depends on the traffic shape — and getting it wrong is one of the most common cost mistakes.

The trap — the serverless cost cliff This is Scenario 1. Serverless bills per invocation and per unit compute-time, which is a fantastic deal for spiky, low-utilization, event-driven work (you pay nothing when idle). But for a high-throughput, always-on workload, those per-invocation charges — and per-step charges in orchestrators like Step Functions, plus per-call costs to services like S3 — accumulate past what a steadily-utilized container or VM would cost, often by a large multiple (Prime Video: ~90% cheaper on a long-running process). The lever: match the compute model to utilization — serverless for spiky/idle-heavy, containers/reserved for steady high load, spot for interruptible batch. The cliff is real and counter-intuitive: serverless is cheapest at low/spiky volume and most expensive at high steady volume. (See Containers/K8s/Serverless.)

Lever 2 — Storage tiers

Storage isn't one price — cloud providers offer tiers from hot (frequent access, fast, expensive) through warm/infrequent-access to cold/archival (Glacier — very cheap to store, slow/costly to retrieve). Lifecycle policies automatically move data to cheaper tiers as it ages. Matching each dataset's tier to its actual access pattern can cut storage cost dramatically.

The trap — keeping everything hot forever Leaving all data on the hot/standard tier indefinitely — logs from two years ago, backups never read, raw data already aggregated — means paying premium per-GB prices for data nobody touches. The fix: lifecycle policies that transition data to infrequent-access after weeks and archival after months (and delete what has no retention requirement), matching tier to access frequency. The trap also runs the other way — putting data you do read often into archival incurs steep retrieval fees and latency. The lever is access-pattern-aware tiering: hot for what's read constantly, cold for what's kept "just in case." Most organizations are over-paying because old data silently sits hot. (Related: Object Storage storage classes, metrics downsampling, observability retention.)

Lever 3 — Data transfer (egress)

Moving bytes costs money in ways that surprise people: egress to the internet is charged per GB, cross-region transfer is charged (often heavily), and even cross-AZ transfer within a region has a cost. Compute and storage are visible and estimated; data transfer is the line item teams routinely forget — and it can dominate the bill.

The trap — the silent egress killer This is Scenario 2. Data-transfer/egress charges are the classic silent budget-killer because they don't show up in the obvious "compute + storage" estimate — yet a chatty cross-region architecture, serving large media directly from origin, or pulling big datasets out to the internet can make transfer the largest line item. Design to minimize it: keep data and the compute that uses it in the same region/AZ (co-locate to avoid cross-region/cross-AZ transfer), use a CDN so user traffic is served from the edge (cheaper + offloads origin egress), cache aggressively to avoid re-fetching across boundaries, and be deliberate about which data crosses regions (often you can replicate derived/aggregated data instead of raw). Always include data transfer in the cost estimate — the bytes-in-motion bill is the one that ambushes teams.

Lever 4 — The cost of each "nine"

Reliability isn't free, and it's exponentially priced: going from 99% to 99.9% to 99.99% to 99.999% each requires more redundancy, more multi-region infrastructure, more replication, more operational sophistication — for shrinking user-perceptible benefit. The SLO decision is therefore also a cost decision.

The trap — over-buying reliability This is Scenario 3. Each additional nine roughly multiplies cost (more replicas, multi-region active-active, synchronous cross-region replication, 24/7 ops) while delivering diminishing — often imperceptible — user value. Architecting an internal tool for five nines is pouring money into reliability nobody needs. The lever, straight from SLOs: set the target at the fewest nines the business actually requires (a user on a flaky network can't tell 99.9% from 99.99%), and spend the saved budget elsewhere. This connects directly to other levers — e.g. consistency as a cost choice (strong consistency requires coordination = latency + infrastructure; relax it where the data tolerates it to save money), and replication factor vs durability (more replicas = more durability + more storage/transfer cost; pick N by what the data actually needs). Buy the reliability you need, not the maximum.

Lever 5 — Precompute vs compute-on-read

For derived data (aggregates, recommendations, rendered pages), you can precompute it (compute once on write/batch, store it, read cheaply) or compute on read (compute each time it's requested). This is a cost trade-off between storage/compute-now and compute-later, and the right choice depends on the read/write ratio.

The trap — paying for the wrong side of the trade Picking the wrong side wastes money. Compute-on-read for something read constantly but rarely changing (e.g. recomputing an expensive aggregate on every dashboard load) burns compute over and over for the same answer — precompute it once and serve cheap reads (the fan-out-on-write idea, the rollup). Conversely, precomputing something rarely read but frequently changing wastes compute + storage producing results nobody asks for — compute it on read instead. The lever is the read/write (or read/change) ratio: read-heavy + stable → precompute and cache; write-heavy + rarely-read → compute on demand. Caching is the same lever (trade storage for repeated compute). Match the strategy to the ratio, and you stop paying to compute the same thing a million times or to precompute things nobody reads.

The takeawayThe cloud gives you the levers as features — commitment discounts (reserved/spot), storage lifecycle automation, CDNs and private networking to cut egress, and cost-attribution tooling. The FinOps practice is to tag everything (so you can attribute cost per service/tenant/request — making it an SLI) and build cost into the design review, not the post-mortem. The platform won't right-size your architecture; the levers above will.

Famous implementations — FinOps in practice

Cost-awareness as real practice. Same facets — what it is, how it works, the pitfall.

Right-sizing the compute model (Prime Video)

  • What it is. Choosing the compute model by utilization — the Prime Video monolith-from-serverless being the famous example.
  • How it works. Profile the workload's traffic shape; serverless for spiky/idle, steady-utilization containers/reserved for sustained load, spot for interruptible batch.
  • The pitfall. The serverless cliff — assuming serverless is always cheapest; it's cheapest at low/spiky volume, not high steady volume.

Storage tiering & lifecycle

  • What it is. Automatically moving data through hot → warm → cold tiers by age/access.
  • How it works. Lifecycle policies transition (and expire) objects; analytics/observability data is also downsampled as it ages to shrink volume.
  • The pitfall. Leaving everything hot (over-paying) or archiving frequently-read data (retrieval fees + latency).

FinOps: cost as an SLI

  • What it is. The organizational practice of treating cost as a first-class, measured, attributed metric.
  • How it works. Tag resources for attribution; track cost-per-request / per-tenant; set cost budgets/alerts; include cost in design reviews; iterate like any SLO.
  • The pitfall. Treating cost as a quarterly finance problem instead of an engineering design dimension — the bill surprises you because no one owned it.

Build it yourself

Work each before opening the approach.

Find the serverless break-even point

A workload runs at a steady 1,000 requests/sec, each ~100ms of compute. Estimate roughly whether serverless or a container fleet is cheaper, and where the break-even is.

Reveal the approach

Reason about utilization. Serverless: 1,000 req/s × 100ms = 100 compute-seconds/sec of work = ~100 concurrent executions continuously, billed per-invocation + per-GB-second the whole time — you're paying serverless's premium per-unit rate at essentially 100% duty cycle, 24/7. Containers: ~100 concurrent 100ms tasks ≈ a handful of well-utilized container instances running continuously, billed at the (much lower) provisioned per-hour rate. At steady high utilization like this, containers win clearly — serverless's value (pay-nothing-when-idle) is wasted because you're never idle. The break-even is roughly where utilization drops enough that the idle savings outweigh serverless's higher per-unit rate: spiky/bursty or low-average-utilization workloads (e.g. a few req/s with peaks, or jobs that run minutes per hour) favor serverless; steady high-throughput favors containers (and reserved/spot for further discount). The lesson: estimate duty cycle, not just peak — that's what flips the answer (Scenario 1).

Cut a cross-region egress bill

Your bill is dominated by cross-region data transfer because services in region A constantly read data stored in region B, and you serve large media from origin. Redesign to cut the transfer cost.

Reveal the approach

Attack the bytes-in-motion. (1) Co-locate compute with data: the chatty cross-region reads are the killer — either move the compute to region B (next to the data) or replicate the needed data into region A so reads are local (cross-region replication of the dataset once is far cheaper than reading it across regions on every request). Prefer replicating derived/aggregated data rather than raw if you can. (2) Serve media via a CDN: large media served from origin incurs origin egress on every download; a CDN caches at the edge so most downloads are served from the edge (cheaper egress + offloads origin). (3) Cache across boundaries so you don't re-fetch the same cross-region data repeatedly. (4) Use private networking (VPC endpoints / Private Link) where it reduces transfer cost vs going over the internet. And going forward, always include data transfer in the cost estimate — the fix is mostly architectural (co-location + CDN + caching), and the root cause was not estimating egress up front (Scenario 2).

Design-it-yourself: the open-ended ones

  • Set cost-appropriate SLOs. For three services (payments, internal tool, marketing site) pick the number of nines and justify the cost trade. → SLI/SLO/SLA.
  • Make cost an SLI. Design the tagging + attribution so you can track and alert on cost-per-tenant, and bake cost into design reviews. → Observability.
  • Optimize a data pipeline's cost. Apply storage tiering, columnar formats, and precompute-vs-on-read to cut a daily analytics pipeline's bill. → Batch & Big Data, Object Storage.
