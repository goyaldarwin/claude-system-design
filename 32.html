Distributed Transactions

How to make multiple services, shards, or databases agree on a single all-or-nothing outcome — and why the best answer is often "don't."

The bottleneck No shared truth & concurrency. atomicity across machines, paid in blocking (2PC) or compensations (saga).

The atomic commit problem

A local ACID transaction is easy: one storage engine, one log, one fsync, and the database guarantees that a set of writes either all become durable together or none do. The problem starts the moment the writes span two independent failure domains — two database shards, two microservices each owning its own datastore, or a database plus a message broker. Now there is no single log and no single party that can unilaterally decide the outcome.

The classic example: transfer $100 from account A (in service/shard X) to account B (in service/shard Y). You must debit X and credit Y atomically. If the process crashes between the two writes, you have either created or destroyed money. Multiply this by "place order, reserve inventory, charge card, allocate shipping" and you have the canonical distributed-transaction problem.

Three properties are in tension, and you can never get all of them at once across independent nodes:

  • Atomicity — all participants commit or all abort.
  • Isolation — concurrent transactions don't see each other's partial state.
  • Availability — the system keeps making progress despite node and network failures.
Key insight Distributed atomic commit is a consensus problem in disguise — every participant must agree on a single bit (commit or abort). That immediately ties it to the CAP theorem: under a partition you must sacrifice availability (block) or atomicity (proceed and reconcile later). 2PC chooses to block; Sagas choose to proceed and compensate.

Two-Phase Commit (2PC)

2PC is the textbook protocol for atomic commit across participants. One node acts as the coordinator (transaction manager); the others are participants (resource managers). It runs in two round trips:

Phase 1 — PREPARE (voting)
  coordinator -> all participants: PREPARE
  each participant:
      do the work, write it to a durable redo/undo log,
      acquire and HOLD all locks,
      reply VOTE_COMMIT (or VOTE_ABORT if it cannot)
  // after voting COMMIT a participant is in the "prepared"
  // (in-doubt) state: it has PROMISED it can commit and may
  // no longer abort unilaterally.

Phase 2 — COMMIT / ABORT (decision)
  if all votes == COMMIT:
      coordinator durably logs "COMMIT"   // <-- commit point
      coordinator -> all: COMMIT
  else:
      coordinator -> all: ABORT
  each participant: apply or roll back, release locks, ACK

The critical detail is the commit point: once the coordinator writes COMMIT to its own log, the decision is final and durable, even across coordinator restarts. Participants that voted COMMIT are bound to honor it.

The blocking problem

2PC's fatal flaw is what happens when the coordinator crashes after participants have voted COMMIT but before they receive the decision. Each participant is now in-doubt: it has promised to commit, is holding locks, and cannot safely abort (the coordinator may have already told another participant to commit) nor commit (the decision may have been ABORT). It must block — holding its locks — until the coordinator recovers.

Pitfall 2PC is not partition tolerant. A coordinator failure or a network partition during the in-doubt window stalls every prepared participant, and those held locks cascade into latency spikes and lock-wait timeouts across unrelated transactions. In a microservices mesh this turns a single slow node into a system-wide brownout. This is why 2PC is acceptable inside a single datacenter across DB shards but is widely considered an anti-pattern across service boundaries.

Production 2PC (XA transactions, the X/Open standard implemented by JTA, MSDTC, and many RDBMS/JMS brokers) mitigates but never eliminates this: a recovery process reconnects in-doubt branches to the coordinator's recovery log, and operators get manual "heuristic commit/rollback" levers as a last resort — which can silently violate atomicity. Coordinator durability and recovery is itself a hard problem; production-grade coordinators are typically replicated via consensus (Raft/Paxos) so the commit log survives a node loss. Google's Spanner essentially runs 2PC over Paxos groups, using TrueTime to bound clock uncertainty (see Time, Clocks and Ordering).

Property2PC verdict
AtomicityStrong — true all-or-nothing
IsolationStrong — locks held across both phases
Availability under partitionPoor — participants block while in-doubt
Latency2 RTTs + multiple fsyncs; locks held the whole time
CouplingHigh — all participants must be up to commit

Three-Phase Commit and why nobody uses it

3PC tries to make 2PC non-blocking by inserting a PRE-COMMIT phase between PREPARE and COMMIT. The idea: before anyone actually commits, all participants first acknowledge that they know the decision is COMMIT. If the coordinator dies, a recovered participant can infer the outcome from whether it (or its peers) reached the pre-commit state, and elect a new coordinator to finish — instead of blocking.

Phase 1: CAN-COMMIT?   -> votes
Phase 2: PRE-COMMIT    -> "everyone agreed, get ready"  (ACK)
Phase 3: DO-COMMIT     -> actually commit
Trade-off 3PC is non-blocking only under the synchronous model with fail-stop crashes and bounded message delay. The moment you admit network partitions (the real world), 3PC is unsafe: a partition can split nodes into groups that independently reach contradictory decisions, producing a split-brain commit. So 3PC trades a real safety guarantee for liveness that doesn't hold under realistic faults. The extra round trip also makes it slower.

In practice nobody ships 3PC. The modern answer to "make commit fault-tolerant without blocking" is not 3PC — it is to run the commit decision through a proper consensus protocol (Raft/Paxos/Multi-Paxos), which is non-blocking with a majority quorum and provably safe under partitions. If you find yourself reinventing 3PC in practice, the strong move is to say "I'd put the commit record behind a Raft group instead."

The Saga pattern

A Saga abandons cross-service atomicity entirely. Instead of one global transaction, it is a sequence of local transactions, each in its own service, where every step has a corresponding compensating transaction that semantically undoes it. If step N fails, you run the compensations for steps N-1 … 1 in reverse. This is the dominant pattern for long-running business workflows in microservices, and it pairs naturally with Event-Driven Architecture.

Order Saga (happy path):
  T1: OrderService    create order (PENDING)
  T2: PaymentService  charge card
  T3: InventoryService reserve stock
  T4: ShippingService schedule delivery
  T5: OrderService    mark order CONFIRMED

If T3 fails:
  C2: PaymentService  refund / void authorization
  C1: OrderService    mark order CANCELLED
  // note: there is NO "uncharge" — compensation is a NEW
  // business transaction (a refund), not a rollback.

Choreography vs orchestration

  • Choreography — no central brain. Each service emits domain events; downstream services subscribe and react, emitting their own events. Decentralized and loosely coupled, but the workflow logic is smeared across services — there's no single place to see "what is this saga doing right now," and cyclic event dependencies are easy to create accidentally. Works well for 2–3 steps.
  • Orchestration — a dedicated saga orchestrator (often a state machine: AWS Step Functions, Temporal, Camunda, Netflix Conductor) explicitly drives each step and issues compensations on failure. Centralizes the workflow, makes it observable and testable, at the cost of a component that must itself be made durable and HA. Preferred for anything beyond a few steps.

The hard part: isolation anomalies

Sagas give you atomicity (eventually, via compensation) but no isolation. Because each local transaction commits immediately, other transactions can observe intermediate state. The well-known anomalies (from Garcia-Molina & Salem's original 1987 saga paper, and Richardson's Microservices Patterns):

  • Lost update — a saga reads data that a second saga then overwrites before the first finishes.
  • Dirty read — another transaction reads the uncommitted (will-be-compensated) state, e.g. reads a balance that's about to be refunded.
  • Fuzzy / non-repeatable read — the saga reads the same row twice and sees different values across its steps.

Countermeasures (you raise these to show you understand the consequences, not just the pattern):

  • Semantic lock — a saga sets an application-level flag (order.state = PENDING, account.status = AUTH_HOLD) that signals "in flight." Other transactions either wait or take a defined path. The compensation/completion clears the lock. This is a soft lock you implement, not a DB lock.
  • Commutative updates — design operations so order doesn't matter; e.g. use balance += delta / balance -= delta rather than balance = X. Then a debit and its compensating credit commute and the net result is correct regardless of interleaving (related to CRDTs).
  • Pessimistic view ordering — reorder saga steps so the step most likely to need rollback runs first, shrinking the window where compensation is needed.
  • Reread value / version check — before committing, re-read and verify the data hasn't changed (optimistic concurrency on a version column).
  • By-value handling — route high-risk transactions (large transfers) through 2PC and let low-risk ones use a saga; a hybrid by business value.
Pitfall Not everything can be compensated. You cannot un-send an email, un-ship a package, or un-fire a missile. The strong design move is to put retriable/pivot/compensatable steps in the right order: do all compensatable steps first, then the single pivot step (the point of no return — e.g. "capture payment"), then only retriable steps after it (steps that must eventually succeed and so are retried, never compensated). The pivot defines the commit point of the whole saga.

TCC: Try-Confirm-Cancel

TCC is a structured saga where each participant exposes three idempotent operations and the workflow runs in two phases — making it look like an application-level 2PC without holding database locks:

  • Try — reserve resources / put a hold. Don't commit the business effect yet, just guarantee you can. (e.g. authorize the card and place a hold; reserve inventory into a "reserved" bucket.)
  • Confirm — make the effect permanent using only the resources reserved in Try. Must be idempotent and must not fail for business reasons (the Try already validated).
  • Cancel — release the reservation if any participant's Try failed.
Phase 1 (Try all):
   payment.try(reserve $100 hold)       -> OK
   inventory.try(reserve 1 unit)        -> OK
Phase 2:
   all Try OK?  -> payment.confirm(); inventory.confirm()
   any Try failed? -> payment.cancel(); inventory.cancel()
Trade-off TCC gives you 2PC-like coordination semantics while avoiding long-held DB locks (the "lock" is a business-level reservation in your own data model, e.g. an auth_hold row). The cost: you must design and implement Try/Confirm/Cancel for every resource, all idempotent, plus a coordinator that times out abandoned Tries and a background sweeper that cancels orphaned reservations. It's a real engineering investment — justified for payments/booking, overkill for most CRUD. This is the model behind reservation systems and the core of many payment systems.

Outbox + CDC and idempotency

The most common "distributed transaction" in practice is the dual-write problem: a service must update its database and publish an event/message (to Kafka, SQS, etc.) describing that change. These are two systems; if you write to the DB and then crash before publishing, downstream consumers never learn — and if you publish first and the DB commit fails, you've lied. Wrapping both in a transaction is exactly the 2PC trap.

The Transactional Outbox pattern solves this with a single local transaction:

BEGIN;
  INSERT INTO orders (...);                    -- the business write
  INSERT INTO outbox (id, aggregate, payload,  -- the event, SAME txn
                      created_at, published=false);
COMMIT;                                         -- atomic: both or neither

-- A separate relay/CDC process reads the outbox and publishes:
--   (a) Polling publisher: SELECT ... WHERE published=false, send, mark
--   (b) Log-tailing via CDC: Debezium tails the DB's WAL/binlog and
--       streams the outbox inserts to Kafka — no polling, low latency

Because the business row and the outbox row are written in the same local ACID transaction, they are atomic. The relay then guarantees the message is published at least once. Log-based CDC (Change Data Capture) via Debezium tailing the WAL/binlog is the production-grade relay — see Stream Processing and CDC and Kafka and Log-based Messaging.

Key insight The outbox gives you at-least-once delivery, never exactly-once. The relay can crash after publishing but before marking the row processed, so duplicates are inevitable. "Exactly-once-ish" is achieved by making consumers idempotent, not by trying to make delivery exactly-once. Effectively-once = at-least-once delivery + idempotent processing.

Idempotency keys

Every message carries a stable unique key (the outbox row id, or a client-supplied Idempotency-Key header on the originating request). Consumers (and external APIs like Stripe's charge endpoint) record processed keys and dedupe:

def handle(msg):
    if seen.contains(msg.idempotency_key):   # processed-keys table
        return ack()                          # duplicate, no-op
    with local_txn():
        apply(msg)                            # business effect
        seen.insert(msg.idempotency_key)      # SAME txn -> atomic dedupe
    ack()

The dedupe insert and the business write must be in the same local transaction, or you reintroduce a dual-write problem at the consumer. Pair with consumer offset management (Kafka) and a retention/TTL on the processed-keys table to bound its growth.

When to avoid distributed transactions entirely

The most senior instinct is to question whether the distributed transaction needs to exist at all. Most "we need a distributed transaction" situations are actually a service or data boundary drawn in the wrong place.

  • Co-locate the data. If two pieces of state must change atomically and frequently, they probably belong in the same aggregate/service/shard, where a single local ACID transaction suffices. Don't split an invariant across a network boundary. (See Sharding — choosing a shard key so related entities co-reside.)
  • Embrace eventual consistency. Many invariants don't need to be enforced synchronously. "The order total should equal the sum of line items" can be reconciled by an async process. Push for the weaker consistency model that the business actually requires.
  • Use a saga + reconciliation job. Run the happy path optimistically; a periodic reconciliation/sweeper job detects and repairs stragglers (refund stuck holds, cancel abandoned orders). This is how real payment and booking systems stay correct.
  • Single-shard transactions. If you must keep separate stores, design access so the common case touches one shard; reserve cross-shard 2PC for the rare path and accept its cost there only.
Trade-off 2PC buys you strong atomicity + isolation at the price of availability and tight coupling. Sagas/TCC buy you availability and loose coupling at the price of isolation and a lot of compensation/idempotency engineering. There is no free lunch — the question is always which consistency the business invariant truly requires, and at what blast radius you can tolerate failure.

Principal-level mastery check

What strong engineers do They treat "atomic commit across services" as a consensus problem and immediately surface the blocking/availability cost of 2PC rather than reaching for it reflexively. They default to sagas with idempotent steps and the outbox pattern for cross-service workflows, and they name the isolation anomalies sagas introduce and the specific countermeasures (semantic locks, commutative updates, pivot-step ordering). Most importantly, they challenge the premise — asking whether the transaction boundary is wrong and the data should be co-located, or whether eventual consistency plus a reconciliation job is sufficient. They never hand-wave "exactly-once"; they say at-least-once delivery plus idempotent consumers.
  • Walk me through exactly what happens to a participant when the 2PC coordinator crashes after it has voted COMMIT. What is it allowed to do, and what is it forced to do? How long does it hold its locks?
  • Why is 3PC not used in practice? Under what failure model does its non-blocking guarantee break, and what would you use instead?
  • Design the "place order → charge → reserve inventory → ship" flow as a saga. Where is the pivot step? Which steps are compensatable vs retriable, and in what order do they run? What can't be compensated?
  • Your saga reads an account balance that another in-flight saga is about to refund — a dirty read. Show me two different countermeasures and their costs.
  • A service must update its DB and publish a Kafka event. Why can't you just wrap both in a transaction, and how does the outbox pattern fix it? Where do duplicates still come from, and how do you make the consumer correct?
  • When would you tell a team that they don't need a distributed transaction at all? Give me a concrete example of a boundary you'd redraw.
