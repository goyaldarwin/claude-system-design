Distributed Transactions

One transaction must commit on several machines — debit one database, credit another, all-or-nothing — even though any participant can crash mid-commit. There's no shared clock and no shared memory to make it atomic. This is the atomic-commit problem, and every solution trades a different guarantee for a different kind of pain.

The trade-off Atomicity across independent systems — in exchange for either blocking (2PC) or giving up isolation and embracing compensation (Sagas). You cannot have a clean, non-blocking, fully-isolated atomic commit across machines. Lock everything and you block when a coordinator dies; stay available and you must reason about partial states and undo steps. Pick your pain.

What atomic commit is

A local database transaction is atomic for free: a single engine controls the disk, so it either writes everything or nothing. A distributed transaction loses that luxury — the work spans two or more independent systems (two databases, a database and a payment gateway, three microservices) with no shared transaction manager. The atomic commit problem is: get all participants to either all commit or all abort, even though any one of them — or the coordinator — can crash at the worst possible instant.

Two broad families of answer:

  • Hold locks and vote (2PC) — keep everything locked until everyone agrees, then commit together. Strong atomicity and isolation, but it blocks when the coordinator fails.
  • Break it into steps with undo actions (Saga) — do each step's local transaction, and if a later step fails, run compensating transactions to undo the earlier ones. Never blocks, but gives up isolation — other transactions can see intermediate states.
The one-line frameDistributed atomic commit trades availability for isolation, or isolation for availability — you cannot keep both across machines. 2PC keeps isolation by holding locks and pays with blocking; Sagas keep availability by releasing locks between steps and pay with visible intermediate states and the burden of writing correct undo logic.

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide first. If you know all three cold, skip to the comparison.

Scenario 1 — GitHub, 21 October 2018 (real incident) A 43-second network blip between GitHub's East and West coast datacenters triggered an automated failover. Writes landed on both coasts' databases — each had records the other lacked. GitHub could not automatically reconcile them without risking data loss, so they spent over 24 hours manually merging, choosing a longer outage over losing a single user's write. What property of atomic commit failed here, and why is "all-or-nothing across two datacenters" so much harder than within one?
Scenario 2 — the coordinator dies mid-commit A 2PC coordinator collects "yes" votes from all participants, then crashes before sending the "commit" message. The participants have locked their rows and promised to commit — but they don't know the final decision. What can they do? How long are those rows locked, and who else is now stuck?
Scenario 3 — the dual-write trap Your service must save an order to its database and publish an "order placed" event to Kafka. You write to the database, then publish to Kafka — but the process crashes between the two. What inconsistent state did you just create, and why can't you fix it by just reordering the two writes?

Scenario 1 is why we avoid distributing when we can and accept compensation when we can't. Scenario 2 is 2PC's blocking problem. Scenario 3 is the dual-write problem the outbox solves. Each maps to an idea below.

The five ideas to understand

Theory, not knobs — ideas to hold correctly, each guarded by a misconception.

Idea 1 — Two-Phase Commit (2PC)

2PC is the textbook atomic-commit protocol, run by a coordinator:

  1. Phase 1 — Prepare/Vote. The coordinator asks every participant "can you commit?" Each does the work, locks the affected rows, writes a durable "prepared" record, and votes yes (or no).
  2. Phase 2 — Commit/Abort. If all voted yes, the coordinator records "commit" and tells everyone to commit. Any single no → everyone aborts.

Once a participant votes yes it is bound — it must be able to commit if told to, so it holds its locks until the decision arrives.

The misconception to avoid — "2PC gives clean, safe atomicity" 2PC gives atomicity and isolation in the happy path, but it is a blocking protocol. A participant that has voted yes and then loses contact with the coordinator is stuck holding locks — it cannot unilaterally commit (others may have voted no) nor abort (the coordinator may have decided commit). This is Scenario 2. Worse, 2PC's atomicity needs all participants alive and reachable, so its availability is the product of every participant's — more participants, lower uptime.

Idea 2 — The blocking problem (and why 3PC isn't the answer)

The core defect of 2PC is the window in Scenario 2: coordinator dies after votes, participants block indefinitely, locked rows freeze out other transactions. Three-Phase Commit (3PC) adds a "pre-commit" phase to let participants time out and decide on their own, removing the block — but it assumes bounded network delay (no partitions) and so is unsafe in real asynchronous networks (it can commit on one side and abort on another during a partition). Nobody uses 3PC in production; instead, modern systems either back 2PC with a consensus-replicated coordinator (so the coordinator can't simply vanish) or avoid 2PC entirely.

The misconception to avoid — "3PC fixes 2PC's blocking" 3PC removes blocking only under a synchronous-network assumption that real networks violate. Under a partition it sacrifices safety — different participants can reach different decisions. The real fix isn't a third phase; it's making the coordinator highly available via consensus (Spanner's approach: the coordinator's decision is itself a Paxos entry), or abandoning lock-based commit for Sagas.

Idea 3 — The Saga pattern: a sequence of local transactions with undo

A Saga replaces one distributed transaction with a sequence of local transactions, each in its own service, plus a compensating transaction for each step that semantically undoes it. If step 4 fails, you run the compensations for steps 3, 2, 1 in reverse. Two coordination styles:

  • Orchestration — a central saga orchestrator tells each service what to do and triggers compensations. Easier to reason about, one place to see the flow.
  • Choreography — each service listens for events and reacts, emitting the next event. No central brain, but the flow is scattered across services and hard to follow.
The misconception to avoid — "sagas are atomic and isolated" A Saga gives eventual atomicity (it'll all-commit or all-compensate) but no isolation — between steps, other transactions can see the half-done state (the money is debited but not yet credited). You must design for that: use semantic locks (a "pending" status), make compensations commutative and idempotent, and accept that compensation isn't a true rollback — you can't un-send an email, so you send an apology instead. Sagas move the hard part from the database into your business logic.

Idea 4 — Idempotency and the transactional outbox

Two patterns make distributed workflows survivable:

  • Idempotency keys — every operation carries a unique key; retrying it (after a timeout you weren't sure about) is a no-op if already applied. Essential because in a distributed system you will retry, and "did my first attempt succeed?" is often unanswerable.
  • The transactional outbox — instead of writing to your DB and then publishing an event (two systems, no atomicity), you write the event into an outbox table in the same local transaction as your data. A separate process (or CDC tailing the write-ahead log) reads the outbox and publishes — at-least-once, so consumers must be idempotent.
The misconception to avoid — "just write to the DB and the queue" This is Scenario 3 — the dual-write problem. Writing to two systems without a shared transaction means a crash between them leaves them inconsistent, and reordering doesn't help (whichever is second can fail). The outbox makes the data-write and the event-record one local transaction (atomic for free), then moves the cross-system step to an idempotent, retryable publisher. One local transaction + at-least-once delivery + idempotent consumers beats any attempt at a synchronous dual write.

Idea 5 — When NOT to distribute the transaction

The best distributed transaction is the one you don't have. Often a "distributed" transaction is an architecture smell: data that changes together should usually live together. Co-locating the entities in one service / one database turns a distributed commit back into a free local one. Where you genuinely can't (crossing trust or scaling boundaries, integrating a third party), reach for Saga + outbox + idempotency — not 2PC.

The misconception to avoid — "every multi-step workflow needs a distributed transaction" This is the lesson of Scenario 1: cross-datacenter / cross-service atomicity is so costly and failure-prone that GitHub took a 24-hour outage rather than risk an automated merge. Before reaching for 2PC or even a Saga, ask: can these things live in one transactional boundary? Redrawing service boundaries so related data commits locally eliminates the problem instead of managing it. Distribute the transaction only when the boundary is truly unavoidable.

  • Scenario 2 (coordinator dies): Exactly why are the participants stuck, and why can't 3PC safely save them in a real network? What's the real fix?

Famous implementations

The patterns made concrete. Same facets — what it is, the mechanism, and the failure to watch.

2PC — XA / Spanner

  • What it is. The classic prepare/commit protocol. XA is the standard interface (used by JTA, traditional app servers, some message brokers).
  • Mechanism. Coordinator + vote + commit. Spanner runs 2PC across its Paxos shards, but each participant and the coordinator's decision are consensus-replicated, so a coordinator crash doesn't strand anyone.
  • The failure to watch. Plain XA 2PC blocks on coordinator failure and couples availability to every participant — fine inside one datacenter with a HA coordinator, dangerous across services or regions.

Saga — orchestrators (Step Functions, Temporal, Camunda)

  • What it is. Long-running workflows expressed as a state machine of local transactions + compensations.
  • Mechanism. The orchestrator durably records progress and drives each step; on failure it runs compensations in reverse. Temporal/Cadence keep the workflow as durable, replayable code.
  • The failure to watch. No isolation between steps — you must handle visible intermediate states, and compensations must be idempotent and able to handle "the thing I'm undoing never fully happened."

Outbox + CDC — Debezium

  • What it is. The transactional-outbox pattern automated by change-data-capture.
  • Mechanism. You write business data + an outbox row in one local transaction; Debezium tails the DB's write-ahead log and publishes the outbox rows to Kafka — at-least-once.
  • The failure to watch. Delivery is at-least-once, so every consumer must be idempotent (dedupe on the event/idempotency key) or you'll process duplicates.

Build it yourself

This sticks when you design it. Work each before opening the approach.

Design a saga with compensations

Design "book a trip" = reserve flight + reserve hotel + charge card, where any step can fail. Define each step's local transaction and its compensation, and explain what a user might briefly observe.

Reveal the approach

Steps: (1) reserve flight → compensate: cancel flight reservation; (2) reserve hotel → compensate: cancel hotel; (3) charge card → compensate: refund. Orchestrator drives them; if charge fails, run cancel-hotel then cancel-flight. Each step uses a semantic lock ("HELD" status) so the seat isn't double-sold, and each compensation is idempotent (cancelling an already-cancelled reservation is a no-op). A user might briefly see "flight reserved, payment pending" — that intermediate visibility is the isolation you gave up for availability. Note charge-then-refund isn't a true rollback (the user saw a pending charge), which is acceptable for this domain.

Build a transactional outbox

Your order service must persist an order and publish an OrderPlaced event with no lost or phantom events. Design the tables and the publisher, and state what guarantee consumers get.

Reveal the approach

In one local transaction, insert the order row and an outbox row (event payload + unique id). A relay process (or CDC on the WAL) reads unsent outbox rows, publishes to Kafka, and marks them sent — retrying on failure. Because the relay can crash after publishing but before marking sent, delivery is at-least-once, so consumers dedupe on the event id (idempotent). No event is ever lost (it's committed with the data) and none is phantom (it only publishes what committed). This is the principled fix for the dual-write problem.

Design-it-yourself: the open-ended ones

No single right answer — reason it out, then follow the link.

  • Eliminate the distributed transaction. You have a 2PC across "orders" and "inventory" services that keeps blocking. Redraw the boundaries so the commit becomes local, or convert it to a saga — argue the trade-off. → Microservices, Domain-Driven Design.
  • Make a payment flow exactly-once-effectively. A client may retry a "charge $50" request after a timeout. Design idempotency so the card is charged at most once. → Payment System.
  • Choose orchestration vs choreography. For a 6-step order pipeline across 5 services, pick one and justify it in terms of observability and coupling. → Event-Driven Architecture.
