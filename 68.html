Choosing a Message Broker

SQS, RabbitMQ, Kafka, Kinesis, Pulsar, Redis Streams — they all "move messages," but choosing wrong means either a queue that can't replay when you need history, or a log you're operating like a pyramid when a managed queue would do. The decision turns on one core dichotomy (queue vs log) and a handful of dimensions; get those right and the specific broker almost picks itself.

The trade-off A broker that matches your messaging pattern — in exchange for resisting "just use Kafka" reflexes and accepting each broker's operational and semantic constraints. The right broker fits how your messages flow (one-time work vs replayable stream, ordering, fan-out). The cost: Kafka's power comes with real operational weight; a simple queue can't replay; managed options trade control for convenience.

The core dichotomy — queue vs log

Every broker is fundamentally one of two things, and this is the first and most decisive split:

  • Traditional queue (SQS, RabbitMQ) — a message is consumed and deleted; it's a unit of work to be done once by one worker. Great for distributing tasks. No replay, no multiple independent readers.
  • Log (Kafka, Kinesis, Pulsar) — messages are an append-only, retained, replayable sequence; many independent consumer groups read it, and you can rewind. Great for event streaming, fan-out, and reprocessing.

Almost every other property (ordering, throughput, fan-out, replay) flows from this choice. (This is the same distinction as designing a message queue — here we're choosing one.)

The one-line frameChoosing a broker trades operational simplicity and convenience for fit to your messaging pattern. The core question — queue (consume-and-delete work) or log (retained, replayable, multi-consumer stream)? — eliminates half the options instantly. Get it wrong and you either can't replay/fan-out (queue when you needed a log) or you're operating a heavyweight log for simple task distribution (log when a queue would do).

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide first. If you know all three cold, skip to the comparison.

Scenario 1 — the task queue that became a Kafka cluster A team needs to distribute background jobs (resize an image, send an email) — simple "do this once" work. They reach for Kafka "because it's what real companies use," and now run a multi-broker cluster, manage partitions and consumer-group rebalancing, and still don't use replay. What broker would have done this with near-zero ops, and what signal indicates a queue suffices?
Scenario 2 — the analytics team that can't replay Events go through SQS (consume-and-delete). Now three new teams want to consume the same event stream for different purposes, and one wants to reprocess last month's events to build a new model. Why can't SQS do this, and what broker property do they actually need?
Scenario 3 — "just use Kafka for everything" An architect mandates Kafka for all messaging — including a low-volume internal RPC-ish workflow and a tiny notification fan-out. The team now operates Kafka's full weight for use cases that a managed queue or even Redis would handle trivially. When is Kafka genuinely the right call, and when is it overkill?

Scenario 1 is queue vs log + ops appetite. Scenario 2 is retention/replay + fan-out. Scenario 3 is the default-to-Kafka failure. Each maps to a dimension.

The decision dimensions, resolved in order

Every dimension below is really asking one question: how long does a message matter, and to how many readers? A queue says “to exactly one worker, for exactly as long as it takes to do the job — then it's gone.” A log says “to anyone who cares, for as long as you choose to keep it, replayable from the start.” Ordering, throughput, and operations are all downstream of that one answer — fix it first and half the field disappears. The trap is reaching for the log's “keep everything forever, fan out to everyone” power when all you ever needed was “do this once.”

So resolve them in order: eliminate on queue-vs-log, narrow on ordering/delivery and throughput/replay, tie-break on managed-vs-self-hosted.

Dimension 1 — Queue or log? (eliminate first)

The dichotomy above. Ask: is each message work to be done once and discarded (→ queue: SQS, RabbitMQ), or an event many consumers care about and you may replay (→ log: Kafka, Kinesis, Pulsar)? This eliminates half the field before any other consideration. If you'll never replay and only one consumer does each message once, you want a queue — full stop.

Dimension 2 — Ordering & delivery semantics (narrow)

How much ordering do you need (none / per-key / strict), and what delivery guarantee (at-most / at-least / exactly-once-effect)? Logs give per-partition order; standard SQS gives best-effort order (FIFO mode for strict); RabbitMQ gives per-queue order with flexible routing. Almost everything is at-least-once (so consumers must be idempotent); "exactly-once" is always exactly-once effect via idempotency, never true exactly-once delivery.

Dimension 3 — Throughput & retention/replay (narrow)

How much volume (thousands/sec vs millions/sec), and do you need retention (keep messages after consumption) and replay (reprocess history)? Very high throughput + replay + multi-consumer fan-out → a log (Kafka/Pulsar/Kinesis). Modest throughput, no replay needed → a queue is simpler and cheaper. Retention/replay is often the deciding feature — if a new consumer must be able to read past events, you need a log.

Dimension 4 — Managed vs self-hosted / ops appetite (tie-break)

Once the type is fixed, tie-break on operations. Managed (SQS, Kinesis, managed Kafka/MSK, Confluent Cloud) for minimal ops; self-hosted (Kafka, RabbitMQ, Pulsar on your own infra) for control/cost at scale or specific features. Self-hosted Kafka is genuinely heavyweight to run (brokers, partitions, rebalancing, monitoring) — only worth it when its throughput/replay capabilities are needed and you can operate it (or pay for managed).

The failure — defaulting to Kafka

The mistake that dominates broker decisions The recurring failure (Scenarios 1 & 3) is treating Kafka as the default for all messaging. Kafka is superb for high-throughput, replayable, multi-consumer event streaming — but it's operationally heavy (a cluster to run, partitions to size, consumer-group rebalancing to tune), and using it for simple "do this task once" work means you pay all that cost for capabilities you don't use. The reverse mistake is rarer but real: using a delete-on-consume queue (SQS/RabbitMQ) when you actually need replay or multi-consumer fan-out (Scenario 2), then discovering you can't add a new consumer of past events. The discipline: let the queue-vs-log dichotomy and the replay requirement drive the choice, not Kafka's popularity. For most task distribution, a managed queue (SQS) is the boring, correct answer.

Famous implementations — the broker-by-broker guide

Each broker as a concrete option. Same facets — what it is, sweet spot, when to avoid.

Amazon SQS — the managed default queue

  • What it is. Fully-managed queue; consume-and-delete; built-in DLQ; standard (best-effort order, at-least-once) or FIFO (ordered, dedup).
  • Sweet spot. Task/work distribution with zero ops — the boring correct default for "do this once."
  • When to avoid. You need replay, multiple independent consumers of the same messages, or very high ordered throughput — that's a log.

RabbitMQ — the flexible smart broker

  • What it is. Traditional broker with rich routing (exchanges/bindings), per-queue order, publisher confirms.
  • Sweet spot. Complex routing logic, RPC-style patterns, when you want the broker to do smart message routing.
  • When to avoid. Very high throughput or replay (it's not a log); a backed-up queue consumes broker memory.

Apache Kafka / Kinesis — the high-throughput log

  • What it is. Partitioned, replicated, retained log; many consumer groups; replay. Kinesis is the managed AWS-native equivalent (shards = partitions).
  • Sweet spot. Event streaming, log aggregation, CDC, fan-out to many consumers, reprocessing history, millions/sec.
  • When to avoid. Simple task distribution (operational overkill, Scenario 1/3); use a managed queue instead.

Pulsar / Redis Streams

  • What it is. Pulsar = log with storage/compute separation, multi-tenancy, queue+stream modes, geo-replication. Redis Streams = a lightweight log inside Redis.
  • Sweet spot. Pulsar: multi-tenant/geo at scale with easier elasticity. Redis Streams: simple, low-latency streaming when you already run Redis.
  • When to avoid. Pulsar: small/simple needs (more components to operate). Redis Streams: large-scale durable streaming (Kafka territory).

Build it yourself

Work each before opening the approach.

Pick a broker for three needs

Choose a broker for: (a) resizing uploaded images in the background, (b) an event stream feeding 5 teams + ad-hoc reprocessing, (c) low-latency notifications in a system that already runs Redis. Justify via the dimensions.

Reveal the approach

(a) Image resize: SQS — dimension 1 says queue (do-once work, no replay, one consumer), dimension 4 says managed (zero ops). A Kafka cluster here is Scenario 1's mistake. (b) Event stream for 5 teams + reprocessing: Kafka (or Kinesis if on AWS) — dimension 1 says log (multiple independent consumer groups), dimension 3 nails it (retention + replay to reprocess history). This is exactly what a log is for and a queue can't do (Scenario 2). (c) Low-latency notifications, already on Redis: Redis Streams — dimension 3 (modest volume, low latency) + dimension 4 (you already operate Redis, no new system) make it the pragmatic choice over standing up Kafka. The method: queue-vs-log eliminates first, replay/fan-out and throughput narrow, ops/existing-stack tie-break.

Resist the "Kafka for everything" mandate

An architect wants Kafka for all messaging, including simple task queues. Build the case for choosing per-use-case.

Reveal the approach

Acknowledge where Kafka is right — high-throughput event streaming, fan-out to many consumers, replay/reprocessing — and show those use cases should indeed use it. Then make the cost explicit for the use cases that don't fit: a simple task queue on Kafka means operating a cluster, sizing partitions, and tuning consumer-group rebalancing (which causes its own outages) for "do this once" work that SQS handles with zero ops and a built-in DLQ. Standardizing on one broker has a real benefit (less to learn/operate), so the compromise is often: use a managed log (MSK/Confluent) for streaming AND a managed queue (SQS) for task distribution — both managed, minimal ops, each fitting its pattern. The principle: the queue-vs-log dichotomy is real and ignoring it costs you either capability (no replay) or operational burden (Kafka for trivial work) — match the broker to the pattern, not to a mandate.

Design-it-yourself: the open-ended ones

  • Migrate a queue to a log. You started on SQS and now need replay + fan-out. Plan the migration to Kafka/Kinesis without dropping messages. → Designing a Message Queue.
  • Design exactly-once-effect across the broker. Whatever broker you pick is at-least-once. Design idempotent consumers so the effect happens once. → Idempotency / outbox.
  • Choose ordering granularity. For an order-events stream needing per-order ordering at scale, pick the broker and partition key. → Kafka.
