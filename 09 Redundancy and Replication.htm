Redundancy and Replication

Duplicating critical data and services to remove single points of failure, and adopting a shared-nothing architecture for reliability and resilience.

The bottleneck Partial failure. machines die — surviving it means duplicating, which costs hardware and a consistency problem between copies.

In this lesson

  • Redundancy

Redundancy

Redundancy means duplication of critical data or services with the intention of increased reliability of the system. For example, if there is only one copy of a file stored on a single server, then losing that server means losing the file. Since losing data is seldom a good thing, we can create duplicate or redundant copies of the file to solve this problem.

This same principle applies to services too. If we have a critical service in our system, ensuring that multiple copies or versions of it are running simultaneously can secure against the failure of a single node.

Creating redundancy in a system can remove single points of failure and provide backups if needed in a crisis. For example, if we have two instances of a service running in production, and if one fails or degrades, the system can failover to the other one. These failovers can happen automatically or can be done manually.

The original lesson showed a diagram here illustrating redundant primary and secondary servers with failover between them; it is described by the surrounding text.

Another important part of service redundancy is to create a shared-nothing architecture, where each node can operate independently of one another. There should not be any central service managing state or orchestrating activities for the other nodes. This helps a lot with scalability since new servers can be added without special conditions or knowledge and most importantly, such systems are more resilient to failure as there is no single point of failure.

Principal-level deep dive

Replication topologies, failover, and split-brain

The original lesson frames replication as "keep extra copies for availability." At principal level the real questions are: who is allowed to accept writes, how do conflicting writes get reconciled, and what exactly happens at the moment a leader dies. Those three questions determine your durability, your consistency model, and whether a network partition silently corrupts your data.

Three topologies, three conflict stories

Replication architectures differ primarily in where writes are accepted, and that single choice dictates the conflict-resolution burden you inherit.

TopologyWrite pathConflict handlingRepresentative systems
Single-leaderAll writes to one leader; replicated to followersNo write conflicts by construction — total order at the leaderPostgreSQL/MySQL primary-replica, MongoDB replica set, Kafka partition leader
Multi-leaderWrites accepted at 2+ leaders (often per-region)Concurrent writes to the same key must be merged: LWW, version vectors, or CRDTsActive-active MySQL, CouchDB; many multi-region setups
LeaderlessClient writes to many replicas directly; quorum reads/writesConflicts surfaced via vector clocks / read-repair; app or DB resolves. Read-after-write needs W + R > N so read and write quorums overlapDynamo, Cassandra, Riak, ScyllaDB

Single-leader is the default for a reason: a single ordering point makes read-after-write reasoning tractable. The moment you accept writes in more than one place — multi-leader or leaderless — you have concurrent writes, and concurrency is undefined order. You then must pick a deterministic merge rule. Last-write-wins (LWW) is seductive but lossy: it silently discards one of two genuinely concurrent updates, and it depends on clock skew you do not control. Version vectors detect concurrency precisely; CRDTs (e.g. an OR-Set or a PN-Counter) make merges associative/commutative/idempotent so that order no longer matters — the principled answer when you truly need multi-master writes.

Synchronous, asynchronous, semi-synchronous

This is the durability-versus-latency dial, and it is the single most consequential replication setting in production.

  • Synchronous: the leader does not ack the client until a replica has durably persisted the write. Zero data loss on leader failure, but every write pays a cross-node (often cross-AZ, ~1–2 ms; cross-region, tens of ms) round trip, and a single slow replica stalls all writes.
  • Asynchronous: the leader acks immediately and ships changes in the background. Lowest latency and best availability, but a leader crash loses every write still in flight — the data-loss window.
  • Semi-synchronous: ack after at least one replica confirms (the rest async). This is the pragmatic middle ground — MySQL rpl_semi_sync, PostgreSQL synchronous_standby_names with ANY 1 (...). You bound data loss to "a replica that also has to fail" without paying for all-replica acks.
The async data-loss windowUnder async replication, replication lag is your RPO. If a leader is lagging followers by 800 ms of write volume and it dies, those 800 ms of acknowledged-to-the-client writes are gone — the client believes they committed. This is precisely how many MySQL async failovers lose data. Always state your RPO in terms of worst-case observed lag, not average.

Replication lag and client-centric guarantees

Async followers are always somewhere in the past. Rather than forcing global linearizability (expensive), mature systems paper over lag with weaker, per-client guarantees:

  • Read-your-writes (read-after-write): a user who just posted must see their own post. Achieve it by routing that user's reads to the leader for a short window, by tracking the write's log position (LSN/GTID) and reading only from a replica that has caught up to it, or by sticky-session pinning.
  • Monotonic reads: a user must never see time go backwards (read v5, then v3 from a more-lagged replica). Achieve it by pinning each user to a single replica via consistent hashing on user id.
  • Consistent prefix reads: causally related writes are seen in order — critical when a partitioned/sharded system could otherwise show an answer before its question.

These are far cheaper than strong consistency and cover the vast majority of user-facing correctness needs. See Consistency Models for how PACELC frames the latency cost you are trading away here even when there is no partition.

Automatic failover and the split-brain trap

Failover sounds simple — promote a follower when the leader dies — but every step hides a failure mode. You must (1) detect the leader is dead (usually a timeout, which cannot distinguish "crashed" from "slow/partitioned"), (2) choose a new leader (ideally the most up-to-date follower, by log position), and (3) reconfigure clients and the old leader to follow the new one.

The lethal case: the old leader was only partitioned, not dead. Now you have two nodes both accepting writes — split-brain — diverging the dataset, and on reunion you face unmergeable conflicts. Defenses are non-optional:

  • Quorum / majority: a node may only act as leader if it can see a majority of the cluster. A minority partition cannot form a quorum and so cannot elect itself — this is why Raft/Paxos clusters use odd counts (3, 5). See Leader Election & Coordination.
  • Witness / tiebreaker node: a lightweight voting-only member (no data) in a third AZ so a two-DC deployment still has an odd vote count.
  • Fencing (STONITH / fencing tokens): when a new leader is promoted, the old one must be forcibly prevented from doing damage — either shot in the head (power-fenced) or issued a monotonically increasing fencing token that storage/clients check, rejecting any write carrying a stale token. Fencing tokens are the software answer when you cannot physically kill the node.
Timeout tuning is a real trade-offA short failure-detection timeout fails over fast but flaps on transient GC pauses or network blips, causing needless promotions (each of which risks losing in-flight async writes). A long timeout is stable but extends downtime. There is no universally correct value — it is a function of your network's tail latency and your RTO target.

How the bytes actually move: WAL shipping vs logical replication

Two fundamentally different replication mechanisms, with different coupling and flexibility:

  • Physical / WAL (log) shipping: ship the storage engine's write-ahead log byte-for-byte. Cheap and exact, but the replica must run an identical engine version and on-disk format — no cross-version upgrades, no cross-engine replication, no selective tables. (PostgreSQL streaming replication; MySQL binlog applied at the storage layer.) The WAL also underpins crash recovery and ties directly into storage-engine design — see Replication Deep Dive.
  • Logical / row-based: replicate decoded logical change events (insert/update/delete of row X with these values). Decoupled from physical format, so it enables zero-downtime major-version upgrades, replicating a subset of tables, fan-out to heterogeneous sinks, and Change-Data-Capture pipelines (Debezium → Kafka). The cost is more CPU to encode/decode and the need to handle schema evolution.

Statement-based replication (ship the SQL) is the legacy third option and is dangerous: non-deterministic functions (NOW(), RAND(), auto-increment races) diverge replicas. Modern systems default to row-based for exactly this reason.

Read replicas: scaling reads and the staleness cost

Adding async read replicas is the standard lever to scale a read-heavy workload — fan reads across N replicas while writes stay funneled to the leader. The catch is that every replica is stale by its replication lag, so this only works for read-tolerant paths. The principal-level discipline is to classify reads by staleness tolerance: route account-balance-after-transfer or "did my comment post" reads to the leader (or a caught-up replica via LSN), and route analytics, feeds, and search to replicas. Beware the read-scaling ceiling: every replica still applies 100% of the write volume, so replicas relieve read CPU/IO but never reduce write load — past that point you must shard, not just replicate.

Principal-level mastery check

  • Can you state the precise data-loss window for async replication after a leader crash, and explain why sync replication trades that window for write latency and availability (one slow/dead follower stalls every commit)? When does semi-sync (ack from one of N) become the right middle ground?
  • Why does single-leader give you a clean ordering and no write conflicts, while multi-leader and leaderless push the conflict-resolution problem onto you (LWW, version vectors, CRDTs)? For each, when is it the correct choice?
  • Can you explain why replication lag breaks read-your-writes consistency, and name three fixes — route post-write reads to the leader, track the client's last-seen LSN/position and only read from a caught-up replica, or pin a session to one replica?
  • Why does monotonic-reads violation (a user sees data, refreshes, and it "disappears") happen when reads bounce across replicas at different lag, and how does session stickiness solve it?
  • Can you describe how split-brain arises (a network partition leaves two nodes each believing they're leader) and why fencing — a monotonically increasing fencing token rejected by storage if stale, or STONITH — is required because a lease/heartbeat timeout alone cannot prevent a paused-then-resumed old leader from issuing a write?
  • Can you reason about the read-scaling ceiling — why adding async read replicas relieves read load but never write load (every replica applies 100% of writes), and at what point you must shard instead of replicate?
  • Why does a quorum system need W + R > N for read-after-write consistency, and what goes wrong (stale reads, lost updates) when you weaken it to W + R ≤ N for latency?
