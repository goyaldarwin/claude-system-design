What changes as you grow toward principal-level depth

Early in your career, system design is largely a vocabulary and component-assembly skill: can you name a load balancer, a cache, a sharded database, a message queue, and wire them into a diagram that survives the back-of-the-envelope math? That skill is necessary but nowhere near sufficient for the systems you will eventually own. At principal level you are setting technical direction for systems other people build, so what matters is judgment, not recall — and judgment is much harder to self-assess, because it's invisible until a design meets reality.

The growth happens along seven axes. Use them as a mirror: read each row and ask honestly which column describes how you reason today. Most engineers sit firmly in the middle column on a few axes and reach the right-hand column only when something forces them to.

AxisDevelopingPrincipal-level depth
Trade-off reasoningPicks a reasonable option, can justify itNames the 2–3 viable options, states the axis of the trade-off, and explains under what future conditions the decision should be reversed
AmbiguityWaits for, or asks for, the requirementsDrives the requirements — proposes a scope, confirms it with stakeholders, and explicitly de-scopes what will not be built
ScaleSays “it scales horizontally”Identifies the specific resource that saturates first (CPU, IOPS, fan-out, hot key, connection count) and the number at which it does
FailureAdds replicas and retriesReasons about partial failure, blast radius, correlated failure, and what the system does while degraded
CostRarely consideredTreats $/request, storage growth, and cross-AZ/region egress as first-class design constraints
EvolutionDesigns the end stateDesigns the migration path from today's system to the end state, online, with rollback
Operability“We'll add monitoring”Names the SLIs, the alert thresholds, the runbook for the top failure mode
Key insight The single most reliable sign of deep understanding is self-driven de-scoping under ambiguity. Anyone can add a component. Deciding what not to build, stating it out loud, and getting buy-in is the behavior that keeps large systems shippable. If you find yourself never narrowing a problem — always trying to design everything — that's the first habit to fix, because it's the cheapest one that separates competent from principal.

The depth rubric: shallow vs deep per dimension

Grade yourself along eight dimensions. For each, the gap between shallow and deep is the column on the right. Don't reduce it to a number — write yourself a sentence of evidence per dimension (“last time I designed X, I did/didn't do this”). Evidence is what tells you whether you actually have the skill or just recognize it when you see it.

1. Requirements clarification

Shallow understandingDeep understanding
Jumps straight to a diagram. Asks one or two surface questions (“how many users?”).Separates functional from non-functional. Pins read/write ratio, latency budget, consistency expectation, and durability target. States explicit non-goals (“I'm not handling moderation or billing in this design”).

Self-test: “What can I drop from v1 and still ship something useful?” If you have a crisp answer, you're thinking like an owner. See 14 — the step-by-step guide for the canonical clarification flow.

2. Estimation / capacity

Shallow understandingDeep understanding
Picks round numbers with no derivation; never converts QPS to bytes, IOPS, or machines.Derives peak QPS from DAU and a peak-factor, converts to storage growth/year, derives the bottleneck resource, and sizes the fleet. Distinguishes average from p99.
Pitfall Don't be impressed with yourself for fast arithmetic. The arithmetic only matters when it changes the design. The point of estimation is to discover that, say, the write volume needs an LSM engine (38) rather than a B-tree, or that the fan-out at 100M followers forces a hybrid push/pull feed (26). If your numbers never feed back into a decision, they're theater — even when correct.

3. Data modeling & partitioning

Shallow understandingDeep understanding
One big table. Partition key is “user_id” with no thought about skew.Chooses an access-pattern-driven schema, picks a partition key that avoids hot shards, and reasons about re-sharding / consistent hashing (12) when the fleet grows. Knows when to denormalize.

4. Deep-dive on the bottleneck

Shallow understandingDeep understanding
Stays at the box-and-arrow level the whole time.Picks the riskiest component and goes three layers down — e.g. inside the queue, reasons about partition assignment, consumer-group rebalancing, and exactly-once vs at-least-once (39, 54).

5. Failure modes

Shallow understandingDeep understanding
“We have replicas, so we're fine.”Walks through leader loss, network partition, slow-but-alive node (gray failure), thundering herd on cache miss, and poison messages. Knows the difference between fail-stop and Byzantine. Applies resilience patterns (49) — circuit breakers, bulkheads, backpressure.

6. Consistency & correctness

Shallow understandingDeep understanding
Says “strongly consistent” or “eventually consistent” as a slogan.Names the actual model (linearizable, read-your-writes, monotonic reads, causal), maps it to PACELC (31), and accepts the latency cost. Knows where consensus (30) is needed and where it's overkill.

7. Operability & SLOs

Shallow understandingDeep understanding
“We'll set up Grafana.”Defines SLIs that match user pain, sets an SLO and an error budget (47), names the one metric worth paging on, and sketches the top runbook. Designs for debuggability (48) with trace IDs end-to-end.

8. Evolution & migration

Shallow understandingDeep understanding
Designs only the steady state.Explains how to get there from a monolith/legacy DB online: dual writes, backfill, shadow reads, cutover with a kill switch, and rollback. Treats schema/version evolution as a permanent constraint, not a one-time event.
Sign of real depth You volunteer the migration story before anyone asks for it. “The end state is sharded Postgres with Vitess, but we can't flip overnight — here's the dual-write + backfill + shadow-read sequence and how I'd verify parity before cutover.” That single sentence proves you've actually shipped large changes, not just whiteboarded them. If you can't produce it, that's a concrete area to go practice on a real system.

The self-test question bank by topic

Use these as the “go deeper” layer once you've sketched a baseline design — either for a real project or a practice problem. Ask them of yourself. The goal is not to trip yourself up; it's to find the exact depth at which you run out of genuine understanding and start hand-waving. You should be able to take at least one topic four levels down. Wherever you can't, you've found your next study target.

Consistency & transactions

  • I said “eventually consistent.” What's the actual convergence bound, and what does a client observe in the meantime? Can a user read their own write?
  • Where in this design do I genuinely need linearizability, and where would causal or read-your-writes be enough? What does the stronger choice cost in latency and availability? (31)
  • This operation spans two services. Two-phase commit, Saga, or an outbox? Can I trace the failure where the coordinator dies after prepare? (32)
  • How do I order events across nodes without a global clock? When does a Lamport clock suffice and when do I need a vector clock or TrueTime-style bounds? (33)
  • If two replicas concurrently update the same item, who wins — and how does the loser find out? Would a CRDT remove the conflict entirely here? (35)

Partitioning & hot spots

  • My partition key is X. Can I name the access pattern that creates a hot shard, and what I'd do when one celebrity/tenant is 30% of traffic?
  • I need to add capacity at 2× the fleet. With my hashing scheme, how many keys move? (12)
  • How do I do a range scan when data is hash-partitioned? What changes if the workload becomes range-heavy?
  • Re-sharding under live traffic — can I describe the split, the double-read window, and how I avoid losing writes during the move? (05)

Caching

  • Cache-aside vs write-through vs write-back — which, and what's my stale-read window? (04)
  • A hot key expires and 50k requests hit the DB in the same millisecond. Can I walk through my stampede prevention — single-flight, lease, or probabilistic early expiry?
  • How do I invalidate? What's the consistency contract between cache and source of truth, and how do I detect when they drift?
  • At what hit ratio does the cache stop paying for itself given the cross-AZ network cost of a miss?

Consensus & coordination

  • I put a Raft group here. Why? What's the latency floor (one round-trip to a majority) and can the rest of the system tolerate it? (30)
  • What happens to writes during a leader election? How long is the window, and is that acceptable for this SLO?
  • I'm using ZooKeeper/etcd for leader election. What's my fencing-token story so the old leader can't keep writing after it loses the lock? (36, 62)
  • Can I avoid consensus here entirely — e.g. with idempotency + a CRDT or single-writer-per-partition? Consensus is expensive; can I justify it?

Reliability & SLOs

  • Which single SLI does a user actually feel? What's the SLO, and what does the error budget buy the team? (47)
  • A dependency I call is at 50% error rate. What does my service do — fail open, fail closed, degrade, shed load? (49)
  • Can I describe a correlated failure that takes out a whole AZ? What's the blast radius, and how do I fail over without a thundering retry storm? (51)
  • How would I deploy a risky change to this service safely? Canary, blue-green, feature flag — and what's the automated rollback trigger? (50)
  • One request fans out to twelve services and is slow. How would I find the culprit at 3am? (48)

Security

  • Where's the trust boundary? What's authenticated, what's authorized, and what's encrypted in transit vs at rest? (52)
  • How do I stop one tenant from reading another's data in a shared multi-tenant store?
  • This endpoint takes user input that becomes a query/key. What are the injection and key-collision risks?
  • How do I rate-limit and protect against abuse without hurting legitimate burst traffic? (23)

Cost

  • What's the dominant cost line — compute, storage, or network egress? Can I estimate $/million requests?
  • I'm replicating across three regions. What does cross-region egress cost, and can I cut it with locality or async replication?
  • Storage grows N TB/month forever. What's my tiering / TTL / compaction story so cost doesn't grow unbounded? (38)

Signs of shallow vs deep mastery

Pitfall Signs your understanding is still shallow — catch these in your own designs and treat each as a study target:
  • Over-engineering. Reaching for Kafka, Kubernetes, and a service mesh before establishing that a single Postgres won't do. A solution in search of a problem usually means you're pattern-matching instead of reasoning.
  • Cargo-culting microservices. Splitting into 15 services on the first diagram with no discussion of transaction boundaries or the operational tax. (43)
  • Ignoring failure. The happy path is fully drawn; “what if this node dies?” produces a blank or “we add a replica.”
  • No numbers. You can't connect QPS to machines, bytes, or dollars. Every choice is vibes.
  • Can't de-scope. You try to build everything and end up shallow everywhere and deep nowhere.
  • Defending instead of adapting. When a flaw is pointed out, you argue rather than update the design. Deep understanding means changing your mind on evidence.
Sign of real depth Signs you've truly internalized the material:
  • You drive the design. You set the agenda, manage scope, and decide what to skip and why — without waiting to be told.
  • You quantify. Every major decision is backed by a number that actually moved the design.
  • You name explicit trade-offs. “I'm choosing availability over consistency here because a stale like-count is fine; I'd choose the opposite for the payment ledger.” (11, 59)
  • You design for failure and operability by default, not as an afterthought.
  • You consider migration/evolution — how this ships incrementally and how it changes in two years.
  • You know what you don't know. “I haven't run this at that scale; here's how I'd de-risk it with a load test before committing.”

A 45-minute self-practice loop

A useful way to drill is to take any prompt from the index and design it solo under a clock, narrating your reasoning out loud (or in writing) as if explaining it to a colleague. Time-box the phases so the depth lands where the signal is:

MinutesPhaseWhat to watch for in yourself
0–5Frame the problemDo you clarify and de-scope, or start drawing immediately?
5–10EstimationDo the numbers actually drive a decision?
10–20High-level designCoherent baseline; clear data flow; sensible component choices
20–35Deep divePick the riskiest part; push yourself for depth and failure reasoning
35–42Operate & evolveSLOs, top runbook, migration story
42–45ReviewThe gaps you noticed are your study list
Trade-off Breadth vs depth in the deep-dive. You can either probe one component to bedrock or sample three components shallowly. When practicing for real mastery, prefer depth: one bedrock dive tells you whether you actually understand how systems behave in production, whereas breadth only re-confirms vocabulary you already exercised in the high-level phase. Sample breadth only when your baseline is so generic you can't yet tell where your real understanding lies.
Key insight The best self-calibration trick: after you make a choice, change one requirement on yourself (“now writes are 10× reads”, “now it must be multi-region active-active”) and watch whether your design flexes coherently or collapses. Deep understanding is a mental model you can re-run under new constraints; shallow understanding is a memorized diagram that doesn't survive perturbation.

Internalized vs memorized: the final check

Sign of real depth The thing worth grading is never the diagram — it's the reasoning that produced it and whether it would survive contact with production. When you've truly internalized the material, you think out loud, surface your own assumptions, quantify, name the trade-off explicitly, choose, and then state the conditions under which you'd revisit the choice. You reach bedrock on at least one component, and you volunteer the migration and failure stories without being prompted. You're comfortable saying “I'd de-risk that with a load test rather than guess” — because real engineers measure rather than assert.

Sharp questions to keep in your pocket and ask yourself about any design, regardless of the topic:

  • “If I had to cut this design in half to ship in one quarter, what's the half I cut — and what breaks if I'm wrong about it being safe to cut?”
  • “Which single resource saturates first as we 10× the load, and at what number? What's the second bottleneck after I fix the first?”
  • “What's the worst 3am page this system produces? What fired, what's the runbook, and why didn't it auto-heal?”
  • “Where did I choose availability over consistency, and where the opposite? Can I convince a skeptic a stale read is acceptable in the first case and unacceptable in the second?”
  • “How would I migrate the live system from today's design to this one with zero downtime? What's the exact sequence and the rollback trigger?”
  • “What's the dominant cost line, and what design change would halve it without halving reliability?”

The test of memorized vs internalized is simple: a memorized answer is the same every time, while an internalized one bends to fit a new constraint. If you can answer these about a problem you've never seen before — not just the ones in this course — you've leveled up.
