Domain-Driven Design

DDD belongs in a system-design course for one reason: it gives you a principled way to decide where the boundaries go — which is the hardest and most consequential decision in microservices. Its core ideas (bounded contexts, ubiquitous language, aggregates) are really about drawing lines that match how the business actually works, so your services and teams fall along seams that don't tear. Used well it's a boundary-finding discipline; used as dogma it's over-engineering.

The crux Boundaries that match the business, so services and teams split cleanly — in exchange for the upfront work of modeling the domain and the discipline to apply it only where complexity warrants. Get the boundaries right and your architecture has natural seams: services own coherent capabilities, teams own services, and changes stay local. You pay with domain-modeling effort and the judgment to not over-apply DDD's heavy tactical patterns to a simple CRUD app.

What DDD is (for system design)

Domain-Driven Design (Eric Evans) is a way to build software around a deep model of the business domain. For a system-design course, strip it to its most useful core: DDD is the discipline that tells you where to draw boundaries — between models, between services, between teams — so they fall along the natural seams of the business rather than arbitrary technical lines. It splits into strategic DDD (the big-picture boundaries: bounded contexts, ubiquitous language, context maps) and tactical DDD (the building blocks inside a context: aggregates, entities, value objects, domain events).

Why it matters here: the single hardest question in microservices is "where do the service boundaries go?" — and getting it wrong gives you a distributed monolith. DDD's strategic patterns are the best-known answer, which is why it shows up at the architecture level, not just in code.

The one-line frameDDD trades upfront domain-modeling effort and the judgment to apply it selectively for boundaries that match reality — services and teams that own coherent capabilities and can evolve independently. It is a boundary-finding tool, not a ceremony; its value is concentrated in the strategic ideas, and its biggest risk is dogmatic over-application of the tactical patterns where they aren't needed.

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide first. If you know all three cold, skip to how DDD maps to system design.

Scenario 1 — the one giant "Customer" model that no one can change A company models "Customer" once, globally, with every attribute any team ever needed — sales fields, support fields, billing fields, shipping fields — in one shared model. Now every team is coupled to it: a change for billing breaks support, and no one can evolve their part. What DDD idea says this is the wrong approach, and how should "Customer" actually be modeled across the business?
Scenario 2 — the team that drew service boundaries by database table A team splits a monolith into microservices — one service per database table (UserService, OrderService, OrderLineService, AddressService) — and ends up with services so chatty and interdependent they must deploy together. Why did "boundaries by table" fail, and what should the boundaries have followed?
Scenario 3 — DDD applied to a simple CRUD admin tool A team builds an internal CRUD admin tool and applies the full DDD tactical playbook — aggregates, repositories, domain events, value objects, layers of abstraction — for what is essentially forms over a database. It takes 5× longer and no one understands it. When is DDD's heavy machinery appropriate, and when is it over-engineering?

Scenario 1 is bounded contexts. Scenario 2 is context mapping / boundaries + bounded contexts. Scenario 3 is the over-engineering misconception. Each maps to an idea below.

The four ideas to understand

DDD has many concepts; for system design these four carry the weight. Each is guarded by a misconception.

Idea 1 — Bounded context (the central idea)

A bounded context is an explicit boundary within which a particular model and language apply consistently. The key insight: the same word means different things in different parts of the business, and trying to force one universal model is the root of coupling. "Customer" in Sales (a lead with a pipeline stage) is a different model than "Customer" in Shipping (an address + delivery preferences) or Billing (a payment method + invoices). Each context has its own model of Customer, tailored to its needs, with a clear boundary between them. Bounded contexts are the natural unit that microservice and team boundaries should follow.

The misconception to avoid — "one model for the whole system" This is Scenario 1. The instinct to build a single, canonical, enterprise-wide model of each concept ("the one true Customer") seems clean but is the deepest source of coupling: every team depends on it, every change ripples, and the model bloats trying to serve everyone (and serves no one well). DDD says the opposite: let each bounded context have its own model of the concept, sized to that context's needs, with explicit boundaries and translation between them. Sales' Customer, Shipping's Customer, and Billing's Customer are different models that may share only an identifier. Accepting that "one concept ≠ one model" is the central mental shift — and it's what lets services and teams decouple.

Idea 2 — Ubiquitous language

Within a bounded context, everyone — engineers, product, domain experts — uses the same precise vocabulary, and that language is reflected directly in the code (class names, methods, events). The point is to eliminate the lossy translation between "what the business says" and "what the code calls it," which is where bugs and misunderstandings breed. The language is ubiquitous within its context but doesn't have to match other contexts' language — that's fine, because contexts are bounded.

The misconception to avoid — "names don't matter / we'll translate" Treating the domain vocabulary as cosmetic — engineers using their own terms, a constant mental translation between business-speak and code-speak — quietly corrodes a system: requirements get mistranslated, the same thing has three names, and the model drifts from what the business actually means. The language is part of the model. When the code says policy.renew() and the domain expert says "renew the policy," there's no translation gap to introduce errors. The misconception that naming is bikeshedding misses that consistent, business-aligned language is what keeps the model correct and the team aligned — especially across the engineer/expert divide.

Idea 3 — Aggregates (the consistency boundary)

The most system-design-relevant tactical idea. An aggregate is a cluster of related objects treated as a single unit for data changes, with one aggregate root as the entry point, and — crucially — the aggregate is the transactional consistency boundary: changes within one aggregate are atomic and strongly consistent; changes across aggregates are eventually consistent (via domain events). This directly shapes your data and transaction design: an aggregate maps to "what must be consistent together," which maps to "what lives in one service / one transaction."

The misconception to avoid — "bigger aggregates are simpler" (and over-engineering) Two related traps. First, making aggregates too large (e.g. an "Order" aggregate that includes the entire customer and product catalog) means every change locks a huge object, killing concurrency and forcing unrelated things to be transactionally consistent — aggregates should be the smallest cluster that must change together. Second, and the broader Scenario 3 trap: applying aggregates/repositories/domain-events everywhere, including simple CRUD where there's no real domain complexity, is pure over-engineering — you've added layers of abstraction to forms-over-a-database. DDD's tactical patterns earn their cost only in genuinely complex domains (rich business rules, invariants, behavior); for simple CRUD, skip them. Match the tool to the complexity.

Idea 4 — Context mapping & domain events

Contexts must relate without re-coupling. Context mapping names the relationships (e.g. an anti-corruption layer that translates another context's model so it can't pollute yours; or a published interface a context exposes). And domain events ("OrderPlaced", "PaymentReceived") are the integration mechanism between contexts: instead of one context reaching into another's data, it reacts to events — which is exactly event-driven integration, with each context owning its own data.

The misconception to avoid — "contexts can share a database" This is half of Scenario 2. If two bounded contexts read and write the same database tables, the boundary is fake — they're coupled at the deepest level, schema changes break both, and you've recreated the shared-database coupling DDD exists to eliminate. Each context (and the service that implements it) must own its data and integrate via well-defined interfaces or domain events, not shared tables. Context mapping makes these relationships explicit (who depends on whom, who translates), and domain events let contexts collaborate while staying decoupled — the bridge from DDD to microservices and event-driven architecture. Boundaries that don't extend to the data aren't boundaries.

DDD in real systems — the concepts applied

The ideas made concrete in how real systems are built. Same facets — the concept, how it shows up, the pitfall.

Bounded contexts → microservice boundaries

  • How it shows up. Well-designed microservice systems draw one service per bounded context (Orders, Payments, Inventory) — each owns its model, language, and database.
  • Why it works. Boundaries fall on business seams that change together, so a feature touches one service and teams align with services (Conway's law).
  • The pitfall. Boundaries drawn by technical layer or table instead of context → distributed monolith (Scenario 2).

Aggregates + domain events → sagas & eventual consistency

  • How it shows up. An aggregate = one local transaction; a business process spanning aggregates/services becomes a saga driven by domain events ("OrderPlaced" → reserve inventory → charge).
  • Why it works. In-aggregate changes are strongly consistent; cross-aggregate are eventually consistent — matching what's actually achievable across services (CAP).
  • The pitfall. Aggregates too large (concurrency death) or expecting cross-service ACID (you get sagas + eventual consistency instead).

Context maps / anti-corruption layers

  • How it shows up. When integrating a legacy system or third party, a context puts an anti-corruption layer in front to translate the foreign model into its own clean model.
  • Why it works. The foreign/legacy model can't leak in and corrupt your domain; the boundary stays clean as systems evolve (key during a strangler-fig migration).
  • The pitfall. Skipping it → the external model's quirks infect your core, re-coupling you to what you were trying to isolate.

Apply it yourself

DDD sticks when you draw the boundaries. Work each before opening the approach.

Find the bounded contexts for an e-commerce system

You have Users, Products, Carts, Orders, Payments, Inventory, Shipping, Reviews, Support tickets. Group them into bounded contexts and decide how "Customer/User" is modeled in each.

Reveal the approach

Group by business capability, not entity: e.g. Catalog (Products, Reviews), Ordering (Carts, Orders), Payments, Fulfillment (Inventory, Shipping), Identity (Users/auth), Support (tickets). Each is a bounded context that becomes a service owning its data. Crucially, "Customer" is a different model in each (Scenario 1): in Ordering it's a buyer with a cart and order history; in Payments it's a billing profile + payment methods; in Fulfillment it's a shipping address + delivery prefs; in Support it's a person with a ticket history. They share only a customer id — no single universal Customer model. Contexts integrate via domain events ("OrderPlaced" → Fulfillment reserves inventory) and each owns its tables. The test of good boundaries: a typical change touches one context, and contexts don't share a database (Scenario 2's fix). This is exactly the service-boundary exercise, with DDD as the method.

Size an aggregate as a consistency boundary

For an Order with line items, a shipping address, and a link to the customer and products, decide what belongs inside the Order aggregate and what doesn't — and why.

Reveal the approach

Ask "what must change together, atomically, to keep an invariant true?" The Order aggregate should include the things whose consistency is intrinsic to the order: the order itself + its line items (the invariant "order total = sum of line items" must hold atomically) and maybe a snapshot of the shipping address at order time. It should not include the full Customer or Product entities — reference them by id only. Why: making the aggregate huge (embedding customer + catalog) would mean every order change locks those, killing concurrency and forcing unrelated consistency (Scenario 3's "too big" trap). Cross-aggregate consistency (e.g. decrement Inventory when an Order is placed) is handled by a domain event + saga, accepting eventual consistency — because Inventory is a different aggregate (and context). So: smallest cluster that must be atomic = the aggregate; reference other aggregates by id; integrate across them with events. The aggregate boundary directly becomes your transaction and service boundary.

Design-it-yourself: the open-ended ones

  • Decide where DDD pays off. For a system with one genuinely complex domain (insurance underwriting) and several simple CRUD areas (admin, settings), decide where to apply tactical DDD and where to skip it. → Architectural Styles.
  • Integrate a legacy system cleanly. Design an anti-corruption layer so a messy legacy model can't corrupt a new bounded context during a migration. → Microservices (strangler-fig).
  • Turn contexts into an event-driven system. Take your bounded contexts and design the domain events that integrate them, with each owning its data. → Event-Driven Architecture.
