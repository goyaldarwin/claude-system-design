System Design Basics

Before the building blocks, three orienting truths: every design reduces to three questions, there is no single "correct" architecture (only one that fits the requirements), and you scale when you need to — not before, but not blind to where it'll bend.

The trade-off Headroom vs. simplicity — under-engineering means re-architecting under fire; over-engineering means paying for capacity you never use. The craft is finding the point where a small, reversible investment in headroom is worth it, and stopping there.

The three questions behind every design

Designing a large system reduces to three recurring questions — simple to state, hard to answer well. The rest of the course gives you vocabulary and reusable answers for each:

  1. What pieces are available? The menu of building blocks — load balancers, caches, databases, queues, proxies, indexes — you can assemble.
  2. How do these pieces interact? How a request flows from client → edge → application tier → data tier and back. Where state lives, who talks to whom.
  3. What are the right trade-offs? Every block buys something (throughput, latency, availability) at a cost (complexity, consistency, money). Choosing well — and defending it — is the whole game.
Key insightThere is no single "correct" architecture. A design is good only relative to a set of requirements and constraints. The same problem can have a perfectly reasonable single-server answer and a perfectly reasonable globally-distributed one — the difference is the scale, the SLAs, and the budget you were handed.

Scale only when you need to — but plan for it

Investing heavily in scaling infrastructure before you need it is neither smart engineering nor smart business. Premature scaling burns money, multiplies operational surface, and slows you with unused complexity. Many products would be better served by a boring single beefy machine for far longer than engineers admit.

That said, a little forethought goes a long way: knowing where the system will bend as load grows — which component becomes the bottleneck first, which state is hardest to shard, which call sits on the critical path — lets you avoid decisions that are cheap today and brutal to undo later.

The trade-offUnder-engineering → you scramble and re-architect under fire when traffic spikes. Over-engineering → you pay, in dollars and velocity, for capacity and complexity you never use. The craft is finding the point where a small, reversible investment in headroom is worth it, and stopping there.

This is exactly why you ask about scale up front: "How many users? Requests per second? Read- or write-heavy? Latency and availability targets?" The answers decide whether a single database is fine or you genuinely need partitioning, replication, and a CDN. Designing for unstated scale is a common way to lose points — the framework lesson makes this a disciplined first step.

Self-check

  • State the three questions behind every design, in your own words.
  • Why is there no single "correct" architecture — what makes a design "good"?
  • Give the failure mode of over-engineering and of under-engineering, and where the sweet spot is.
  • What scale questions do you ask up front, and what does each answer change in the design?

The shape to remember: three questions (pieces / interactions / trade-offs); no one right architecture; scale when needed but know where it'll bend.
