Resilience Patterns

Every dependency you call will eventually be slow, fail, or lie — at scale, all the time. Resilience patterns are the small set of defensive mechanisms that keep one failing component from taking down everything around it. The deepest lesson: the techniques you'd reach for to recover from failure (especially retries) are, applied naively, exactly what turns a small failure into a catastrophic cascade.

The trade-off Containing failure so one slow/broken dependency can't sink the whole system — in exchange for added complexity, deliberately failing fast, and shedding some load instead of serving it. These patterns keep a partial failure partial. You pay with the engineering complexity of timeouts/breakers/bulkheads everywhere, and with the choice to reject work (fail fast, shed load) rather than queue it — degrading gracefully beats collapsing completely.

What resilience means — designing for failure

Resilience is the system's ability to keep functioning (perhaps degraded) when its dependencies fail — and at scale, failure is constant: a server dies, a network blips, a downstream service gets slow. You cannot prevent failure; you can only contain it. The mindset shift is from "make components not fail" to "assume they will, and make sure one failure stays local."

The enemy is the cascading failure: a small failure (one slow dependency) triggers behaviors (retries, blocked threads, growing queues) that amplify it into a system-wide outage. Almost every large outage is a cascade. The patterns below are the toolkit for breaking the amplification at each link — and they compose: timeouts make breakers possible, breakers stop retry storms, bulkheads stop one breaker's failure spreading.

The one-line frameResilience trades complexity and the willingness to fail fast / shed load for containment. You add machinery (timeouts, breakers, bulkheads) and you accept rejecting some requests cleanly rather than accepting all and collapsing — because a system that serves 90% of traffic and sheds 10% beats one that tries to serve 100% and crashes to 0%.

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide first. If you know all three cold, skip to the patterns.

Scenario 1 — AWS US-EAST-1, December 2021 (real incident) An automated scaling action triggered a surge of connection attempts on AWS's internal network. The congestion caused delays and errors — which made clients retry, which added more connections, which worsened the congestion, which caused more retries. The system had effectively DDoS'd itself; the recovery systems' own retry logic collapsed under the load, and engineers needed multiple manual interventions to break the loop. What turned a transient overload into a self-sustaining outage, and what two changes to retry behavior would have damped it?
Scenario 2 — the dependency that's down, getting hammered A downstream service is completely down. Your service keeps calling it — every request waits for the timeout, fails, retries, fails again — consuming threads and CPU on calls that cannot succeed, slowing your service for everyone (even requests that don't need that dependency). What pattern would let you stop calling the dead dependency immediately and fail fast, then automatically test when it recovers?
Scenario 3 — one slow feature drains the whole thread pool Your service uses one shared thread pool for all requests. A non-critical dependency (a "recommendations" call) gets slow; requests using it pile up holding threads, until all threads are stuck waiting on it — and now even your critical, unrelated endpoints can't get a thread and the whole service goes down. What isolation pattern would have confined the damage to just the recommendations feature?

Scenario 1 is retries & the retry storm. Scenario 2 is circuit breakers. Scenario 3 is bulkheads. Each maps to a pattern below.

The five patterns that contain failure

Five patterns, applied together. Each has a named failure nested under it — the thing that happens without it.

Pattern 1 — Timeouts: the foundation

Every network call must have a timeout — a maximum time to wait before giving up. It sounds trivial, but it's the foundation everything else builds on: without a bound, a slow dependency holds your resources indefinitely. Set timeouts based on the dependency's actual latency distribution (e.g. a bit above its p99), not a round number — too short and you abort healthy slow requests, too long and you hold resources through a hang.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the infinite wait A call with no timeout (or a too-long one) waiting on a hung dependency holds its thread/connection forever. Under load, every such call accumulates — threads and connections leak into the stuck dependency until the pool exhausts and your service stops serving anything. A slow dependency is more dangerous than a dead one: a dead one fails fast, a slow one silently consumes your capacity. Timeouts convert "hang forever" into "fail quickly," which is the prerequisite for retries, breakers, and bulkheads to even function. Default-no-timeout (the default in many HTTP clients!) is a classic latent outage.

Pattern 2 — Retries with backoff and jitter

Retrying a transiently-failed request improves success rates — but only if done carefully. Three rules: exponential backoff (wait 1s, 2s, 4s… between attempts, easing pressure on a struggling dependency), jitter (randomize the wait so clients don't retry in synchronized waves), and a cap on attempts (give up rather than retry forever). And only retry idempotent operations — or use idempotency keys — or you'll duplicate side-effects.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the retry storm This is Scenario 1. Naive retries are the most common cause of cascading failure. When a dependency gets slow, every client retries; the retries multiply the load on the already-struggling dependency (a small overload becomes a retry storm — potentially N× the original traffic), which makes it slower, which triggers more retries — a self-sustaining amplification (AWS Dec 2021 DDoS'd its own network this way). The damping fixes: exponential backoff + jitter (so load decreases and de-synchronizes instead of pulsing) and a retry budget / circuit breaker (cap total retries; stop retrying a clearly-down dependency). Also retry only at one layer — retries stacked across layers multiply exponentially (3 layers × 3 retries = up to 27× load).

Pattern 3 — Circuit breakers

A circuit breaker wraps calls to a dependency and tracks failures. Like an electrical breaker, it has three states: closed (calls flow normally), open (after too many failures, it stops calling the dependency entirely and fails fast — no waiting, no load on the dead service), and half-open (after a cooldown, it lets a trial request through to test recovery; success → close, failure → re-open). It turns "slowly time out on every call to a dead service" into "instantly fail and give the dependency room to recover."

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — hammering a dead dependency This is Scenario 2. Without a breaker, your service keeps sending requests to a down dependency — each one waits out the timeout, consumes a thread, fails, maybe retries — pure waste that slows your service (even for requests not needing that dependency) and denies the struggling dependency any breathing room to recover. The breaker opens after a failure threshold and fails those calls instantly (so callers can use a fallback fast and threads aren't held), then half-opens to probe recovery automatically. It both protects you (fail fast, free your resources) and protects the dependency (stop the flood so it can recover).

Pattern 4 — Bulkheads and resource isolation

Named after a ship's watertight compartments: bulkheads isolate resources so a failure in one area can't drain the resources shared by others. Concretely: give each dependency (or each class of work) its own connection/thread pool, so if one dependency gets slow and saturates its pool, other dependencies and critical paths still have their own pools and keep working.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — shared-pool exhaustion This is Scenario 3. With a single shared thread/connection pool, a slow dependency lets its requests pile up holding threads until the whole pool is exhausted — and now every endpoint, including critical ones that never touch the slow dependency, can't get a thread and fails. One non-critical feature took down everything. Bulkheads partition the pool: recommendations gets pool A, checkout gets pool B; recommendations saturating pool A leaves checkout's pool B untouched. The cost is some resource fragmentation (you can't fully share capacity), bought in exchange for containment — one feature's failure stays one feature's failure.

Pattern 5 — Load shedding and graceful degradation

When demand exceeds capacity, you have two choices: try to serve everything (and collapse) or deliberately shed load — reject some requests cleanly (return 429/503 fast, prioritizing critical traffic) so the rest get served. Pair it with graceful degradation: when a dependency is unavailable, return a fallback (cached/default/partial data) instead of an error — show the page without recommendations rather than no page. Backpressure propagates "slow down" upstream so work queues at a durable buffer, not in memory.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — total collapse under overload A system with no load shedding, faced with more traffic than it can handle, tries to accept all of it — queues grow, latency climbs, memory fills, and it crashes to serving 0% when it could have served, say, 80%. Worse, requests time out while still queued, so you do work nobody receives (goodput collapses). Load shedding caps accepted work at what you can actually serve and rejects the excess fast (cheaply, prioritizing important requests); graceful degradation keeps the core experience working when extras fail. The principle: controlled partial service beats uncontrolled total failure — degrade on purpose before you're forced to collapse by accident.

Together they break the cascade at every link — and chaos engineering proves they actually work.

  • Scenario 2 (hammering a corpse): How does a circuit breaker's open/half-open behavior protect both you and the dependency?
  • Scenario 3 (drained pool): How do bulkheads stop one slow feature from taking down unrelated endpoints, and what do they cost?

  • Why does controlled load shedding beat trying to serve everything under overload?

The shape to remember: 5 patterns — timeouts, retries+backoff+jitter, circuit breakers, bulkheads, load shed/degrade — each breaking the cascade at one link.

Cloud & managed building blocks

PatternWhere it lives
Timeouts / retries / circuit breaking (network)Service mesh (Istio/Envoy, App Mesh, Linkerd) — configured, no app code
Circuit breaker / bulkhead (in-app)resilience4j (Java), Polly (.NET), Hystrix (legacy), gobreaker
Load shedding / throttlingAPI Gateway throttling, ALB, mesh rate limits; adaptive concurrency (Envoy)
Chaos engineeringAWS Fault Injection Simulator, Gremlin, Chaos Mesh, (Netflix) Chaos Monkey
The takeawayA service mesh implements timeouts, retries (with budgets), circuit breaking, and outlier ejection at the network layer — uniformly across services, no per-app code — which is a major reason to adopt one. But in-app patterns (bulkheads around specific dependencies, graceful-degradation fallbacks, idempotency) still need code. And none of it is trustworthy until chaos engineering has proven it under real injected failure.

Famous implementations

The patterns made concrete. Same facets — what it is, what it provides, and the failure to watch.

Service mesh / Envoy

  • What it is. Sidecar proxies (Envoy under Istio/App Mesh) applying resilience at the network layer.
  • Provides. Timeouts, retries with budgets, circuit breaking, outlier ejection (eject a bad host), and rate limiting — configured centrally, no app code.
  • The failure to watch. Mesh retries + app retries stack (multiply load — Scenario 1); aggressive outlier ejection can eject too many hosts and cause its own cascade (the ejection cascade).

Libraries — Hystrix / resilience4j / Polly

  • What it is. In-process resilience libraries (Netflix Hystrix was the pioneer; resilience4j/Polly are the modern successors).
  • Provides. Circuit breakers, bulkheads (separate thread pools), retries, rate limiters, and fallbacks as code-level wrappers around calls.
  • The failure to watch. Misconfigured thresholds (breaker too sensitive flaps, too lax never trips); per-call overhead; must wrap every external call to be effective.

Chaos engineering

  • What it is. Deliberately injecting failure in production-like environments to validate resilience (Netflix Chaos Monkey kills instances; Gremlin/FIS inject latency, errors, partitions).
  • Provides. Proof that your timeouts/breakers/bulkheads actually contain failure — finding the cascade before a real incident does.
  • The failure to watch. Running chaos without observability or a blast-radius limit; start small (one instance, staging) and expand with confidence.

Build it yourself

These click when you trace the failure path. Work each before opening the approach.

Stop a cascading failure in a call chain

Checkout calls Payments (critical) and Recommendations (nice-to-have), both synchronously, sharing one thread pool. Recommendations gets slow. Place the resilience patterns so a slow Recommendations can never take down checkout.

Reveal the approach

Layer the patterns: (1) Timeout on the Recommendations call — short (its p99), so a slow response is abandoned fast, not held. (2) Bulkhead — give Recommendations its own small thread pool separate from Payments/checkout, so even if it saturates, critical paths keep their threads (Scenario 3 fix). (3) Circuit breaker on Recommendations — after N failures/timeouts it opens and fails calls instantly (Scenario 2 fix), stopping the waste and giving Recs room to recover. (4) Graceful degradation — when the breaker is open or the call times out, return the checkout page without recommendations (a fallback), not an error. (5) Retries on Recommendations: few, with backoff+jitter, or none (it's optional). Net effect: a totally-down Recommendations causes checkout to render slightly degraded, with zero impact on the critical path — the cascade is broken at every link.

Design safe retries that can't storm

Your client retries failed calls to a backend. Design the retry policy so that when the backend gets slow, your retries reduce rather than amplify the pressure.

Reveal the approach

Exponential backoff: wait 1s, 2s, 4s… between attempts — so a struggling backend sees decreasing pressure, not constant hammering. Jitter: randomize each wait (e.g. random between 0 and the backoff) so thousands of clients don't retry in synchronized waves that pulse the backend (de-synchronization is what jitter buys). Cap attempts (e.g. 3) and add a retry budget — if more than X% of recent calls are retries, stop retrying (or trip a circuit breaker) so you can't multiply load on a clearly-down backend (Scenario 1). Retry at one layer only — never stack retries across client + gateway + mesh (3 layers × 3 attempts = 27× amplification). And only retry idempotent operations / use idempotency keys. The combination turns retries from an amplifier into a damper.

Design-it-yourself: the open-ended ones

No single right answer — reason it out, then follow the link.

  • Make a system shed load gracefully. Under a 5× traffic spike you can only serve 1×. Design prioritized load shedding so critical users get served and the system never collapses. → Rate Limiter.
  • Run a safe chaos experiment. Design a game-day that injects latency into a key dependency in production, with a hypothesis, blast-radius limit, and abort criteria. → Observability.
  • Survive a regional dependency outage. A dependency in one region goes down. Design fallbacks/degradation so your service stays up on stale or partial data. → Multi-Region & DR.
