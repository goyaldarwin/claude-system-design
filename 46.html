Event-Driven Architecture

Designing systems around the things that happened — and the discipline required to keep an asynchronous, eventually-consistent fabric from collapsing into an undebuggable mess.

The bottleneck Partial failure & latency. decoupling via events buys resilience, paid in eventual consistency and the dual-write problem.

Why event-driven, and what it actually buys you

An event-driven architecture (EDA) inverts the dependency direction of a request/response system. Instead of a service calling the services it depends on, a service emits a fact — "OrderPlaced", "PaymentCaptured", "InventoryReserved" — and any number of consumers react. The producer does not know, and deliberately should not know, who consumes the event. That single property — producer/consumer decoupling — is the whole point, and everything else is a consequence of it.

Three concrete benefits follow:

  • Temporal decoupling: the consumer need not be alive when the event is produced. The broker buffers, so a downstream outage degrades to lag, not data loss. Contrast a synchronous chain in microservices, where one slow hop blocks the whole call.
  • Extensibility without modification: a new consumer (fraud scoring, analytics, a search indexer) subscribes to an existing stream with zero change to the producer. This is the open/closed principle at the system level.
  • Load leveling and backpressure: the log/queue absorbs spikes; consumers drain at their own rate. See Queues.
PitfallEDA does not remove coupling — it moves it into the event schema and into temporal/ordering assumptions. A naive team replaces explicit, type-checked RPC contracts with implicit, runtime-discovered dependencies on event shape and arrival order. You traded a compiler error for a 3 a.m. page. The cost of EDA is paid in operational and schema discipline.

Events vs commands vs messages

Engineers routinely conflate these. Precise vocabulary signals you have actually run one of these systems.

TermIntentDirectionalityCardinality
EventA statement of fact that already happened. OrderShipped. Immutable, past-tense.Fire-and-forget; producer expects nothing back.0..N consumers.
CommandA request to do something. ShipOrder. Imperative, may be rejected.Directed at exactly one owner who decides.1 logical handler.
MessageThe transport envelope. Carries either an event or a command plus headers/metadata.Neutral.Depends on payload.

The distinction is not pedantic. A command embeds an expectation of authority — the sender is telling the receiver to do work, and ownership/validation lives with the receiver. An event embeds no expectation; the producer asserts a fact and walks away. Mixing them — e.g. emitting "SendEmailCommand" onto a broadcast topic where three consumers all act — produces duplicate side effects and is one of the most common EDA design errors.

Key insightName events in the past tense and as domain facts, not as instructions. PaymentCaptured, not UpdateBalance. The moment your event name contains a verb in imperative mood, you have smuggled a command onto a pub/sub topic and re-coupled producer to consumer.

Pub/sub vs point-to-point

Two delivery topologies sit underneath every broker:

  • Point-to-point (queue): each message is delivered to exactly one consumer in a competing-consumers pool. Used for work distribution — "process this order." Scales horizontally by adding workers; the queue load-balances. SQS standard/FIFO, RabbitMQ work queues, a Kafka consumer group on a topic.
  • Publish/subscribe (topic): each message is delivered to every subscriber. Used for fan-out — one fact, many independent reactions. SNS, Kafka with multiple distinct consumer groups, Google Pub/Sub.

Kafka is worth calling out because it unifies both: a consumer group gives point-to-point semantics within the group (each partition consumed by one member) while multiple groups give pub/sub across them. The log-based design also means messages are retained and replayable rather than destructively consumed — covered in Kafka and Log-based Messaging. That replayability is what makes event sourcing and stream reprocessing possible.

Trade-offQueue (delete-on-ack) vs log (offset-based, retained). A traditional queue is cheaper to operate and naturally bounds storage, but once a message is acked it is gone — you cannot add a new consumer that needs history, and a bug that drops messages is unrecoverable. A retained log lets you add consumers and replay after a bug fix, at the cost of storage, partition/ordering management, and consumer-managed offsets.

Choreography vs orchestration

Once a business process spans several services, you must choose how the steps are coordinated. This is the single highest-leverage design decision in an EDA and the one principal engineers are graded hardest on.

Choreography

No central coordinator. Each service reacts to events and emits its own. OrderPlaced → Payment service captures → emits PaymentCaptured → Inventory reserves → emits InventoryReserved → Shipping dispatches. The "workflow" is an emergent property of who-subscribes-to-what; it exists in no single place.

  • Pro: maximal decoupling, no single bottleneck, easy to add a participant.
  • Con: the process is invisible. There is no one place to ask "what state is order #5512 in?" Cyclic event dependencies and infinite loops are easy to create accidentally. Compensation logic (the saga rollback) is scattered.

Orchestration

A central orchestrator (a saga coordinator, a workflow engine like Temporal, AWS Step Functions, or Netflix Conductor) owns the process. It sends commands to services and listens for replies, advancing an explicit state machine and invoking compensations on failure.

  • Pro: the workflow is explicit, observable, versioned, and testable. Failure handling and timeouts live in one place. This is how you implement the saga pattern for distributed transactions without 2PC.
  • Con: the orchestrator is a coupling point and can become a god service if it accretes business logic that belongs in the participants.
What strong engineers doThey don’t pick a side dogmatically. The mature answer: use choreography for loosely related reactions (analytics, notifications, cache invalidation — "fire and forget" fan-out) and orchestration for multi-step business transactions with money, inventory, or compensation. A common rule of thumb: choreography stays manageable up to ~3 participants in a flow; beyond that, the implicit state machine becomes unobservable and orchestration earns its keep.

Event notification vs event-carried state transfer vs event sourcing

Martin Fowler’s three patterns describe how much state an event carries, and they have very different operational consequences.

PatternPayloadConsumer behaviorTrade-off
Event notificationThin. Just an ID + type: {type: OrderPlaced, orderId: 5512}.Calls back to the producer to fetch details.Minimal coupling on data shape, but re-introduces a synchronous read dependency and N+1 callbacks.
Event-carried state transferFat. The full relevant state at the time of the event.Maintains its own local read model; no callback needed.Full temporal decoupling and read scalability, at the cost of data duplication and stale local copies.
Event sourcingThe event is the system of record. State is the fold over the event log.Rebuilds state by replaying events; projects to read models (CQRS).Perfect audit/time-travel and replay, at the cost of schema-evolution pain, snapshotting, and query complexity.

Event-carried state transfer is the default for most service-to-service integration: it removes the runtime callback, which is precisely the coupling you adopted EDA to escape. Event sourcing is a much bigger commitment — the log of events is the truth, and current state is a derived projection. It shines for auditability (finance, see Designing a Payment System) and for systems that need to reconstruct historical state, but it imposes real costs: you can never "just edit a row," replaying years of events requires snapshots, and changing an event’s meaning retroactively is genuinely hard.

PitfallEvent sourcing is frequently cargo-culted. If your team does not need a full audit log or temporal queries, you are paying its cost (eventual consistency between write and read models, upcasting old events, snapshot management) for benefits you will not use. Reach for event-carried state transfer first; adopt full event sourcing only where the audit log is the product requirement.

Delivery guarantees and the dual-write problem

Every EDA must answer: what delivery guarantee does the broker give, and does my application preserve it end-to-end?

  • At-most-once: fire and forget; messages can be lost. Acceptable only for disposable signals (metrics samples).
  • At-least-once: the realistic default. Messages are redelivered on uncertain failure, so consumers must be idempotent. This is what Kafka/SQS effectively give you.
  • Exactly-once: the holy grail and mostly a marketing term across a network boundary. Kafka offers exactly-once processing within its transactional read-process-write boundary, but the instant a side effect leaves that boundary (an email, a third-party charge), you are back to at-least-once + idempotency.

The dual-write problem

This is the defining correctness trap of EDA. A service handling an order naively does two writes:

// BROKEN: two independent writes, no atomicity
db.save(order);              // write 1: local DB
broker.publish(orderPlaced); // write 2: message broker

These are not in one transaction. Any of the following leaves the system inconsistent:

  • DB commits, then the process crashes before publish → the order exists but no one downstream knows. Lost event.
  • Publish succeeds, then the DB transaction rolls back → downstream acts on an order that does not exist. Phantom event.

You cannot fix this with retries or ordering tricks — there is no atomic commit spanning a database and a broker (and 2PC across them is both rare and operationally toxic; see Distributed Transactions).

What strong engineers doThey immediately name the Transactional Outbox pattern. Write the business row and an outbox row in the same local transaction — now atomicity is back, guaranteed by the single database. A separate relay publishes outbox rows to the broker, typically driven by Change Data Capture tailing the DB transaction log (Debezium reading the binlog/WAL). Delivery is at-least-once, so consumers stay idempotent. This converts an unsolvable distributed-atomicity problem into a solved local-transaction-plus-CDC problem.
BEGIN;
  INSERT INTO orders (id, status, ...) VALUES (5512, 'PLACED', ...);
  INSERT INTO outbox (id, aggregate_id, type, payload, created_at)
    VALUES (uuid(), 5512, 'OrderPlaced', '{...}', now());
COMMIT;
-- A CDC connector (Debezium) tails the WAL/binlog and publishes
-- new outbox rows to Kafka, then the relay/connector marks/cleans them.

Poison messages, dead-letter queues, and retry strategy

At-least-once delivery plus imperfect consumers means some messages will fail processing. A poison message is one that fails every time — malformed payload, a referenced entity that was deleted, a bug. Without handling, it blocks the partition/queue head (head-of-line blocking) and the retry loop hammers the consumer forever.

The standard machinery:

  • Retry with exponential backoff and jitter. Retry transient failures (timeouts, 503s) with delays like 1s, 2s, 4s, 8s plus random jitter to avoid synchronized retry storms. Cap the attempts. See Resilience Patterns.
  • Dead-letter queue (DLQ). After N failed attempts, move the message to a DLQ with full context (original payload, error, attempt count, stack). The main flow keeps moving; the poison message is quarantined for human/automated triage and later redrive.
  • Distinguish retryable from non-retryable. A validation error (400-class) will never succeed on retry → DLQ immediately. A downstream timeout (503-class) is worth retrying. Blindly retrying non-retryable errors wastes capacity and delays the DLQ.
PitfallIn a partitioned log like Kafka there is no per-message ack — a stuck message at an offset blocks the entire partition behind it. Naive "retry in place" stalls everything sharing that partition. Production patterns use separate retry topics (with delay tiers) and a DLQ topic so the main partition keeps advancing. Also: make DLQ depth a first-class alert — a silently filling DLQ is data loss with extra steps.

Schema registry and event versioning

In a synchronous API you can coordinate a deploy. In EDA the producer and a dozen consumers deploy independently and on different days, and events sit in the log for weeks. The event schema is therefore a long-lived, multi-party contract, and managing its evolution is non-optional.

  • Schema registry (Confluent Schema Registry with Avro/Protobuf/JSON Schema) stores versioned schemas and enforces compatibility rules at produce time. The message carries a schema ID, not the full schema, keeping payloads small.
  • Backward compatible change: new consumers can read old events (e.g. add an optional field with a default). Forward compatible: old consumers can read new events (they ignore unknown fields). Full compatibility requires both — the safe default for independently deployed services.
Key insightThe cardinal rule: additive, optional changes only. Never rename a field, never change its type, never remove a required field, never repurpose a field’s meaning. To make a breaking change, emit a new event version (OrderPlaced.v2) and run both in parallel until every consumer has migrated — the expand/contract (parallel-change) pattern. Tombstoning a field takes two releases, not one.

Eventual consistency as a deliberate design choice

EDA is fundamentally an eventually consistent architecture. The moment a producer emits and returns before consumers have reacted, there is a window where the system is in disagreement with itself: the order is "placed" but the inventory count and the search index have not yet updated. This is not a bug to be eliminated — it is the price of the temporal decoupling that gives EDA its scalability and resilience. The job is to make it a conscious, bounded choice rather than an accident.

This connects directly to the CAP theorem and PACELC: by choosing async events you are choosing availability and partition tolerance over immediate consistency, and even in the absence of partitions (the "else, latency" of PACELC) you are choosing lower latency over strong consistency. Engineering the consistency model means:

  • Bound the lag. Measure end-to-end and consumer lag; SLO it. "Eventually" must have a number — p99 propagation under 2s — or it is a euphemism for "never, sometimes." See SLI/SLO/SLA.
  • Design the UX for it. "Your order is processing" instead of asserting a state the system has not converged to. Read-your-writes where the user expects it (often via a synchronous read of the producer’s own store).
  • Make consumers idempotent and order-tolerant. Events may arrive out of order or duplicated; use event IDs for dedup and version/timestamp fields to drop stale updates. CRDTs and ordering tools from Time, Clocks and Ordering apply here.

A concrete place all of this lands is a Notification System: events fan out to email/push/SMS channels, each channel is an at-least-once idempotent consumer with its own DLQ and retry tier, and the whole thing is eventually consistent with the source domain by design.

Principal-level mastery check

What strong engineers doThey treat EDA as a set of trade-offs, not a default. They reach for the outbox + CDC pattern the instant a service both writes a DB and publishes an event, without being prompted. They distinguish events from commands precisely, default consumers to idempotent + at-least-once, and put a number on "eventual." They choose choreography vs orchestration based on whether the flow involves money/compensation and how many participants it spans, and they name schema-registry compatibility rules as a hard requirement, not a nicety. Crucially, they can articulate when not to use EDA — a strongly-consistent, low-fan-out, request/response interaction is often better served synchronously.
  • A service saves an order to Postgres and publishes OrderPlaced to Kafka. Walk me through every way this becomes inconsistent and how you make it atomic. (Listen for: dual-write, outbox, CDC, at-least-once + idempotency.)
  • Your checkout saga spans payment, inventory, and shipping. Choreography or orchestration? Defend it, then tell me how you roll back when inventory reservation fails after payment succeeded.
  • A consumer hits a message it can never process. Trace what happens in Kafka specifically — what about the partition behind it? Design the retry-topic/DLQ topology and the alerting.
  • The producer team wants to rename a field and change a type on an event with eight consumers deployed independently. How do you ship that without an outage?
  • How do you decide between event notification, event-carried state transfer, and event sourcing for a given integration? Give me a case where event sourcing is the wrong choice.
  • Your product team says "the dashboard must always show the live count." You have an async pipeline. How do you reconcile the consistency requirement with the architecture — and when do you push back and go synchronous?
