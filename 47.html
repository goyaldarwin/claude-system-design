Containers, Kubernetes & Serverless

Where does your code actually run, and who manages the machine under it? The answer — a container on your own cluster, a Kubernetes-orchestrated fleet, or a serverless function with no servers in sight — trades control for operational burden in opposite directions. Pick too much abstraction and you hit cold starts and cost cliffs; pick too little and you're running infrastructure you didn't need to.

The trade-off Less infrastructure to manage and finer-grained scaling — in exchange for less control, cold-start latency, and cost models that can flip against you at scale. Moving up the abstraction ladder (VM → container → Kubernetes → serverless) hands more operational work to the platform and scales more granularly. You pay with reduced control over the runtime, cold-start latency, and per-invocation pricing that's cheap at low volume but can become very expensive at high, steady load.

What the substrate question is — the abstraction ladder

Every workload runs on something, and you choose how much of that something you manage. The ladder, from most control/most work to least:

  • VM — a full virtual machine; strong isolation (its own kernel), but heavy and slow to start; you manage the OS.
  • Container — your app + dependencies packaged to run anywhere, sharing the host kernel; lightweight, fast, portable; you still manage where they run.
  • Kubernetes — an orchestrator that runs containers across a fleet: scheduling, scaling, self-healing, networking. Enormous power, enormous operational surface.
  • Serverless / FaaS — you ship a function; the platform runs it on demand, scales to zero, and you never see a server. Least ops, least control.

The whole subject is a single trade made repeatedly: climb the ladder and the platform takes more work off you, but you give up control and inherit new failure modes (cold starts, cost cliffs, orchestrator complexity).

The one-line frameThe substrate choice trades control and predictable cost for less operational burden and finer-grained scaling. There's no universally right rung — match it to the workload's traffic shape, latency needs, and your team's operational appetite. The two classic mistakes are running Kubernetes when a PaaS would do, and reaching for serverless on a workload whose shape makes it slow and expensive.

Three scenarios — decide before you read

Don't scroll past these. Decide first. If you know all three cold, skip to the comparison.

Scenario 1 — Prime Video's serverless cost cliff, 2023 (real case) Prime Video's video-monitoring pipeline ran on serverless: Step Functions orchestrating Lambdas, shuttling data through S3. At scale, Step Functions state-transition charges and S3 calls became enormous, and it hit a hard scaling limit at ~5% of target load. Moving it into a single long-running container cut cost ~90%. Why did serverless get so expensive here, and what traffic/work shape makes serverless the wrong fit?
Scenario 2 — the function that's slow only sometimes A user-facing API on Lambda is fast under steady traffic, but after a quiet period the first request takes 2–5× longer. Under bursty load, a fraction of requests are mysteriously slow. What is this latency called, why does it happen, and what are the ways to reduce or hide it?
Scenario 3 — the autoscaler that's always one step behind Your Kubernetes service scales on CPU. A traffic spike hits; the autoscaler notices, requests new pods, the cluster autoscaler requests new nodes, images pull, pods start — and by the time capacity arrives, users have been getting errors for 90 seconds. Why is reactive autoscaling too slow for spikes, and what do you configure so capacity is ready before you need it?

Scenario 1 is the serverless cost/scaling cliff. Scenario 2 is cold starts. Scenario 3 is autoscaling & headroom. Each maps to a decision below.

The four decisions that pick a substrate

Choosing where code runs is these four questions. Each has a named failure nested under it.

Decision 1 — Containers vs VMs: what isolation do you need?

A VM virtualizes hardware — each has its own kernel, so a compromised or misbehaving VM is strongly isolated, at the cost of size (GBs) and slow boot. A container virtualizes the OS — many containers share the host kernel, so they're tiny (MBs), start in milliseconds, and pack densely, but isolation is weaker (a kernel exploit, or a noisy container, affects neighbors). Containers are the default for app deployment; VMs (or stronger sandboxes like Firecracker microVMs) where you need hard isolation between tenants.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the shared-kernel blast radius Because containers share the host kernel and (without limits) host resources, one container can become a noisy neighbor — hogging CPU/memory/IO and starving co-located containers — and a kernel-level vulnerability potentially crosses the container boundary. Mitigations: set resource requests/limits (and quotas) so no container can starve others, and for untrusted/multi-tenant workloads use stronger isolation (gVisor, Firecracker microVMs, or separate VMs/nodes). Don't assume a container is a security boundary as strong as a VM — it isn't.

Decision 2 — Do you actually need an orchestrator?

Kubernetes earns its keep when you run many services across many nodes and need automated scheduling, self-healing (restart/replace failed pods), rolling deploys, autoscaling, service discovery, and a declarative reconciliation model (you declare desired state; the control loop continuously drives reality toward it). That power comes with a steep operational surface: control plane, networking, RBAC, upgrades, YAML.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — Kubernetes you didn't need A small team running a handful of services adopts Kubernetes "for scale" and spends more engineering time operating the cluster (upgrades, networking debugging, YAML, RBAC) than building product — the operational tax dwarfs the benefit. For most small-to-medium workloads a container PaaS (Cloud Run, Fargate, App Runner, Container Apps) gives you containers + autoscaling + rolling deploys with a fraction of the burden. Reach for Kubernetes when you genuinely have the scale, multi-service complexity, or portability needs that justify it — not by default. (Mirror of the premature-microservices mistake.)

Decision 3 — How do you scale and stay available?

On a cluster you scale at multiple levels: HPA (Horizontal Pod Autoscaler — add/remove pod replicas on CPU/memory/custom metrics), VPA (right-size a pod's resources), and the cluster autoscaler (add/remove nodes when pods can't be scheduled). Availability comes from running multiple replicas across nodes/zones, health probes (liveness/readiness), and graceful shutdown (connection draining on deploy).

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — no headroom for the spike This is Scenario 3. Reactive autoscaling has a lag chain: metric observed → scale decision → new node provisioned → image pulled → pod ready — often 30–120 seconds. A sudden spike outruns it, and users get errors before capacity arrives. Fixes: keep headroom (run at, say, 60% so you absorb bursts while scaling), use pre-provisioned/over-provisioned spare capacity (pause pods that get evicted to make room fast), scale on a leading signal (queue depth, RPS) rather than lagging CPU, and pre-pull images. You can't scale reactively faster than your provisioning chain — so buy time with headroom.

Decision 4 — Should it be serverless? (cold starts and the cost cliff)

Serverless/FaaS is unbeatable for spiky, event-driven, or low-traffic workloads: it scales to zero (you pay nothing when idle), scales out automatically, and you manage no servers. But it has two characteristic costs: cold starts (a request hitting a not-yet-warm function pays initialization latency) and a pricing model (per-invocation + per-GB-second) that's cheap at low/spiky volume but can become far more expensive than a container at high, steady load.

The failure that happens when you get this wrong — the serverless cost/latency cliff This is Scenarios 1 & 2. Cost cliff: serverless bills per invocation and per unit compute-time; a high-throughput, always-on, or chatty workload (lots of state transitions, lots of small calls — Prime Video's Step Functions + S3) racks up charges that a single long-running container handles for a fraction of the price — and orchestration services impose their own scaling ceilings. Cold start: after idle (or on a burst needing new instances), the platform must initialize a runtime before serving — adding latency to those requests; mitigate with provisioned/warm concurrency, smaller deployment artifacts, and lighter runtimes. Rule: serverless for spiky/event-driven/low-steady-state work and glue; containers for sustained high-throughput or latency-critical paths.

Famous implementations

The runtimes made concrete. Same facets — what it is, the model, and the failure to watch.

Kubernetes

  • What it is. The dominant container orchestrator; declarative, reconciliation-loop-based.
  • Model. You declare desired state (Deployments, Services); controllers continuously reconcile actual toward desired; HPA/VPA/cluster autoscaler handle scaling; etcd stores cluster state (consensus underneath).
  • The failure to watch. Operational complexity (Scenario's Decision 2); scaling lag without headroom (Scenario 3); stateful workloads are the hard case (need StatefulSets + persistent volumes, and careful failover).

Serverless / FaaS (Lambda, Cloud Functions)

  • What it is. Run a function on demand; scale to zero and out automatically; pay per invocation + compute-time.
  • Model. Event/HTTP triggers spin up function instances (often on Firecracker microVMs); no servers to manage.
  • The failure to watch. Cold starts (Scenario 2 — mitigate with provisioned concurrency, lean runtimes); the cost cliff at high steady load (Scenario 1); execution-time/memory limits; local state is ephemeral.

Container PaaS (Cloud Run / Fargate / Container Apps)

  • What it is. Run a container with platform-managed scaling and deploys — the middle ground between raw K8s and FaaS.
  • Model. Push an image; the platform schedules, autoscales (often to zero), and load-balances it; no cluster to operate.
  • The failure to watch. Less control than Kubernetes (limited custom networking/operators); scale-to-zero reintroduces cold starts for latency-sensitive paths.

Build it yourself

These click when you reason about traffic shape. Work each before opening the approach.

Configure autoscaling that survives a spike

Your K8s service gets sudden 5× traffic spikes that currently cause ~90s of errors while it scales. Design the autoscaling so capacity is ready in time.

Reveal the approach

You can't scale reactively faster than the provision chain (metric → HPA → cluster autoscaler → node up → image pull → pod ready), so buy time: (1) run with headroom — target ~60% CPU so existing pods absorb the first part of a spike; (2) keep over-provisioned spare capacity (low-priority "pause" pods that get preempted to instantly free room for real pods while new nodes spin up); (3) scale on a leading metric (requests-per-second or queue depth) rather than lagging CPU, so you react sooner; (4) pre-pull images to nodes so pod start is fast. Combine with readiness probes so traffic only hits ready pods. The spike is absorbed by headroom while real scaling catches up — reactive scaling alone is always one step behind (Scenario 3).

Pick a substrate for three workloads

Choose VM / container-PaaS / Kubernetes / serverless for: (a) a steady high-traffic web API, (b) a nightly image-thumbnail batch triggered by uploads, (c) a 50-microservice platform run by 8 teams. Justify each.

Reveal the approach

(a) Steady high-traffic API: a container on a PaaS (or K8s if already there) — sustained load makes serverless's per-invocation pricing and cold starts a bad fit (the cost cliff), while a long-running container is cheap and warm. (b) Upload-triggered thumbnails: serverless — spiky, event-driven, idle much of the time; scale-to-zero means you pay only when uploads happen, and cold starts don't matter for async batch work. (c) 50 services, 8 teams: Kubernetes (or a managed flavor) — this is the scale/complexity that justifies an orchestrator's operational cost: many services, independent scaling, self-healing, shared platform. The lesson: match the rung to traffic shape + scale, never one-size-fits-all (and recall Prime Video collapsing serverless back to a container for a sustained workload).

Design-it-yourself: the open-ended ones

No single right answer — reason it out, then follow the link.

  • Cost-model a workload across substrates. Estimate the monthly cost of a steady 1000-RPS API on Lambda vs a container, and find the break-even point. → Cost-Aware System Design.
  • Run a stateful service on Kubernetes. Design how a database or stateful consumer survives pod rescheduling (StatefulSets, persistent volumes, leader election). → Leader Election, Replication.
