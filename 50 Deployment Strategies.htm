Deployment Strategies

How to ship changes to a running distributed system without taking it down, and how to undo a bad change before it burns your users.

The core problem: change is the leading cause of outages

Most production incidents are not caused by random hardware failure or organic traffic — they are self-inflicted, triggered by a change someone deployed. Google's SRE writings put the figure at roughly 70% of outages tracing to a change in a live system. That single fact reframes the whole discipline: a deployment strategy is fundamentally a risk-management mechanism, not a delivery convenience. The question is never "how do I get my code out?" It is "how do I limit the blast radius and the time-to-detect when this change is wrong, and how fast can I get back to a known-good state?"

Three properties matter for any strategy, and you should be able to name them under pressure:

  • Blast radius — what fraction of traffic/users sees the new code before you have a signal it is healthy.
  • Time to detect (TTD) — how long between a bad version going live and you knowing it is bad. This is bounded by your observability and your SLOs.
  • Time to recover (TTR) — how long to get back to good once you know. The whole point of blue-green and canary is to make TTR a config flip, not a rebuild.
Key insightEvery deployment strategy is a different point on the curve trading off cost/complexity against blast radius and recovery speed. Rolling is cheap but slow to recover; blue-green recovers instantly but doubles capacity cost; canary minimizes blast radius but needs real automated analysis to be worth the operational weight.

Decoupling deploy from release

The most important conceptual move a principal makes is separating two things that juniors conflate:

  • Deploy — getting new binary/code onto production machines. This is an operational, infrastructure event.
  • Release — making new behavior visible to users. This is a product event.

If these are coupled, every behavior change requires a deploy, and every deploy is a high-stakes event because it simultaneously changes what's running and what users experience. Once you decouple them — typically with feature flags — you can deploy a binary that contains dormant code many times a day with low risk, then turn on the actual feature independently, gradually, and reversibly. This is why mature orgs deploy continuously but release deliberately.

What strong candidates doThey volunteer the deploy/release distinction early and use it to simplify the rest of the design: "I'd ship the new code path behind a flag defaulting to off, deploy it through a rolling update, verify it's inert, then ramp the flag as a canary on the release dimension." Now the rollback for a behavioral bug is a flag flip with sub-second TTR, no redeploy.

Rolling updates

A rolling update replaces instances of the old version with the new version incrementally — N at a time — until the fleet is fully converted. This is the Kubernetes default RollingUpdate strategy and the baseline for most stateless services. See Containers, Kubernetes and Serverless for the orchestration mechanics.

strategy:
  type: RollingUpdate
  rollingUpdate:
    maxUnavailable: 25%   # never drop below 75% capacity during roll
    maxSurge: 25%         # may briefly run 125% to keep capacity
readinessProbe:           # CRITICAL: gate traffic on real readiness
  httpGet: { path: /healthz/ready, port: 8080 }
  initialDelaySeconds: 5
  periodSeconds: 5

The decisive detail is the readiness probe. Kubernetes only routes traffic to a pod once its readiness probe passes, and a rolling update will not proceed to the next batch until new pods are ready. A weak or missing readiness check is the classic cause of a "successful deploy" that black-holes traffic: the orchestrator declares the new pods up, the load balancer (see Load Balancing) sends them requests, and they 500 because the connection pool or cache warm-up isn't done.

PropertyRolling update
Extra capacity neededMinimal (just maxSurge)
Blast radiusGrows continuously — no clean "X% of traffic" knob
Mixed versions live?Yes, throughout the roll — both versions serve simultaneously
RollbackRoll backward, also incremental and slow (TTR in minutes)
PitfallBecause old and new run concurrently during the roll, both versions must be mutually compatible — same as the constraint for online schema changes. A v2 that writes a new message format an in-flight v1 consumer cannot parse will break during the roll itself, not just at the end. This is the single most overlooked rolling-update failure mode.

Blue-green deployment

Blue-green runs two complete production environments. blue = current serves all traffic; green = new is deployed in full and tested in isolation. You cut over by repointing the router/load balancer (or DNS, or a service selector) from blue to green atomically. Rollback is the reverse cutover — near-instant, because blue is still running, fully warmed, untouched.

# Conceptually, the cutover is one atomic pointer move:
router.active_pool = green_pool   # was blue_pool
# rollback:
router.active_pool = blue_pool    # blue never went away
Blue-greenRolling
Capacity cost2x during cutover~1x
Mixed versionsNo (clean cut)Yes
TTR (rollback)Seconds (flip back)Minutes (re-roll)
Blast radius at cutover100% at onceGradual
Trade-offBlue-green gives you the fastest rollback and a clean, un-mixed version boundary — but it flips 100% of traffic at once, so a bad version that passed your pre-cutover tests still hits every user instantly. You've optimized TTR at the expense of blast radius. That is why serious shops layer a canary on top: route 1% to green first, then flip the rest. Blue-green also doubles compute cost during the window, and it does not magically solve stateful concerns — a shared database is touched by both, so schema compatibility and in-flight sessions still need the expand-contract discipline below.

Sticky sessions and long-lived connections (WebSockets, gRPC streams — see Long-Polling vs WebSockets vs SSE) complicate the clean cut: existing connections pinned to blue must drain. Production blue-green typically allows a connection-draining window where blue keeps serving established connections while new connections go to green.

Canary releases and automated analysis

A canary release routes a small slice of traffic (often 1%, then 5%, 25%, 50%, 100%) to the new version and measures it against the baseline before widening. The name comes from canaries in coal mines — a small, expendable early-warning signal. The blast radius at each step is bounded, and you only proceed when the data says the canary is healthy.

A naive canary — "route 5%, eyeball a dashboard, click promote" — is barely better than nothing because it depends on a human watching at the right moment. The principal-grade version is automated canary analysis (ACA): a controller compares the canary's metrics against a control (ideally a same-age baseline pulled from the same release, not the old production version, to avoid confounds like cache warmth) and promotes or aborts automatically.

# Progressive canary with SLO-burn-based auto-rollback (e.g. Argo Rollouts / Flagger)
steps:
  - setWeight: 1
  - pause: { duration: 5m }
  - analysis:                         # query Prometheus/Datadog
      metrics:
        - name: error-rate
          successCondition: result < 0.01      # <1% 5xx
        - name: p99-latency
          successCondition: result < 300       # ms
      # if ANY metric fails -> abort, route 100% back to stable
  - setWeight: 25
  - pause: { duration: 10m }
  - analysis: { ... }
  - setWeight: 50
  - pause: { duration: 10m }
  - setWeight: 100

The metrics that gate promotion should be your actual SLIs: error rate, latency percentiles (p50/p95/p99 — never the mean), and saturation. The strong pattern is to tie the abort condition to SLO error-budget burn rate: if the canary is burning budget fast enough that, extrapolated, it would blow the monthly budget, abort. This is the same fast-burn alert logic from SLO theory applied at deploy time, which is far more robust than a fixed "error rate > 1%" threshold because it scales with how much room you actually have.

Key insightCanary analysis is a statistical comparison, not a threshold check. Two pitfalls dominate: (1) low traffic to the canary means too few samples for the difference to be significant — a 1% slice of a low-QPS service may take an hour to detect a regression; consider weighting by request count, not just percentage. (2) confounding — comparing a cold canary against warm production conflates the version change with cache/JIT warmth. Compare canary vs. a freshly-deployed baseline of the current version when you can.

PitfallCanaries catch problems proportional to the traffic they see. They are excellent for latency/error regressions and bad for rare-event and time-delayed bugs: a data-corruption bug that only manifests on a nightly batch, a memory leak that takes 6 hours to OOM, or a bug triggered by a specific tenant that never lands in the 1% slice. Don't claim a canary "proves" correctness — it bounds risk, it doesn't eliminate it.

Feature flags, dark launches, and shadow traffic

Feature flags are runtime conditionals that gate behavior without a deploy. They are the mechanism that makes deploy/release decoupling real, and they enable per-user, per-tenant, or percentage-based rollout independent of which binary is running.

if (flags.isEnabled("new_ranking_v2", ctx.userId, ctx.tenantId)) {
    return rankWithV2(query);     // new path, dark until ramped
} else {
    return rankWithV1(query);     // stable path
}
  • Dark launch — deploy and even execute the new code path in production without surfacing its result to users, to validate it under real load and data before exposure. Facebook famously dark-launched features by running the new backend for real traffic and discarding the output.
  • Shadow traffic (traffic mirroring) — duplicate a copy of real production requests to the new version, which processes them but whose responses are discarded (and must not have side effects). This is the gold standard for de-risking a rewrite of a read path: you compare v2's output and latency against v1 on identical real traffic, with zero user-facing risk. Service meshes (see Microservices and Service Mesh) make mirroring a config line.
PitfallShadow traffic is only safe for idempotent, side-effect-free paths. Mirror a request that writes to the DB, charges a card (see Payment System), or publishes to Kafka, and you double the side effect. Shadow consumers must hit a sandboxed datastore or have all writes stubbed. Also account for doubled load on shared downstream dependencies — mirroring 100% traffic effectively doubles QPS on any backend the shadow path calls.

Trade-offFlags are powerful but become technical debt and a combinatorial test problem. Every flag is an if that doubles the code paths; ten flags are 1024 theoretical combinations. The discipline: flags must have owners and expiry dates, and a process to retire them (delete the dead branch) once a release is fully rolled out. Long-lived "permanent" flags blur into config and should be reclassified as such. Stale flags have caused major incidents — the 2012 Knight Capital collapse was, in part, dead flag-gated code reactivated by a partial deploy.

Zero-downtime database migrations

This is where most candidates fall apart, and where principals earn their title. Code is easy to roll forward and back; schema and data are not. The constraint is the same one rolling updates impose: during any rollout, old code and new code run against the same database simultaneously, so the schema must be readable and writable by both. You cannot do a destructive schema change in lockstep with a code change.

Expand-contract (parallel-change)

The canonical pattern decomposes a breaking schema change into a sequence of individually backward-compatible steps. To rename column email to email_address:

  1. Expand — add the new column email_address (nullable / with default). Schema now has both. Old code ignores it; this deploy is safe because it adds, never removes.
  2. Dual-write — deploy code that writes to both email and email_address on every write, but still reads from the old one. Now new rows are consistent in both.
  3. Backfill — run a batched migration to copy email → email_address for all historical rows. Batch it (e.g. 5k rows/txn) to avoid long locks and replication lag (see Replication Deep Dive).
  4. Migrate reads — deploy code that reads from email_address. Verify. The dual-write window means a rollback to step 3 code still works.
  5. Stop writing old — deploy code that only writes/reads the new column.
  6. Contract — after a soak period with no rollback need, drop the old email column.
Key insightThe dual-write window is the safety net that makes each step independently reversible. At every point, the version you might roll back to can still read and write the database correctly. The cost is that the application carries temporary complexity (writing two columns, a backfill job) and the migration spans multiple deploys over days — you cannot do it in one PR. A candidate who proposes "ALTER TABLE RENAME COLUMN" in a single deploy has just designed an outage.

Other rules of zero-downtime DDL

  • Additive changes are safe; destructive ones need the contract phase. Adding a nullable column or a new table is non-breaking. Dropping a column, renaming, narrowing a type, or adding a NOT NULL without a default is breaking.
  • Adding NOT NULL — add the column nullable, backfill, then add the constraint, rather than blocking on a default-fill that locks the table.
  • Index creation — use CREATE INDEX CONCURRENTLY (Postgres) / online DDL (MySQL 8 / gh-ost / pt-online-schema-change) so you don't take a long table lock. A naive index build on a large table is a multi-minute write lock = outage.
  • Beware long-running migrations on replicas — a single huge transaction can stall the replication stream and blow read-replica lag, indirectly degrading the whole service.
PitfallThe dual-write window introduces a consistency hazard: if a write to column A succeeds and the write to column B fails (or the process crashes between them), the two diverge. For a single-row multi-column write inside one transaction this is fine. But if "dual write" spans two datastores (e.g. old DB and a new service), you've created a distributed write with no atomicity — exactly the problem distributed transactions and CDC / outbox patterns exist to solve. Don't hand-roll cross-store dual writes; use change-data-capture to derive the second store from the first.

Rollback vs roll-forward

When a deploy goes bad, you have two recoveries, and a principal knows when each applies:

  • Rollback — revert to the previous known-good version. Fast, low-risk for stateless code. The default reaction to a freshly-deployed regression. Blue-green and flag-flips make this seconds; rolling makes it minutes.
  • Roll-forward — fix the bug and deploy a new version. Necessary when rollback is impossible or unsafe — most commonly because a schema/data migration already ran and the old code can no longer read the new state. Once you've passed the "contract" phase of expand-contract, you cannot roll back; you can only roll forward.
Trade-offRollback is faster and safer for code, but data migrations are frequently irreversible, which is precisely why expand-contract keeps the old schema alive through the rollout — it preserves the option to roll back. The architectural decision is: how long do you keep the rollback option open (the soak period before contract), versus the carrying cost of dual schemas and dual writes? High-stakes systems keep it open for days; the cost is acceptable complexity for the recovery guarantee.

What strong candidates doThey state the recovery plan as part of the design, not as an afterthought: define the automated rollback trigger (SLO burn / canary abort), confirm the rollback target is still healthy and warm, and explicitly call out the point of no return where the strategy switches from rollback to roll-forward. They also know that rolling back doesn't undo emitted side effects — messages already published, emails already sent, payments already taken — and that those need compensating actions, linking deployment safety to resilience patterns like idempotency and sagas.

Principal-level interview lens

What strong candidates doThey treat deployment as risk management with explicit blast-radius / TTD / TTR levers, not as a CI/CD checkbox. They decouple deploy from release with flags, propose canary with automated SLO-burn-based analysis rather than hand-watched dashboards, and — the real differentiator — they handle the database. They reach for expand-contract unprompted, articulate the dual-write window as the thing that preserves reversibility, and name the exact point where the strategy crosses from rollback to roll-forward. They connect deployment safety to error budgets, observability, and resilience instead of treating them as separate topics.

Sharp probing questions an interviewer should ask:

  • You're renaming a heavily-used column on a 2-billion-row table with zero downtime. Walk me through every deploy and the exact failure mode at each step if you skipped it. Where is the point of no return?
  • Your canary is at 1% on a service doing 50 QPS, and you're hunting a regression that affects 1 in 500 requests. How long until you'd detect it, and how would you change the canary to detect faster?
  • You want to validate a rewritten pricing service with shadow traffic. The service writes to a ledger and calls a payment provider. What breaks, and how do you mirror safely?
  • A blue-green cutover passed all pre-cut tests but green is failing for 30% of users post-flip. What's your recovery, and why might the failure not have shown pre-cut?
  • How would you wire your canary's abort condition to an SLO error budget rather than a fixed error-rate threshold, and why is that better?
  • You have 40 feature flags, three are years old. What's your governance, and what's the incident risk of leaving them? (Probe: do they know the Knight Capital story?)
